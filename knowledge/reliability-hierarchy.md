# Reliability Hierarchy -- Peat's Diagnostic Trust System

Peat consistently argued that standard blood tests are interpreted against reference ranges derived from sick populations, making "normal" a misleading baseline. His diagnostic philosophy placed functional indicators above laboratory values, and within laboratory values, sharply distinguished between tests he trusted and tests he considered misleading or actively harmful to rely on.

---

## The Hierarchy

1. **Functional indicators** (temperature, pulse, sleep, warmth of extremities) -- the gold standard
2. **Tests reflecting metabolic state** (CO2/bicarbonate, lactate, cholesterol, albumin) -- direct windows into cellular energy production
3. **Upstream hormone precursors** (pregnenolone, DHEA, progesterone) -- reflect the body's synthetic capacity
4. **Specific markers with known reliability** (transferrin saturation, PTH, free fatty acids) -- useful when interpreted correctly
5. **Standard tests requiring heavy context** (TSH, cortisol, ferritin, estrogen) -- frequently misleading without companion markers
6. **Tests Peat considered unreliable or harmful** (saliva hormone panels, isolated LDL, melatonin levels) -- distrusted or dismissed

---

## Tier 1: Gold Standard Tests

### CO2 / Bicarbonate
- **What it measures:** Dissolved carbon dioxide and its bicarbonate buffer in blood, reflecting cellular oxidative metabolism.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** CO2 is a direct product of glucose oxidation in mitochondria. High CO2 means cells are burning fuel efficiently. Low CO2 means cells have shifted to glycolysis, stress metabolism, or the person is hyperventilating. "CO2 is probably the best single indicator of how well you're oxidizing fuel." CO2 also facilitates oxygen delivery via the Bohr effect -- without it, oxygen stays locked on hemoglobin.
- **Peat's target:** Venous CO2 of 26-30+ mEq/L. Higher is generally better. Below 24 suggests metabolic stress.
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (CO2 section, claims 1-5); cbc-organ-raw (CO2/Bicarbonate section, claims 1-5)

### Transferrin Saturation Percentage
- **What it measures:** The percentage of iron-binding sites on transferrin that are occupied by iron.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** "The transferrin saturation percentage is probably the most useful single iron test." Unlike ferritin (which rises with any inflammation), transferrin saturation reflects actual circulating iron availability. It is less distorted by acute phase responses.
- **Peat's target:** Under 30%. Mainstream considers 20-50% normal, but Peat viewed anything approaching 45-50% as dangerous.
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (TIBC section, claim 15)

### PTH (Parathyroid Hormone)
- **What it measures:** Parathyroid gland output, reflecting calcium/vitamin D/phosphate balance.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** "The PTH test reflects your status of phosphate, calcium, and Vitamin D. It is rarely done but extremely relevant." PTH directly poisons mitochondrial function. It simultaneously causes osteoporosis AND arterial calcification. It varies closely with cortisol and prolactin as a stress marker.
- **Peat's target:** As low as possible. Mainstream range is 15-65 pg/mL, but Peat wanted it suppressed through adequate calcium (1.5-2g daily), vitamin D (~50 ng/mL), and low phosphate intake.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (PTH section, claims 8.1-8.7); cbc-organ-raw (Calcium/PTH section, claims 1-10)

### Albumin
- **What it measures:** The most abundant blood protein, synthesized by the liver.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** Albumin is a negative acute phase protein -- it drops when the body shifts liver synthesis toward inflammatory proteins (CRP, ferritin, fibrinogen). "Every point that your albumin drops below 5.0, your mortality rate doubles." It reflects liver function, nutritional status, and inflammatory burden simultaneously.
- **Peat's target:** 4.9-5.0 g/dL or higher. Standard labs consider 3.5-5.0 normal, but Peat viewed anything below 4.5 as a red flag. Hospitalized patients below 3.5 have dramatically increased mortality.
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Albumin section, claims 55-60); cbc-organ-raw (Albumin section, claims 1-7)

### Free Fatty Acids (FFA)
- **What it measures:** Unbound fatty acids circulating in the blood, released from adipose tissue under stress.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** "Free fatty acids are rarely tested but extremely important." Elevated FFA indicates lipolysis from stress (adrenaline, cortisol). PUFA-type FFA specifically suppress thyroid function, block glucose oxidation, and activate the entire stress cascade. The test distinguishes stress-driven metabolism from healthy metabolism.
- **Peat's target:** As low as possible. High FFA = the body is running on emergency fat-burning. "Only the polyunsaturated fatty acids have the terrible anti-energy effects."
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (Free Fatty Acids section, claims 1-8); hormones-raw (Adrenaline section, claim 10.2)

### Cholesterol (Total)
- **What it measures:** Total circulating cholesterol -- the raw material for all steroid hormones.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** Cholesterol is a master indicator of the thyroid-to-steroid conversion pipeline. High cholesterol means the body is making plenty of raw material but failing to convert it (usually hypothyroid). Low cholesterol means insufficient precursor for protective steroids. "Cholesterol rises with age because low thyroid and low Vitamin A interfere with the conversion of cholesterol into pregnenolone and progesterone."
- **Peat's target:** 160-220 mg/dL. Below 160 increases cancer, violence, and suicide risk. Above 220 suggests impaired thyroid conversion. Mainstream targets below 200 as "desirable," which Peat found dangerously reductive.
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (Total Cholesterol section, claims 1-18); hormones-raw (Pregnenolone section, claim 6.7)

### Lactate / Lactic Acid
- **What it measures:** A byproduct of anaerobic glycolysis.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** "Lactic acid rises when something is going wrong metabolically." When cells cannot oxidize glucose fully (due to thyroid deficiency, PUFA damage, or oxygen deprivation), they produce lactate instead of CO2. Lactic acid is itself harmful -- it promotes inflammation, edema, and can indicate tissue damage.
- **Peat's target:** As low as possible. Elevated lactate = cells are in emergency glycolytic mode.
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (Lactate section, claims 1-6); hormones-raw (Recommended Panel section)

## Tier 2: Useful With Caveats

### TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)
- **What it measures:** Pituitary demand for thyroid hormone.
- **Caveat:** TSH is actively harmful to rely on alone. "TSH itself causes many of the symptoms blamed on hypothyroidism. It acts on bone marrow and many other tissues, increasing inflammatory cytokine chemicals." More critically, stress hormones (adrenaline, cortisol, estrogen) suppress TSH while simultaneously blocking T4-to-T3 conversion. A "normal" TSH of 2-3 can coexist with severe functional hypothyroidism. Peat's goal was TSH "as close to zero as possible."
- **Companion needed:** Temperature, pulse, cholesterol, Free T3. "If someone has a TSH over about 2.0, with symptoms, they should consider thyroid support regardless of what the T3/T4 numbers show."
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.1-1.42)

### Free T3 / Free T4 / T3:T4 Ratio
- **What they measure:** Active thyroid hormone (T3), storage form (T4), and conversion efficiency.
- **Caveat:** Blood levels of T3 may not reflect tissue levels. Peat emphasized that tissue hypothyroidism can exist with normal blood thyroid values because stress hormones redirect T4 conversion to inactive Reverse T3. The T3:T4 ratio matters more than either alone -- a low ratio signals impaired conversion.
- **Companion needed:** Reverse T3, TSH, temperature, pulse, cholesterol.
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (Free T3 section, claims 1-12; Free T4 section, claims 1-5; T3/T4 Ratio section, claims 1-11)

### Reverse T3
- **What it measures:** The inactive form of thyroid hormone produced when T4 is diverted away from active T3.
- **Caveat:** Useful as a stress indicator -- "any cell that is stressed converts more of its T4 to Reverse T3" -- but not widely available and not always interpretable without the full thyroid picture. Reverse T3 actively suppresses respiration, so it is not merely "inactive."
- **Companion needed:** Free T3, Free T4, T3:T4 ratio, temperature, pulse.
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (Reverse T3 section, claims 1-14)

### Prolactin
- **What it measures:** Pituitary stress hormone.
- **Caveat:** Very useful as a stress indicator, but the reference range has been inflated. "Don't feel reassured if your prolactin is only 18 or 20 on the test, because everything is under stress when it's that high." The mainstream range (women: 2-29 ng/mL) includes pathological elevations that have become "normal." Peat implied the ideal was below 10 ng/mL.
- **Companion needed:** TSH, estrogen, thyroid. Prolactin and TSH rise together under hypothyroidism.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Prolactin section, claims 7.1-7.12)

### Progesterone
- **What it measures:** The primary protective steroid hormone.
- **Caveat:** Blood progesterone underrepresents brain levels by roughly 10x, since the brain synthesizes progesterone locally. Saliva testing is unreliable because progesterone is poorly water-soluble -- "if you try to dissolve progesterone in water, it is very hard to dissolve." Serum testing is acceptable but must account for cycle day in women.
- **Companion needed:** Estrogen (the P:E ratio is the master metric), cycle day, thyroid status.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Progesterone section, claims 3.1-3.16; SHBG section, claim 12.3)

### Vitamin D (25-OH)
- **What it measures:** Circulating vitamin D status.
- **Caveat:** Useful and trusted, but must be interpreted alongside calcium and PTH. "The most common blood test finding correlated with PTH excess is low Vitamin D." Supplementing vitamin D without adequate calcium can be counterproductive.
- **Peat's target:** 40-60 ng/mL, with ~50 ng/mL as the sweet spot. Mainstream considers >30 sufficient.
- **Companion needed:** PTH, calcium (dietary and serum), phosphate.
- **Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-raw (Vitamin D section, claims 1-10); hormones-raw (PTH section, claims 8.3-8.5)

### Pregnenolone / DHEA
- **What they measure:** Upstream steroid precursors reflecting the body's synthetic capacity.
- **Caveat:** Brain levels are ~10x blood levels, so serum tests underrepresent neurological status. Both decline ~2% per year after mid-twenties. Very common to become deficient in forties "usually because of an imbalance of thyroid and estrogen."
- **Companion needed:** Thyroid, cholesterol (the raw material), Vitamin A.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (DHEA section, claims 5.1-5.5; Pregnenolone section, claims 6.1-6.7)

## Tier 3: Unreliable Alone

### Ferritin
- **Why misleading:** "Ferritin is a positive acute phase protein whose synthesis increases during inflammation. It should not be used as a primary marker of iron status because inflammation distorts it greatly." Ferritin can be sky-high from inflammation with normal or even low iron stores, or it can be low from protein deficiency despite adequate iron.
- **Pair with:** Transferrin saturation, hemoglobin, CRP. "Only when CRP is simultaneously normal can you trust an elevated ferritin as reflecting true iron stores."
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Ferritin section, claims 1-10)

### Serum Cortisol
- **Why misleading:** "Serum cortisol varies so dramatically throughout the day, in response to meals, and in reaction to the blood draw itself, that a single morning measurement tells you relatively little." Diurnal rhythm, venipuncture stress, and binding protein variation make single values nearly uninterpretable.
- **Pair with:** Temperature, pulse, sleep quality. "Better to assess cortisol functionally -- do you wake at 2-4 AM? Do you need sugar at night? Those are cortisol indicators."
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Cortisol section, claims 1.1-1.2)

### Serum Estrogen (Estradiol)
- **Why misleading:** "Serum estrogen drops with age, but tissue estrogen actually increases." Blood estrogen can look low while tissues are saturated with locally produced estrogen via aromatase. "Lab reference ranges for estrogen are unreliable because each lab has its own standard, and they typically use sick populations to establish normal." Additionally, any injury to any tissue activates aromatase and produces local estrogen that never appears in serum.
- **Pair with:** Progesterone (the P:E ratio matters more than absolute estrogen), temperature, inflammatory markers.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claims 2.1-2.2, 2.9, 2.18)

### Hemoglobin / Hematocrit
- **Why misleading:** Standard "normal" ranges may include pathological iron accumulation. Peat cited research showing "so-called anemic kids with hemoglobin of 10 or 10.5 are healthier than the ones with so-called normal levels." In men, high-normal hemoglobin may reflect iron overload rather than health. The Bohr effect means that functional oxygen delivery depends more on CO2 levels than on hemoglobin quantity.
- **Pair with:** CO2/bicarbonate, transferrin saturation, iron status, reticulocyte count.
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Hematocrit/Hemoglobin section, claims 73-77); cbc-organ-raw (Hemoglobin section, claims 1-12)

### LDL Cholesterol
- **Why misleading:** Peat considered the LDL-as-villain narrative fundamentally flawed. "The problem isn't LDL transport -- it's what happens when PUFA-laden LDL particles oxidize." Oxidized LDL is dangerous; native LDL is not. The test measures total LDL without distinguishing oxidized from unoxidized particles. Statins lower LDL but also lower the cholesterol needed for steroid hormone synthesis.
- **Pair with:** Total cholesterol, thyroid status, PUFA intake history. "The real question is whether cholesterol is being converted to protective steroids, not whether LDL is high."
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (LDL section, claims 1-5)

### Blood Glucose (Fasting)
- **Why misleading:** A single fasting glucose value hides the crucial question: is this person maintaining glucose through healthy glycogen stores or through stress-hormone-driven gluconeogenesis? "A 'normal' fasting glucose maintained by adrenaline and cortisol is not the same as one maintained by good liver glycogen." Low-normal glucose in someone with cold hands and fast pulse indicates stress, not health.
- **Pair with:** Temperature, pulse, free fatty acids, time since last meal.
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (Glucose section, claims 1-12)

## Tier 4: Distrusted

### Saliva Hormone Tests
- **Why distrusted:** "Saliva hormone tests are not reliable for absolute cortisol levels." The free hormone in saliva varies independently of total production depending on binding protein levels, liver function, and other variables. Progesterone is nearly insoluble in water, making saliva progesterone measurements fundamentally questionable -- "since they aren't soluble in water, what is it bound to?"
- **Instead:** Serum testing with functional indicators (temperature, pulse) for confirmation.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Cortisol section, claim 1.1; SHBG section, claim 12.3)

### Isolated LDL as Cardiac Risk Marker
- **Why distrusted:** Peat rejected the lipid hypothesis of heart disease. "Cholesterol accumulates in arterial walls not because of high LDL but because hypothyroidism slows its removal." The fixation on LDL drives statin prescriptions that lower cholesterol below the threshold needed for protective steroid synthesis. "When you force your cholesterol down, you're unable to make progesterone, pregnenolone, DHEA, and the whole range of protective steroids."
- **Instead:** Total cholesterol (target 160-220), thyroid status, and functional indicators.
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (LDL section, claims 1-5; Total Cholesterol section, claims 4-6, 10-11)

### Standard Thyroid Panel (TSH Only)
- **Why distrusted:** A "normal" TSH of 2-4 mIU/L can mask severe functional hypothyroidism. TSH is suppressed by the same stress hormones that cause hypothyroid symptoms. "Those same stress hormones that produce the inactive Reverse T3 also tend to suppress the pituitary TSH, making it look like you're even hyperthyroid." Treating by TSH alone misses the vast majority of hypothyroid patients.
- **Instead:** Temperature and pulse as primary indicators; if TSH is tested, it should be accompanied by Free T3, Free T4, Reverse T3, cholesterol, and comprehensive functional assessment.
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.3-1.7, 1.21-1.22)

### CRP as Standalone Inflammation Marker
- **Why limited:** Peat mentioned CRP only twice, noting it as a positive acute phase protein (rises during inflammation while albumin falls). He did not emphasize it as a primary marker, preferring albumin (more informative because its decline maps directly to mortality risk) and functional indicators.
- **Instead:** Albumin, temperature, pulse, and the broader inflammatory picture (serotonin, histamine, prostaglandin effects assessed functionally).
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (CRP section, claims 24-25)

### Ceruloplasmin as a Health Target
- **Why distrusted:** Some functional medicine practitioners target raising ceruloplasmin as a health goal. Peat flatly rejected this: "Ceruloplasmin is an acute phase reactant. Is increasing ceruloplasmin a goal of a diet or lifestyle? Not my goal." Elevated ceruloplasmin reflects inflammation, not healthy copper status.
- **Instead:** Adequate dietary copper from whole foods (oysters, liver, chocolate). Test copper status directly if needed.
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Ceruloplasmin section, claims 79-83)

## Functional Indicators Over Blood Tests

Peat consistently prioritized functional indicators that reflect actual tissue metabolism over laboratory values that reflect a snapshot of circulating molecules. These were his preferred non-blood diagnostic methods:

### Waking Body Temperature
- **Target:** ~97.8F (36.6C) upon waking, before getting out of bed.
- **Why:** Reflects overnight metabolic rate. Below 97.4F strongly suggests hypothyroidism. Temperature is the "quickest way to assess estrogen status because estrogen lowers your temperature."
- **Protocol:** Measure orally or axillary immediately upon waking, before any activity or food. Use a reliable thermometer. Track for at least one week to establish pattern.
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.2, 1.4, 1.12, 1.17); hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claim 2.12); nutrients-diagnostics-raw (Diagnostic Philosophy section)

### Post-Breakfast Temperature
- **Target:** ~98.6F (37C), measured 30-60 minutes after eating breakfast.
- **Why:** Confirms that the meal stimulates adequate metabolic response. A rise to 98.6F after eating indicates functional thyroid conversion. Failure to rise suggests impaired T4-to-T3 conversion.
- **Protocol:** Eat a carbohydrate-containing breakfast (not just coffee). Measure temperature 30-60 minutes later. The rise from waking temp to post-meal temp should be 0.5-1.0F.
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (TSH section, claim 1.2); nutrients-diagnostics-raw (Diagnostic Philosophy section)

### Resting Pulse Rate
- **Target:** ~80-85 bpm at rest.
- **Why:** Reflects metabolic rate. Below 70 in an adult suggests hypothyroidism (unless the person is a trained athlete, which Peat considered a separate metabolic adaptation). Above 90 at rest may indicate stress-hormone-driven metabolism (adrenaline). The key distinction: pulse driven by thyroid = warm hands; pulse driven by adrenaline = cold hands.
- **Protocol:** Measure while seated and calm, at least 10 minutes after any activity. Measure alongside hand temperature.
- **Sources:** thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.2, 1.12, 1.17); hormones-raw (Adrenaline section, claim 10.5)

### Warmth of Hands and Feet
- **Target:** Warm hands and feet at all times.
- **Why:** "If your hands are cold while your heart rate increases, you're running on adrenaline, which is not good. Your hands should be warm." Cold extremities with fast pulse = adrenaline-driven metabolism (peripheral vasoconstriction). Warm extremities with moderate pulse = thyroid-driven metabolism (even vasodilation). This single check distinguishes healthy metabolic rate from stress-compensated metabolic rate.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Adrenaline section, claim 10.5); thyroid-raw (TSH section, claim 1.12)

### Sleep Quality -- Especially 2-4 AM Waking
- **Target:** Sleep through the night without waking, especially not between 2-4 AM.
- **Why:** "Insomnia is a daytime disorder presenting at night." Waking at 2-4 AM almost always indicates nocturnal hypoglycemia triggering adrenaline and cortisol release. The liver ran out of glycogen. This reflects inadequate daytime carbohydrate intake or impaired glycogen storage (hypothyroidism). Aldosterone and PTH also rise at night, compounding the problem.
- **Protocol:** If waking occurs, eating a small amount of sugar (orange juice, milk with sugar) and salt should allow return to sleep within minutes. Chronic waking indicates the need for more daytime carbohydrate and possibly thyroid support.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Adrenaline section, claim 10.3; Aldosterone section, claim 9.5); lipid-metabolic-raw (Glucose section)

### Digestion and Bowel Function
- **Target:** Regular, well-formed bowel movements without bloating, gas, or constipation.
- **Why:** Impaired digestion allows endotoxin (bacterial lipopolysaccharide) to enter the bloodstream from the gut. "Endotoxin is a toxic component of gram-negative bacterial cell walls that enters the bloodstream when intestinal barrier function is compromised." Endotoxin triggers the entire inflammatory cascade: serotonin release, nitric oxide production, estrogen elevation, liver damage. Constipation also allows enterohepatic recirculation of estrogen and cortisol.
- **Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Endotoxin section, claims 30-41); hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claim 2.21)

### Skin Thickness and Quality
- **Target:** Thick, resilient skin.
- **Why:** Peat listed skin thickness as a functional indicator of metabolic health. Thin, fragile skin indicates cortisol excess, collagen degradation, and protein deficiency.
- **Sources:** hormones-raw (Recommended Panel section, functional indicators)

### Blood Sugar Stability Between Meals
- **Target:** Ability to go 4-5 hours between meals without symptoms (shaking, irritability, brain fog, cold hands).
- **Why:** Needing to eat every 2-3 hours indicates reliance on stress hormones to maintain blood sugar. Adequate liver glycogen, maintained by thyroid function and carbohydrate intake, should provide stable glucose for hours.
- **Sources:** lipid-metabolic-raw (Glucose section, claims 1-12); hormones-raw (Cortisol section, claims 1.6-1.9)

## Enrichment Additions (2026-03-17)

### New Tier 1 Additions

#### Pulse Rate (Functional)
- **What it measures:** Resting heart rate as a direct indicator of metabolic rate.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** Peat personally targeted resting pulse "averaging a little over 90" for the last 35 years. An adrenaline-driven pulse stays variable and reactive; a metabolically-driven pulse from good thyroid function stays close to the same rate all day while sitting. The distinction between these two patterns is the critical diagnostic insight.
- **Peat's target:** 85+ bpm resting for general health. He personally maintained >90 bpm. Therapeutic targets of 100+ bpm are safe for months when treating tumors, cataracts, and chronic problems.
- **Key diagnostic:** Chronically elevated heart rate (150+ bpm) from hypothyroidism can normalize within days of starting thyroid supplementation, as thyroid reduces compensatory adrenaline (which can be 30-40x normal in hypothyroid states).
- **Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.38, E6.39, E6.40

#### Body Temperature (Functional)
- **What it measures:** Core body temperature as a proxy for mitochondrial oxygen consumption and metabolic rate.
- **Why Peat trusted it:** Average normal body temperature has dropped 1.06°F in men and 0.58°F in women since Fahrenheit's era, paralleling increases in serious diseases. The temperature rise during the day is the most important diagnostic measure, since nocturnal stress hormones can give a misleading impression in the morning.
- **Peat's target:** Waking ~97.8°F, rising to 98.6°F after breakfast. The daytime rise confirms thyroid-driven metabolism.
- **Key diagnostic:** A high rate of mitochondrial oxygen consumption produces heat; when temperature is lowered, reactive oxygen species increase because elimination decreases greatly. Low temperature is not merely a symptom of hypothyroidism -- it actively drives the degenerative cascade.
- **Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.41-E6.45

### New Tier 2 Additions

#### Aldosterone
- **What it measures:** Mineralocorticoid hormone that governs sodium-potassium-magnesium balance.
- **Caveat:** Rarely tested but connects to multiple threads. Aldosterone rises during the night (part of the nocturnal catabolic cascade), inhibits uncoupling proteins (UCP), poisons mitochondria alongside PTH, and forms a vicious loop with serotonin. Low sodium intake drives aldosterone up, causing the very cardiovascular damage salt restriction claims to prevent.
- **Companion needed:** Sodium (dietary), magnesium, potassium, cortisol, PTH.
- **Sources:** hormones-enrichment E4.10, E4.11; metabolic threads 15, 19

#### IL-18 (Interleukin-18)
- **What it measures:** Pro-inflammatory cytokine linking TSH to liver damage and ferritin elevation.
- **Caveat:** Not a standard lab test, but clinically important because TSH directly increases IL-18, which damages the liver and raises ferritin independent of iron stores. When ferritin is elevated but CRP is normal, IL-18 may explain the discrepancy. Elevated IL-18 is "another argument for keeping your TSH very low."
- **Companion needed:** TSH, ferritin, liver enzymes (ALT/AST), CRP.
- **Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.47; iron-inflammation-enrichment E3.1

### New Tier 3 Addition

#### Hepcidin
- **Why context-dependent:** The master regulator of intestinal iron absorption, hormonally controlled. Estrogen suppresses hepcidin (increasing iron absorption), progesterone stimulates hepcidin (decreasing iron absorption). Not a standard lab test, but understanding the hepcidin axis is essential for interpreting iron panels in women -- the same hemoglobin and ferritin values mean different things depending on estrogen/progesterone status.
- **Pair with:** Estrogen, progesterone, transferrin saturation, ferritin, cycle day in women.
- **Sources:** cbc-organ-enrichment E5.2; metabolic thread 21

### Updated Tier 4 Additions

#### Saliva Hormone Tests (strengthened)
- **Additional evidence:** Saliva tests are "not reliable for absolute values" -- only useful for seeing whether cortisol's daily pattern follows the normal rhythm. Progesterone in saliva is fundamentally questionable because progesterone is nearly water-insoluble. Urinary hormones "represent liver metabolism more than hormonal effects" since they are mostly glucuronidated or sulfated.
- **Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.49, E6.50

#### Hair Mineral Testing
- **Why distrusted:** "Only rarely useful" (e.g., unsuspected heavy metal poisoning). Toenails are better than hair for mineral analysis because they absorb more from the body and less from environmental air and water exposure.
- **Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.51

### Enrichment Updates to Existing Entries

**TSH (Tier 2):** Enrichment confirms Peat's target was specifically ~0.4 or below (not just "as close to zero as possible"). People with TSH below 0.4 were the freest from thyroid cancer. After starting T4, wait at least two weeks on stable dose before interpreting blood tests (T4 half-life is about two weeks). -- nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.46, E6.48

**Vitamin D (Tier 2):** Enrichment adds Peat's explicit blood level goal of 50 ng/mL -- "the point at which it lowers parathyroid hormone." He personally applied ~20,000 IU topically in winter rather than taking it orally. Low thyroid function increases vitamin D requirements. Persistent low 25-OH-D despite supplementation may indicate liver dysfunction preventing hydroxylation. -- nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.1-E6.6

**PTH (Tier 1):** Enrichment dramatically expands PTH's pathological scope. PTH causes insomnia, seizures, dementia, psychosis, cancer, heart disease, respiratory distress, osteoporosis, sarcopenia, histamine release, inflammation, and soft tissue calcification. PTH ends the hair growth cycle (morning hair loss from nocturnal PTH surges). A glass of milk at bedtime may protect hair as it protects bone. If vitamin D has been very low for a long time, parathyroid glands may have enlarged and take extended time to normalize. -- nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.14-E6.21

**Ferritin (Tier 3):** Enrichment confirms ferritin is distorted by TSH-driven IL-18 cascade (TSH → IL-18 → liver damage → elevated ferritin independent of iron). Hypothyroidism causes low temperature of extremities, slowing red cell formation in long bones, which may alter ferritin through an adaptive mechanism separate from inflammation. -- nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.32; iron-inflammation-enrichment E3.1
