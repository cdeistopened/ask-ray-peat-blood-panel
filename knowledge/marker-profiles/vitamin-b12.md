# Vitamin B12

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered serum B12 levels unreliable without companion functional markers; blood levels do not always correlate with functional status
**Companion markers:** Methylmalonic acid, homocysteine, MCV, reticulocyte count, vitamin A (retinol), folate, B6
**Peat's range:** Not specified; Peat favored signs/symptoms and functional markers over serum B12 levels
**Mainstream range:** 200-900 pg/mL (varies by lab); <200 = deficient

## Peat's Position

Peat approached B12 with a characteristic mixture of respect for its biological importance and skepticism of how it is measured and supplemented. He acknowledged B12 as essential — it is required for DNA synthesis in red blood cell precursors, for recycling homocysteine, and for converting carotene to retinol (active vitamin A). A genuine B12 deficiency is serious: it produces megaloblastic anemia (large, immature red blood cells with elevated MCV), neurological symptoms, and elevated homocysteine with its associated cardiovascular and cognitive risks.

However, Peat questioned the reliability of serum B12 levels as a diagnostic tool. Blood levels do not always correlate with functional status. Reference ranges have shifted over time without clear evidence base. Some people test "low" but show no deficiency signs. He focused on clinical signs and symptoms rather than lab values for B12 assessment: neurological symptoms, unexplained anemia, or elevated homocysteine warrant attention; modest blood level drops in asymptomatic people eating animal foods probably do not.

Peat questioned aggressive B12 testing and supplementation protocols, considering true deficiency less common than alternative health advocates claim, particularly for people eating animal foods regularly. He recommended a generous dietary source of both vitamin A and B12 but noted this is "usually not essential" for people with adequate diets. Liver is the best food source, providing B12 alongside folate, riboflavin, niacin, and B6. Peat recommended eating liver weekly.

The functional markers Peat valued over serum B12 were methylmalonic acid and homocysteine. Methylmalonic acid accumulates specifically when B12-dependent enzymes are impaired, making it a more reliable indicator of functional B12 status than the serum level. Elevated homocysteine signals that the B12/folate/B6 recycling pathway is not keeping up with methionine metabolism. These markers tell you whether B12 is actually doing its job, not just whether it is present in blood.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine often treats low serum B12 with supplementation and rechecks the number. Peat argued that serum B12 is a poor proxy for functional status and that functional markers (methylmalonic acid, homocysteine) and clinical symptoms are more meaningful. The mainstream tendency to diagnose B12 deficiency based on serum levels alone, and to supplement aggressively, may create false diagnoses while missing the broader metabolic context (thyroid function, iron utilization, dietary patterns) that determines whether B12 can actually be used by the body.

## Causal Connections

- B12 deficiency → impaired DNA synthesis → megaloblastic anemia → elevated MCV
- B12 deficiency → impaired homocysteine recycling → elevated homocysteine → cardiovascular/neurological risk
- B12 deficiency → impaired carotene-to-retinol conversion → functional vitamin A deficiency despite adequate carotene intake
- Folate + B12 + B6 → adequate homocysteine recycling → normal homocysteine levels
- Glycine → supports transsulfuration pathway → helps clear homocysteine (complementary to B12/folate pathway)
- Methionine excess (high muscle meat) → overwhelms recycling capacity → elevated homocysteine even with adequate B12
