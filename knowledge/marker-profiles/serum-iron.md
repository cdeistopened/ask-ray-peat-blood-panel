# Serum Iron

**Reliability:** Tier 3 -- Peat considered serum iron unreliable in isolation because inflammation makes iron leak from cells into the bloodstream, creating artificially elevated readings. Serum iron rises with any inflammatory condition independent of true body iron status.
**Companion markers:** Transferrin saturation (preferred test), ferritin, hemoglobin, CRP, albumin, estrogen/progesterone, copper/ceruloplasmin
**Peat's range:** Not specified. Focus on transferrin saturation (<30%) and hemoglobin (low end of normal) instead.
**Mainstream range:** 60-170 mcg/dL

## Peat's Position

Peat viewed serum iron as a deeply unreliable marker that tells you more about inflammation than about iron stores. As inflammation increases, blood iron rises apart from what the real level inside cells may be. Both transferrin and ferritin in blood increase during inflammation, making it impossible to determine stored iron from circulating iron alone. This fundamental limitation means serum iron, in isolation, is nearly useless for clinical decision-making in Peat's framework.

The body has a sophisticated relationship with circulating iron. Fever depletes serum iron as an infection-fighting mechanism -- pathogens need iron, so reducing circulating iron starves them. This is why iron supplementation during infection (especially in malaria-endemic areas) can be lethal. The body deliberately lowers serum iron when fighting pathogens, which means a low serum iron during illness is protective, not a deficiency to be corrected.

One of Peat's most striking claims involves hemoglobin (closely related to serum iron status): estrogen lowers hemoglobin and hematocrit while simultaneously causing iron retention in tissues. For 100 years, doctors saw low hemoglobin in women, prescribed iron, and made them worse. The same estrogen causing low blood counts was blocking bone marrow red cell production while loading tissues with iron. A study of California migrant workers found that children with hemoglobin of 10-10.5 (considered anemic) were healthier than middle-class children with "normal" hemoglobin levels.

The practical implication: rather than chasing serum iron values, monitor hemoglobin at the low end of the normal range and transferrin saturation under 30%. These give more reliable information about functional iron status.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine uses serum iron as part of the standard iron panel (alongside ferritin, TIBC, and transferrin saturation) to diagnose iron deficiency and overload. Low serum iron prompts iron supplementation. Peat argued this is frequently wrong: low serum iron may reflect the body's protective iron sequestration during inflammation, or estrogen-driven suppression of erythropoiesis combined with tissue iron overload. The mainstream reflex to supplement iron based on low serum iron or low hemoglobin has, in Peat's view, harmed millions of people -- particularly women and children in developing countries where iron supplementation during malaria infections can be fatal.

The concept that "anemic" hemoglobin levels (10-10.5 g/dL) may actually be healthier than "normal" levels directly contradicts mainstream hematology's treatment thresholds.

## Sex Differences

Women traditionally show lower serum iron and hemoglobin, which mainstream medicine treats as iron deficiency requiring supplementation. Peat argued this is often estrogen-driven: estrogen lowers hemoglobin and hematocrit while retaining iron in tissues. Premenopausal women lose iron through menstruation (protective), but the appearance of "anemia" coexists with tissue iron overload from estrogen effects. After menopause, iron retention accelerates because progesterone production fails and estrogen's iron-retaining effects become unopposed.

Men accumulate iron progressively throughout life with no regular excretory pathway. This partly explains their shorter lifespan compared to menstruating women. Androgens increase hematocrit and hemoglobin through direct bone marrow effects and by opposing estrogen's suppressive effects on erythropoiesis.

## Medication Effects

- **Iron supplements:** Frequently harmful. May worsen tissue iron overload while not correcting functional deficiency caused by copper, thyroid, or estrogen problems.
- **Coffee/chocolate/tea:** Natural iron absorption inhibitors. Consume with iron-rich meals to reduce iron load.
- **Vitamin D:** Lowers iron retention by suppressing PTH and reducing cellular metal affinity.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Required for copper absorption, which enables proper iron utilization via ceruloplasmin.
- **Copper-rich foods (oysters, liver):** Correct the copper deficiency that causes iron to accumulate in toxic ferrous form.
- **Aspirin + Vitamin E:** Protect against iron-catalyzed oxidative damage.
- **Avoid iron-fortified foods:** US flour and cereal fortification with iron is harmful for most adults who already accumulate excess iron.
- **Blood donation:** Removes iron and "stressins" -- mildly rejuvenating. Recommended especially for men and postmenopausal women.
