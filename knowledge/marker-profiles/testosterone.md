# Testosterone

**Reliability:** Tier 2 -- Peat found testosterone useful to test but warned that total testosterone alone is misleading without context. Aromatase activity, thyroid status, and SHBG all modify what a given testosterone level means functionally.
**Companion markers:** Estradiol, progesterone, DHEA, pregnenolone, SHBG, thyroid (TSH, T3, T4), aromatase activity, cortisol, vitamin D
**Peat's range:** No specific serum target. For a 35-year-old male: cortisol no higher than mid-range, estrogen below mid-range, testosterone above mid-range. Normal daily production: ~4 mg for young men; half that effective for middle-aged. Supplementation hierarchy: pregnenolone first, DHEA second, testosterone only if needed.
**Mainstream range:** Male total T: 300-1000 ng/dL; Female total T: 15-70 ng/dL

## Peat's Position

Peat valued testosterone as a protective hormone but emphasized its vulnerability to aromatase conversion. Unlike progesterone, which cannot be converted to estrogen, testosterone sits one enzymatic step from estradiol. Anything causing stress, irritation, or inflammation activates aromatase, converting testosterone to estrogen and reversing its protective effect. This makes testosterone a conditional protector -- beneficial when thyroid and progesterone are adequate, potentially harmful when they are not. The special difference between testosterone and DHT (dihydrotestosterone) is that testosterone is easily aromatized into estrogen, and DHT is not — the drug industry vilified DHT to justify their treatments.

Testosterone concentrates most in the heart, lungs, and brain -- not muscle, as commonly assumed. When researchers injected radioactive testosterone into athletes expecting to see it in muscles, the radiation appeared primarily in the heart. Testosterone (along with DHEA) protects these vital organs from cortisol-driven catabolism. After the thymus is consumed by cortisol, muscles break down next, while heart, lungs, and brain are spared longest due to their high concentrations of protective androgens. When those androgens are depleted, even vital organs sustain damage.

The thyroid connection is bidirectional and precise: too much thyroid wastes testosterone through accelerated clearance, while too little thyroid causes testosterone to be converted to estrogen via aromatase activation. Both extremes are harmful, making thyroid optimization (not maximization) essential for testosterone status. Low thyroid is the most common cause of a bad cortisol-to-testosterone ratio, because hypothyroidism means cholesterol cannot convert to pregnenolone, so cholesterol rises while all protective steroids (including testosterone) fall. For low testosterone with high cholesterol, a small supplement of Armour thyroid can quickly lower cholesterol by converting it to pregnenolone and DHEA, which would then raise testosterone.

Peat recommended a strict supplementation hierarchy: try pregnenolone first (the mother hormone that converts according to need), then DHEA (which the body can convert to testosterone), and only then direct testosterone if needed. Supplementing pregnenolone is safer than DHEA when thyroid is low, because stress hormones can cause DHEA to convert to estrogen. If a man supplements testosterone daily, the liver adapts over one to two weeks by upregulating testosterone-destroying enzymes, and at medical doses (50-100 mg), the liver may convert as much testosterone to estrogen as it preserves. A high-protein diet is helpful for testosterone, and avoiding polyunsaturated fats increases testosterone (coconut oil, butter instead). Excess tryptophan promotes catabolic cortisol, so supplementing gelatin can be helpful.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats testosterone decline in aging men as localized testicular failure ("andropause") requiring testosterone replacement therapy. Peat viewed this as a systemic metabolic problem -- rising estrogen, falling thyroid, PUFA accumulation -- not a testicular issue. Prescribing testosterone without addressing the systemic causes leads to liver adaptation, estrogen conversion, and potentially worse outcomes. The mainstream approach of high-dose TRT without thyroid, progesterone, or dietary correction would, in Peat's view, feed the aromatase pathway and worsen estrogen dominance over time.

Mainstream also frames SHBG as a problem when high (limiting "free" testosterone). Peat saw high SHBG as the body's defense against estrogen -- women with high SHBG have lower breast cancer risk. The concept of "free" vs. bound hormones is itself questionable: albumin-bound hormones enter cells effectively, so the "free hormone" theory is a laboratory construction, not a biological reality.

## Causal Connections

- Low thyroid -> high cholesterol (impaired conversion) -> low pregnenolone -> low testosterone
- Hypothyroidism -> aromatase activation -> testosterone converted to estrogen
- Hyperthyroidism -> accelerated testosterone clearance
- Stress/inflammation/injury -> aromatase activation -> testosterone to estrogen
- Brief resistance exercise (20-30 seconds) -> muscle shifts from cortisol to testosterone production
- Overtraining -> muscle produces cortisol instead of testosterone
- Testosterone + DHEA -> protect heart, lungs, brain from cortisol catabolism
- High-dose TRT -> liver adaptation in 1-2 weeks -> equal estrogen conversion
- Progesterone (low dose in men) -> inhibits aromatase -> makes testosterone more potent
- Pregnenolone -> converts to testosterone via DHEA according to body's need
- PUFA avoidance + high protein + gelatin -> supports testosterone production
- Thyroid + aspirin -> prevent DHEA-to-estrogen conversion (anti-inflammatory = anti-aromatase)
- Vitamin D supplementation -> often helps improve the hormone balance

## Sex Differences

In men, testosterone decline with aging reflects systemic metabolic deterioration: rising estrogen, falling thyroid, and decades of PUFA accumulation. Old men get low testosterone because stressed tissue converts it to estrogen. High estrogen then raises prolactin and growth hormone as secondary adaptations. The Russian approach -- brief, moderate resistance exercise (1-2 minutes of mild dumbbells and squats) -- shifts muscle from cortisol to testosterone production without the overtraining that activates cortisol.

In women, testosterone is still protective but at much lower concentrations (~0.5 mg/day production vs. ~4 mg/day in young men). The same brief exercise shifts the cortisol-testosterone balance in women. DHEA converts to testosterone and can have masculinizing effects in women, which is why Peat preferred pregnenolone as the first-line supplement for women.

Aromatase-knockout mice (no estrogen production) have twice as much testosterone in the brain as normal mice and age without dementia, demonstrating that estrogen is produced at the expense of brain testosterone.

## Medication Effects

- **Testosterone replacement therapy (TRT):** At medical doses (50-100 mg), the liver adapts within 1-2 weeks, upregulating testosterone-destroying enzymes. May produce equal estrogen effect. Topical testosterone is safe only if diet and thyroid function are good.
- **Pregnenolone:** Preferred first-line. 100 mg pregnenolone + 5 mg DHEA for testicular recovery after exogenous testosterone. Converts to what the body needs. Safer than DHEA when thyroid is low.
- **DHEA:** 10 mg safe for men. Converts to testosterone. For women, can have masculinizing effects -- use pregnenolone instead. Cautious if thyroid is low (converts to estrogen under stress). 5 mg with olive oil or butter can have noticeable effect on mood and muscle tone in a few hours.
- **Progesterone (men):** 5-10 mg reduces estrogen production and makes testosterone more potent. 30 mg may antagonize testosterone.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Essential -- both too much and too little thyroid impair testosterone status. Must be optimized, not maximized. A small supplement of Armour thyroid can quickly lower cholesterol by converting it to pregnenolone/DHEA, raising testosterone.
- **Aromatase inhibitors:** Block testosterone-to-estrogen conversion. Pharmaceutical (anastrozole) or dietary (naringenin from oranges, apigenin from guavas). Thyroid and aspirin also prevent conversion.
- **Statins:** Lower cholesterol, depleting raw material for testosterone synthesis.
- **Birth control pills (in female partners):** Environmental estrogen exposure. Yeast in beer/wine also produces estradiol directly.
