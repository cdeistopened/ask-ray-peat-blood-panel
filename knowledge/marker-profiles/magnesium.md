# Magnesium

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered serum magnesium unreliable because only 1% of body magnesium is in serum; RBC magnesium is better but still incomplete without thyroid context
**Companion markers:** RBC magnesium (preferred over serum), thyroid markers (TSH, T3), ATP, calcium, CO2/bicarbonate, potassium, progesterone, estrogen, sodium, prolactin
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically; ~420 mg RDA with average diet is enough; good thyroid function + adequate calcium/potassium/sodium decreases requirement. Build magnesium reserves BEFORE increasing thyroid supplementation.
**Mainstream range:** Serum Mg 1.7-2.2 mg/dL

## Peat's Position

Magnesium occupies a unique position in Peat's metabolic framework: it is the mineral signature of a well-energized cell. Most intracellular magnesium is bound to ATP molecules, forming the Mg-ATP complex that represents stored, immediately available energy. When ATP breaks down to ADP (the low-energy state), magnesium is released and calcium floods in. High Mg-ATP means the cell is relaxed, stable, and ready to work. Low Mg-ATP means the cell is excitable, stressed, and vulnerable. The magnesium-to-calcium balance inside cells is therefore a direct readout of metabolic health — and it depends entirely on the rate of ATP production. Magnesium retention by cells is dependent on thyroid hormone and is essential for cell relaxation. The Achilles tendon reflex relaxation test and the T-wave in ECG both indicate the rate of energy restoration, which depends on magnesium-thyroid interplay.

This is why Peat insisted that supplementing magnesium without adequate thyroid function is addressing a symptom rather than a cause. The cell loses magnesium because it cannot maintain ATP production, and it cannot maintain ATP production because thyroid is insufficient. Giving magnesium to a hypothyroid person may help temporarily, but the underlying energy deficit continues to drain magnesium from cells. The root intervention is thyroid support, which restores ATP production and allows cells to retain magnesium naturally. Sodium helps the body retain magnesium — both sodium and magnesium are lost easily when thyroid function is low. When thyroid status is good, the requirement for magnesium is easily met by ordinary foods.

However, Peat also warned emphatically that magnesium reserves must be built BEFORE increasing thyroid supplementation. When thyroid is increased in a magnesium-depleted body, the newly activated skeletal muscles (the body's largest magnesium consumer) soak up available magnesium from the blood, leaving the heart and brain deficient. This causes cramps, palpitations, anxiety, and arrhythmias — symptoms often misinterpreted as thyroid "sensitivity" or overdose. The heart and skeletal muscles literally compete for scarce magnesium. This explains why some people get heart palpitations when starting thyroid: it is not that thyroid is harmful, but that it reveals and intensifies a pre-existing magnesium deficit.

Magnesium deficiency causes seizures (calcium-driven neural excitation), insomnia (neurons cannot return to resting state), inflammation (calcium-activated enzymes go unopposed), cramps, constipation, anxiety, elevated blood pressure, and irregular heartbeat. These are all expressions of inadequate ATP-magnesium complexes allowing calcium to dominate cellular signaling. Correcting magnesium deficiency can help correct excessive prolactin.

CO2 retention — supported by adequate thyroid function, bag breathing, and avoidance of hyperventilation — helps cells retain both magnesium and potassium. Progesterone supports magnesium retention by opposing estrogen. Hyperventilation depletes CO2, destabilizing Mg-ATP complexes and releasing magnesium from cells.

Peat's preferred magnesium sources were the water from boiling greens (beet, chard, turnip, kale) and coffee. Coffee (dry instant) is approximately 0.5% magnesium, providing about 40 mg per strong cup — coffee and cocoa are very good magnesium sources. He used magnesium carbonate personally but reported that all chemical magnesium supplements contain manufacturing impurities that can cause bowel inflammation, such as hemorrhoids. Magnesium oxide is very poorly absorbed and should not be used; magnesium carbonate is well absorbed. Magnesium glycinate was reported tolerable by many. He stated "I rarely recommend any supplement. And I think it's best to get it from pure natural foods." Magnesium can also be absorbed through the skin using either MgCl2 or MgSO4 (Epsom salts) mixed with baking soda — Epsom salt baths were specifically recommended for hypothyroid patients.

## Mainstream Inversion

Standard medicine checks serum magnesium, which represents only 1% of body magnesium and is maintained by homeostatic mechanisms even during severe intracellular depletion. A "normal" serum magnesium can coexist with critically low cellular magnesium. RBC magnesium is somewhat better but still does not capture the ATP-bound magnesium that matters most. More importantly, mainstream medicine treats magnesium deficiency by supplementing magnesium without addressing why cells cannot retain it. In Peat's framework, cellular magnesium retention depends on ATP production, which depends on thyroid function. Supplementing magnesium without thyroid support is treating a downstream effect.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → low ATP production → Mg released from ATP → calcium influx → cell stress
- Magnesium depletion + thyroid increase → muscles compete with heart for scarce Mg → palpitations/arrhythmias
- Low CO2 (from hyperventilation or metabolic impairment) → unstable Mg-ATP → magnesium loss from cells
- Progesterone → supports magnesium retention; Estrogen → opposes magnesium retention
- Magnesium deficiency → calcium-driven excitation → seizures, insomnia, cramps, anxiety, hypertension
- CO2 retention (bag breathing, adequate thyroid) → helps cells retain magnesium and potassium
- Adequate thyroid → adequate ATP → Mg-ATP stable → cells relaxed and functional
- Sodium → helps retain magnesium; both lost easily when thyroid is low
- Magnesium deficiency → excessive prolactin; correcting Mg helps correct prolactin
- T3 (Cytomel) → improves magnesium retention → cellular relaxation
- Achilles tendon reflex relaxation rate → clinical indicator of Mg-thyroid-ATP axis

## Sex Differences

Estrogen opposes magnesium retention while progesterone supports it. Women in estrogen-dominant states (relative to progesterone) would be expected to have greater difficulty retaining intracellular magnesium, potentially explaining higher rates of cramps, migraines, anxiety, and insomnia in these hormonal states. The premenstrual period, when progesterone drops and estrogen becomes relatively dominant, often coincides with magnesium-deficiency-type symptoms.

## Medication Effects

- **Thyroid supplementation:** Increases magnesium utilization — beneficial if reserves are adequate and dangerous if they are not. Build magnesium reserves first, then increase thyroid gradually. T3 specifically improves magnesium retention and cellular relaxation.
- **Magnesium carbonate:** Well absorbed; Peat's preferred supplement form. Can cause intestinal irritation due to manufacturing impurities.
- **Magnesium oxide:** Very poorly absorbed — do not use.
- **Magnesium glycinate:** Reported tolerable by many people.
- **Epsom salt baths (MgSO4):** Transdermal absorption with baking soda. Specifically recommended for hypothyroid patients.
- **MgCl2 topical:** Can also be absorbed through the skin with baking soda.
- **Food sources:** Water from boiled greens (beet, chard, turnip, kale), coffee (~40mg per cup), cocoa. With good thyroid function, food sources sufficient without supplements.
- **Pharmaceutical diuretics:** Deplete magnesium and potassium, worsening the cellular energy deficit. Adequate sodium (natural diuretic) is preferred.
