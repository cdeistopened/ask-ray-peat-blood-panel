# Vitamin D (25-OH)

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat explicitly recommended vitamin D blood testing as useful and actionable; one of the few blood tests he endorsed
**Companion markers:** PTH, calcium, phosphate, thyroid markers, vitamin K, cholesterol
**Peat's range:** 40-60 ng/mL, target ~50 ng/mL (explicitly stated: "I think 50 ng/ml is a good goal. The point at which it lowers parathyroid hormone would be the right amount.")
**Mainstream range:** 30-100 ng/mL (sufficient); <20 ng/mL = deficient; <30 ng/mL = insufficient

## Peat's Position

Vitamin D holds a unique position in Peat's diagnostic framework: it is one of the few blood tests he specifically and repeatedly recommended. Despite broad skepticism of most lab values, Peat stated plainly that "having a Vitamin D blood test is a good idea; levels should be around 50 ng/mL." He recommended maintaining levels in the 40-60 ng/mL range, with 50 as the specific target — the point at which parathyroid hormone is lowered.

Peat classified vitamin D not as a vitamin but as a steroid hormone synthesized from cholesterol under sunlight, placing it in the same family as progesterone, pregnenolone, and DHEA. The vitamin D receptor exists in virtually every cell, reflecting its systemic importance. But its most critical function in Peat's framework is suppressing parathyroid hormone (PTH), which Peat identified as a central driver of aging, calcification, and metabolic disease. Vitamin D and calcium together suppress PTH. When either is deficient, PTH rises, triggering bone dissolution, soft tissue calcification, and impaired oxidative metabolism. This makes vitamin D not primarily about bone health (the mainstream framing) but about suppressing one of the body's most destructive stress hormones. PTH increases with age and in excess causes insomnia, seizures, dementia, psychosis, cancer, heart disease, respiratory distress, osteoporosis, sarcopenia, histamine release, inflammation, and soft tissue calcification.

Low thyroid function increases the body's requirement for vitamin D. People with persistent low 25-OH-D despite supplementation may have liver dysfunction preventing hydroxylation — the liver catalyzes hydroxylation of cholecalciferol to 25-OH-D, and many nutritional deficiencies can affect the liver's functions.

The clinical urgency of vitamin D status was underscored by a Mississippi study Peat cited: of 278 ICU patients, only 3 had normal vitamin D. Some had levels as low as 8-10 ng/mL. Supplementing vitamin D cut ICU stays in half. This connects critical illness to vitamin D deficiency through the PTH-calcium-metabolic axis.

Peat personally recommended 2,000 IU daily as a standard winter dose, with some individuals needing 5,000 IU based on blood tests. Up to 10,000 IU for a couple of winter months is safe, but it is better to increase calcium and vitamin K while keeping vitamin D a little lower, unless blood levels are monitored. Vitamin D, calcium, and vitamin K work as a triad — increasing the cofactors allows a lower D dose to achieve the same PTH-suppressing effect. Vitamin D3, vitamin K, and selenium deficiencies are "extremely widespread" according to Peat. He personally applied approximately 20,000 IU of vitamin D oil to his skin transdermally during winter, and also used several quarts of milk with added vitamin D, ate liver, and occasionally used ultraviolet light.

If vitamin D has been very low for a long time, the parathyroid glands probably enlarged and may take extended time to normalize even with generous supplementation. Peat personally consumed at least two quarts of milk daily (historically averaging a gallon a day) specifically to suppress PTH, combined with 2,000-6,000 IU of vitamin D3 daily during winter.

Sunlight is the ideal vitamin D source, but Peat noted it requires low tissue PUFA to be safe. High PUFA in skin undergoes lipid peroxidation under UV radiation, causing damage. A person who has reduced their tissue PUFA load tolerates sun better and synthesizes vitamin D more safely.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine frames vitamin D primarily in terms of bone health and calcium absorption. Peat's framing centers PTH suppression as the critical function — vitamin D's importance lies in preventing the cascade of damage caused by elevated PTH (bone dissolution, soft tissue calcification, impaired oxidative metabolism, accelerated aging). The mainstream threshold of 30 ng/mL as "sufficient" is too low in Peat's view; his 40-60 range demands a higher target. There has been very little attention given to the optimal level of parathyroid hormone, as opposed to the "normal range" — Peat implied the optimal level is much lower than the standard reference range.

Additionally, mainstream warnings about calcium supplementation causing heart disease reflect a misunderstanding of the calcium-PTH axis: dietary calcium suppresses PTH, which is the hormone that actually causes arterial calcification.

## Causal Connections

- Low vitamin D → elevated PTH → bone dissolution + soft tissue calcification + impaired cellular respiration
- Vitamin D + adequate calcium → PTH suppression → calcium stays in bones, out of arteries
- Vitamin D synthesized from cholesterol via UV → connects to cholesterol-steroid hormone axis
- Low vitamin D + low calcium + high phosphate → maximal PTH elevation → accelerated aging
- High tissue PUFA + UV exposure → lipid peroxidation in skin → damage despite vitamin D synthesis
- Vitamin D + thyroid → synergistic metabolic support (Peat noted they "work closely together")
- Low thyroid → increased vitamin D requirement
- Liver dysfunction → impaired 25-OH-D hydroxylation → persistent low D despite supplementation
- Chronic low vitamin D → parathyroid gland enlargement → slow normalization even with correction
- Deficiency of calcium or vitamin D → ↑PTH → ↑serum calcium + ↑blood pressure
- PTH ends hair growth cycle → nocturnal PTH surges → morning hair loss → milk at bedtime may protect
- Prolactin → calcium mobilization from bone → estrogen-driven prolactin worsens osteoporosis
- PTH is one of the "uremic toxins" in kidney disease

## Sex Differences

No explicit sex-specific differences in vitamin D interpretation were found in the extracted claims. However, given that estrogen promotes PTH secretion and calcium loss from bone, women with estrogen dominance may have a greater need for adequate vitamin D to counterbalance estrogen-driven PTH elevation. Prolactin contributes to osteoporosis by mobilizing calcium from bones; estrogen increases prolactin, so using estrogen to "prevent" osteoporosis may actually worsen it through the prolactin pathway.

## Medication Effects

- **Vitamin D supplementation:** 2,000 IU standard winter dose; 5,000 IU for some individuals based on blood tests; 10,000 IU safe for a couple of months. Better to increase calcium and vitamin K while keeping D moderate. Peat preferred Carlson's brand for oral. Transdermal application (~20,000 IU oil on skin) was his personal method.
- **Vitamin K (K2):** 1 mg should accompany vitamin D to ensure calcium directed to bones rather than arteries. Vitamin D, calcium, and vitamin K work as a triad.
- **Calcium (dairy, eggshell powder):** Essential companion — 2+ quarts milk daily specifically to suppress PTH. A 2:1 calcium-to-phosphorus ratio is safe.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Low thyroid increases vitamin D requirement; adequate thyroid supports the vitamin D-PTH axis.
- **Very high vitamin D doses without adequate calcium or vitamin K:** May worsen calcium misdistribution. Vitamin D has dose-dependent steroid effects.
- **Vitamin A supplements:** Must be balanced with vitamin D; too much vitamin A with low thyroid suppresses thyroid further.
