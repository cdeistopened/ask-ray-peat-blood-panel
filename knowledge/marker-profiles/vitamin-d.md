# Vitamin D (25-OH)

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat explicitly recommended vitamin D blood testing as useful and actionable; one of the few blood tests he endorsed
**Companion markers:** PTH, calcium, phosphate, thyroid markers, vitamin K, cholesterol
**Peat's range:** 40-60 ng/mL, target ~50 ng/mL
**Mainstream range:** 30-100 ng/mL (sufficient); <20 ng/mL = deficient; <30 ng/mL = insufficient

## Peat's Position

Vitamin D holds a unique position in Peat's diagnostic framework: it is one of the few blood tests he specifically and repeatedly recommended. Despite broad skepticism of most lab values, Peat stated plainly that "having a Vitamin D blood test is a good idea; levels should be around 50 ng/mL." He recommended maintaining levels in the 40-60 ng/mL range, with 50 as the specific target.

Peat classified vitamin D not as a vitamin but as a steroid hormone synthesized from cholesterol under sunlight, placing it in the same family as progesterone, pregnenolone, and DHEA. The vitamin D receptor exists in virtually every cell, reflecting its systemic importance. But its most critical function in Peat's framework is suppressing parathyroid hormone (PTH), which Peat identified as a central driver of aging, calcification, and metabolic disease. Vitamin D and calcium together suppress PTH. When either is deficient, PTH rises, triggering bone dissolution, soft tissue calcification, and impaired oxidative metabolism. This makes vitamin D not primarily about bone health (the mainstream framing) but about suppressing one of the body's most destructive stress hormones.

The clinical urgency of vitamin D status was underscored by a Mississippi study Peat cited: of 278 ICU patients, only 3 had normal vitamin D. Some had levels as low as 8-10 ng/mL. Supplementing vitamin D cut ICU stays in half. This connects critical illness to vitamin D deficiency through the PTH-calcium-metabolic axis.

Peat recommended 2,000-6,000 IU vitamin D3 daily in winter, with 5,000 IU as a reasonable adult dose. Up to 10,000 IU for a couple of winter months is safe but unnecessary. However, he was cautious about very high-dose supplementation: excessive vitamin D can promote calcium absorption beyond what the body can distribute, potentially contributing to the soft tissue calcification that moderate D prevents. He preferred keeping the dose moderate while ensuring adequate calcium and vitamin K intake. Vitamin K activates proteins that direct calcium to bones and keep it out of arteries, making it an essential companion to vitamin D.

Sunlight is the ideal vitamin D source, but Peat noted it requires low tissue PUFA to be safe. High PUFA in skin undergoes lipid peroxidation under UV radiation, causing damage. A person who has reduced their tissue PUFA load tolerates sun better and synthesizes vitamin D more safely. Vitamin D and thyroid work closely together — Peat noted this connection explicitly — and adequate vitamin D is part of the metabolic foundation that supports thyroid function.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine frames vitamin D primarily in terms of bone health and calcium absorption. Peat's framing centers PTH suppression as the critical function — vitamin D's importance lies in preventing the cascade of damage caused by elevated PTH (bone dissolution, soft tissue calcification, impaired oxidative metabolism, accelerated aging). The mainstream threshold of 30 ng/mL as "sufficient" is too low in Peat's view; his 40-60 range demands a higher target. Additionally, mainstream warnings about calcium supplementation causing heart disease reflect a misunderstanding of the calcium-PTH axis: dietary calcium suppresses PTH, which is the hormone that actually causes arterial calcification.

## Causal Connections

- Low vitamin D → elevated PTH → bone dissolution + soft tissue calcification + impaired cellular respiration
- Vitamin D + adequate calcium → PTH suppression → calcium stays in bones, out of arteries
- Vitamin D synthesized from cholesterol via UV → connects to cholesterol-steroid hormone axis
- Low vitamin D + low calcium + high phosphate → maximal PTH elevation → accelerated aging
- High tissue PUFA + UV exposure → lipid peroxidation in skin → damage despite vitamin D synthesis
- Vitamin D + thyroid → synergistic metabolic support (Peat noted they "work closely together")

## Sex Differences

No explicit sex-specific differences in vitamin D interpretation were found in the extracted claims. However, given that estrogen promotes PTH secretion and calcium loss from bone (per the calcium extraction), women with estrogen dominance may have a greater need for adequate vitamin D to counterbalance estrogen-driven PTH elevation.

## Medication Effects

Vitamin D supplementation at 2,000-6,000 IU daily in winter is straightforward. Vitamin K (1 mg K2) should accompany vitamin D to ensure calcium is directed to bones rather than arteries. Very high vitamin D doses without adequate calcium or vitamin K may worsen calcium misdistribution. Vitamin D has dose-dependent steroid effects — supraphysiological levels can cause inappropriate calcium distribution. Peat noted that vitamin A supplements and vitamin D must be balanced: too much vitamin A with low thyroid suppresses thyroid further, and these fat-soluble vitamins interact.
