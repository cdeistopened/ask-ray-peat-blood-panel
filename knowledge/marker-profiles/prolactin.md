# Prolactin

**Reliability:** Tier 1 -- Peat explicitly endorsed prolactin as a reliable stress indicator on a lab panel. It reflects pituitary stress state and should be included in routine hormonal workup.
**Companion markers:** TSH, thyroid (T3/T4), estrogen, progesterone, serotonin, vitamin B6, calcium
**Peat's range:** <10 ng/mL ideal (implied). 18-20 ng/mL = already stressed. The mainstream "normal" range was raised because the average person is now hyperprolactinemic.
**Mainstream range:** Women: 2-29 ng/mL; Men: 2-18 ng/mL

## Peat's Position

Prolactin is one of the most straightforward stress indicators available on a standard lab panel. Peat warned against being reassured by prolactin of 18 or 20 ng/mL -- at that level, everything is already under stress. The normal range was lowered about 30 years ago, then raised again because the average person became increasingly hyperprolactinemic. This reference range inflation means "normal" now includes pathologically elevated values.

Prolactin and TSH tend to increase together, and both reflect inadequate thyroid function. This makes prolactin a sensitive early indicator of hypothyroidism before TSH alone would trigger clinical attention. Prolactin rises steadily with aging, paralleling the decline in protective hormones, but Peat considered this pathological, not normal aging. In men, the pattern is particularly clear: stressed tissue converts testosterone to estrogen via aromatase, and high estrogen brings up prolactin, growth hormone, and other adaptive secondary hormones.

A particularly important finding in the corpus: stress induces all pituitary hormones except luteinizing hormone (LH), which produces testosterone and progesterone. This means stress simultaneously raises prolactin while lowering the gonadotropin responsible for protective steroids -- a double blow. Serotonin drives all pituitary hormones, so chronic serotonin excess from stress contributes to all tumor developments driven by the pituitary gland, including prolactinomas. Birth control pills cause an epidemic of prolactin-secreting pituitary tumors through chronic estrogen stimulation of prolactin-producing cells.

Prolactin has direct damaging effects beyond being a stress marker. It has a bone-weakening effect: it normally takes calcium out of bone to put into milk during lactation, but outside that context, it causes calcium loss and contributes to osteoporosis. Both hirsutism (excess hair) and hair loss are associated with high prolactin -- the presentation depends on whether prolactin is steadily high or surging. A common misdiagnosis: hypothyroidism causes pituitary enlargement from a prolactin-secreting tumor, pressing on the optic nerve and causing vision loss often blamed on MS or brain disease.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine tests prolactin mainly when pituitary tumor or galactorrhea is suspected, not as a routine stress marker. A prolactin of 20 ng/mL in a woman would not trigger investigation. Peat considered this a missed diagnostic opportunity -- prolactin at that level already indicates systemic stress. Mainstream also does not connect prolactin to thyroid status as directly as Peat did; the TSH-prolactin co-elevation pattern is recognized in endocrinology but not typically used for early hypothyroid detection. The bone-weakening effect of prolactin is underappreciated outside the context of prolactinoma.

## Causal Connections

- Low thyroid -> pituitary stress -> prolactin rises (alongside TSH)
- Estrogen -> chronic pituitary prolactin cell stimulation -> prolactinoma
- Serotonin -> drives all pituitary hormones including prolactin
- Stress -> all pituitary hormones rise EXCEPT LH -> prolactin up, protective steroids down
- High prolactin -> bone calcium mobilization -> osteoporosis
- High prolactin -> vision loss (pituitary tumor pressing optic nerve)
- Prolactin + cortisol + PTH -> coordinate similar metabolic damage
- Intense breathing / high oxygen -> prolactin rises as stress reaction
- Calcium in diet -> suppresses prolactin
- Thyroid supplementation -> most reliable way to lower prolactin

## Sex Differences

In women, prolactin elevation is more common due to higher baseline estrogen driving pituitary stimulation. Birth control pills are a major cause of prolactinomas. Both hirsutism and hair loss in women are associated with high prolactin; thyroid supplementation is the most reliable control. Steady high prolactin causes hair loss; surging prolactin causes hirsutism.

In men, prolactin rises when stressed tissue converts testosterone to estrogen. High estrogen then brings up prolactin along with growth hormone. The pattern is: stress -> aromatase -> testosterone becomes estrogen -> estrogen raises prolactin. This creates a vicious cycle where prolactin's stress effects worsen the metabolic deterioration.

## Medication Effects

- **Thyroid supplementation:** Most reliable way to lower prolactin. Addresses the root cause.
- **Vitamin B6:** Directly inhibits prolactin secretion. Useful for prolactin resistant to thyroid alone.
- **Calcium supplementation:** Suppresses prolactin alongside cortisol and serotonin.
- **Salt (sodium):** Helps lower prolactin when thyroid alone is insufficient.
- **Vitamin A:** Supports thyroid-prolactin axis normalization.
- **Birth control pills:** Chronic estrogen causes prolactinoma epidemic. Major prolactin elevator.
- **SSRIs:** Raise serotonin, which drives all pituitary hormones including prolactin.
- **Dopamine agonists (cabergoline/bromocriptine):** Standard medical treatment for prolactinoma. Peat's corpus does not extensively discuss these but the mechanism (dopamine suppresses prolactin) is consistent with his framework.
