# HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein)

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered high HDL ambiguous because it rises as a defensive reaction to stress, toxins, and estrogen, not only as a sign of health; enrichment confirms elderly mortality data showing non-HDL cholesterol rising = lower death risk
**Companion markers:** Estrogen, cortisol, alcohol intake, chemical exposure, cancer markers, inflammatory markers, total cholesterol, non-HDL cholesterol
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically; context of elevation matters more than the number. In the elderly, rising non-HDL cholesterol predicts lower mortality.
**Mainstream range:** >60 mg/dL (protective); >40 (men), >50 (women) minimum

## Peat's Position

Peat challenged the simple equation that "higher HDL = better health." While the apolipoprotein that forms HDL (ApoA-I) does have genuine anti-inflammatory properties, the reason HDL rises in many contexts is as a defensive reaction to stress, toxins, and inflammatory substances — not as a sign of robust health. The things that poison the liver or stress the body increase HDL production as a compensatory anti-inflammatory response. Estrogen and alcohol, for example, both increase HDL defensively.

This defensive interpretation changes how extremely high HDL should be read. Under extreme chemical exposure, HDL increases and is associated with higher cancer incidence — suggesting that very high HDL can be a stress marker indicating chronic toxic exposure rather than cardiovascular protection. The body is producing more HDL because it needs more anti-inflammatory capacity, not because it is thriving.

The enrichment data strengthens this framework by providing the elderly mortality data from the opposite direction. In people in their 70s, each 10 mg/dL increase in non-HDL cholesterol was associated with a mortality odds ratio of 0.67 — meaning rising non-HDL cholesterol reduced death risk by 33% per 10-point increase. This is the opposite of what is recommended. The significance for HDL interpretation is that mainstream medicine uses the total-cholesterol-to-HDL ratio or non-HDL cholesterol as risk predictors, but in the elderly these relationships invert. Higher non-HDL cholesterol (which includes LDL) correlates with survival, not death. The standard interpretation of "raise HDL, lower LDL" becomes actively harmful guidance for older patients.

Peat noted that the standard framing of the LDL/HDL ratio oversimplifies the biology. The context of what is in the particles (saturated vs. PUFA content) matters more than the particle count or ratio. The conventional interpretation that high HDL protects and high LDL harms misses the underlying mechanisms entirely.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats HDL as unambiguously protective — "good cholesterol" — and interprets higher values as better cardiovascular health. Estrogen's ability to raise HDL has been cited as evidence that estrogen is heart-protective. Peat inverted both claims. He argued that HDL rises as a stress defense, making it an ambiguous marker. Estrogen raises HDL not because it promotes cardiovascular health but because estrogen is a stressor that triggers anti-inflammatory compensation. The HDL rise from estrogen is a sign of the body defending itself, not of estrogen's benefit. Similarly, alcohol raises HDL defensively, not therapeutically. The association of high HDL with better outcomes in some studies reflects the anti-inflammatory properties of the ApoA-I protein, but the elevation itself may indicate ongoing stress exposure that needs investigation rather than celebration.

The elderly mortality data further undermines the standard interpretation: rising non-HDL cholesterol in the elderly predicts lower death risk, directly contradicting the standard recommendation to minimize non-HDL cholesterol at all ages.

## Causal Connections

- Stress/toxins/estrogen/alcohol → ↑HDL (defensive anti-inflammatory response)
- Chemical exposure → ↑HDL → associated with ↑cancer incidence
- Estrogen → ↑HDL (misinterpreted as cardioprotective)
- ApoA-I protein → genuine anti-inflammatory activity (independent of elevation context)
- Liver poisoning → ↑HDL (compensatory response to hepatotoxic stress)
- Elderly: ↑non-HDL cholesterol → ↓mortality (OR 0.67 per 10 mg/dL increase)
- Standard lipid ratio targets → potentially harmful in elderly (inverting the relationship)

## Sex Differences

Women typically have higher HDL than men, partly because of higher estrogen levels. In Peat's framework, this estrogen-driven HDL elevation is a defensive response rather than evidence of superior cardiovascular protection. The mainstream interpretation that women's higher HDL explains their lower cardiovascular risk misses the stress-defense mechanism behind the elevation.

## Medication Effects

- **Estrogen/birth control:** Raises HDL defensively, which mainstream medicine misinterprets as cardioprotective. In Peat's view, the HDL rise signals estrogen-induced stress, not benefit.
- **Alcohol:** Raises HDL through the same defensive stress mechanism.
- **Statins:** Some statins raise HDL modestly; in Peat's framework this would be secondary to the more important question of what is driving the lipid pattern.
- **Niacin:** Raises HDL in standard medicine; Peat valued niacin primarily for lowering free fatty acids rather than its HDL effects.
