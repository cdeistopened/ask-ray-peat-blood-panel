# Red Blood Cells (RBC / Hemoglobin / Hematocrit)

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat found hemoglobin useful but insisted it must be interpreted alongside thyroid, copper, and iron utilization markers rather than taken at face value
**Companion markers:** TSH, free T3, ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, ceruloplasmin, CO2/bicarbonate, temperature/pulse
**Peat's range:** Hemoglobin at the low end of the normal range is "the safest place to be"
**Mainstream range:** Hemoglobin M: 13.2-16.6 g/dL, F: 11.6-15 g/dL; Hematocrit M: 38.3-48.6%, F: 35.5-44.9%

## Peat's Position

For Peat, red blood cells are not primarily about how many you have or how much hemoglobin they carry. The central question is whether those red blood cells are actually delivering oxygen to the mitochondria. This delivery depends on the Bohr effect: CO2 and low pH cause hemoglobin to release oxygen into tissues. Without sufficient CO2, oxygen stays bound to hemoglobin and never reaches the cells where energy is produced. A person with normal hemoglobin and normal blood oxygen saturation can be in a state of functional hypoxia if their CO2 production is too low. Yandel Henderson showed that at sea level, the relatively high oxygen environment can push too much CO2 out of blood, starving peripheral tissues even under normal conditions. This reframes the entire meaning of the RBC panel from a count-and-concentration exercise to a metabolic function question.

Peat viewed much of what is diagnosed as iron-deficiency anemia as a functional utilization problem rather than a true iron shortage. Hypothyroidism impairs the body's ability to incorporate iron into hemoglobin. Blood tests show low hemoglobin — it looks like iron deficiency — but iron stores may be normal or even high. Supplementing iron in this situation does not fix the hemoglobin problem; it just increases tissue iron stores and accelerates oxidative damage via Fenton chemistry. The correct intervention is thyroid support, not iron pills. Similarly, copper deficiency produces anemia indistinguishable from iron deficiency on standard labs. Without adequate copper, ceruloplasmin cannot oxidize iron for transport, so iron accumulates in toxic ferrous form while hemoglobin production suffers.

The thyroid connection runs deeper than iron utilization. Low thyroid function increases blood viscosity, producing thick, sluggish blood that forces the heart to pump harder. This is a direct mechanism linking hypothyroidism to hypertension. Thyroid supplementation can lower blood pressure by reducing blood viscosity and improving tissue oxygenation. The body also adapts to altitude by producing more RBCs, but Peat emphasized that the more important altitude adaptation is improved CO2 retention — better oxygen delivery through the Bohr effect, not just more hemoglobin.

Red blood cells themselves are metabolically unique: they lack mitochondria and rely entirely on glycolysis. Peat noted this is normal for RBCs and not pathological, unlike when nucleated cells shift to glycolysis. Every heme molecule requires glycine for synthesis, connecting RBC production to adequate gelatin/collagen protein intake. Peat also noted that hemoglobin is safest at the low end of the normal range, viewing high-normal hemoglobin with suspicion because of the associated iron burden.

## Mainstream Inversion

Standard medicine treats low hemoglobin as a near-automatic indication for iron supplementation. Peat argued this is backwards in many cases: the low hemoglobin reflects impaired iron utilization from hypothyroidism or copper deficiency, not insufficient iron. Adding iron to someone who cannot properly incorporate it into hemoglobin increases tissue iron stores, fueling Fenton chemistry and oxidative damage. The mainstream approach treats a lab number while potentially accelerating degenerative disease. Additionally, mainstream medicine views higher hemoglobin as healthier (within range), while Peat considered the low end of normal the safest position because it implies lower tissue iron burden.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → impaired iron incorporation into hemoglobin → "functional anemia" with normal iron stores
- Copper deficiency → low ceruloplasmin → iron stuck in toxic ferrous form → anemia mimicking iron deficiency
- Low thyroid → low CO2 production → impaired Bohr effect → hemoglobin holds oxygen → functional tissue hypoxia despite normal SpO2
- Hypothyroidism → increased blood viscosity → hypertension → heart strain
- Estrogen → altered iron metabolism → iron retention in tissues → reduced hemoglobin synthesis
- Glycine deficiency → impaired heme synthesis → reduced hemoglobin production
- Iron (ferrous) + H2O2 → hydroxyl radicals (Fenton chemistry) → lipid peroxidation → lipofuscin accumulation

## Sex Differences

Women's lower hemoglobin compared to men is not solely explained by menstrual blood loss. Estrogen itself alters iron metabolism, promoting iron retention in tissues while reducing hemoglobin synthesis. Estrogen acts as a redox catalyst that shifts iron toward tissue storage and away from hemoglobin incorporation. Menstrual blood loss is actually protective — it provides a natural iron-reduction mechanism, which Peat argued was one reason premenopausal women have lower rates of heart disease and cancer. After menopause, when this mechanism stops, women's disease rates converge with men's.

## Medication Effects

Thyroid supplementation (T3) can raise hemoglobin by improving iron incorporation — this is treating the root cause rather than the lab number. Iron supplementation without thyroid correction may raise hemoglobin slightly while increasing tissue iron burden and oxidative damage. Aspirin, coffee, and vitamin E are protective against iron toxicity: coffee binds iron in the gut reducing absorption, aspirin acts as an antioxidant and possible iron binder, and vitamin E interrupts iron-catalyzed free radical chains.
