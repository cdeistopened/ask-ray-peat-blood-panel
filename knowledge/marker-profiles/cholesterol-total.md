# Total Cholesterol

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat considered cholesterol one of the most useful blood markers because it serves as a direct mirror of thyroid function
**Companion markers:** TSH, T3, T4, temperature, pulse, pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, vitamin A
**Peat's range:** 160-220 mg/dL (context-dependent; ~270 associated with longest survival in elderly)
**Mainstream range:** <200 mg/dL (desirable), 200-239 (borderline high), >240 (high)

## Peat's Position

Cholesterol occupies a central place in Peat's metabolic framework as both a diagnostic marker and a steroid hormone precursor. He described cholesterol and thyroid as "mirror images" — removing the thyroid gland causes an immediate cholesterol rise; supplementing thyroid causes an immediate drop. They move in exact inverse relationship. This is because thyroid hormone drives the enzymatic conversion of cholesterol into pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, and all other steroid hormones. When thyroid is inadequate, this conversion stalls and cholesterol accumulates in the blood unconverted. High cholesterol, in Peat's view, is not a disease requiring treatment but a symptom of inadequate thyroid function.

Peat suggested keeping cholesterol in the range of 160-220, noting this requires enough thyroid to keep TSH low. He witnessed people with cholesterol of 400-500 mg/dL lower it to normal within a single week using frequent small doses of active T3 thyroid hormone — roughly 5 micrograms per hour. This dramatic response proved that the cholesterol-to-steroid pathway was simply blocked by inadequate thyroid, not that cholesterol itself was pathological. Conversely, orange juice consumption could raise dangerously low cholesterol from 130 to 160-170 in just a couple of weeks, since sugar is a necessary substrate for hepatic cholesterol synthesis.

Peat was equally concerned about cholesterol that is too low. Below 160, combined with low thyroid, protective steroids are not produced in normal amounts, leading to inflammatory processes, connective tissue pain, waist fat, and constipation. Very low cholesterol (below 160-180) is associated with increased cancer, accidents, homicides, and suicide. Violent offenders are connected with cholesterol below 180. The brain produces neurosteroids at 10 times blood levels using cholesterol as the raw material, and the Framingham study showed that people over 50 whose cholesterol was below 200 were more likely to develop dementia. In rest home studies, the longest-lived residents had cholesterol around 270.

The synthesis of pregnenolone from cholesterol requires three inputs: thyroid hormone, vitamin A, and cholesterol itself. Deficiency in any one reduces all downstream protective steroids. Additionally, bacterial endotoxin from the gut poisons the liver and stops it from making adequate cholesterol, representing another pathway to dangerously low levels. Peat acknowledged that no one is really sure of the single optimal cholesterol number, emphasizing that context — thyroid status, age, steroid hormone production — matters more than any absolute value.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats total cholesterol above 200 as a risk factor requiring intervention (usually statins), with lower being better. Peat inverted this on multiple levels. First, he argued that high cholesterol is a symptom of hypothyroidism, not an independent disease — the correct treatment is thyroid support, not cholesterol reduction. Second, he cited the Hungarian study showing that artificially lowering cholesterol increased cancer mortality, total mortality, and death from accidents, homicide, and even heart disease and strokes. Third, he argued that cholesterol-lowering interventions of all kinds — whether drugs, PUFA, or dietary restriction — tend to promote cancer and degenerative disease by suppressing steroid hormone production. Fourth, the original atherosclerosis experiments used toxic oxidized cholesterol, not native cholesterol, and unoxidized cholesterol does not damage blood vessels. The problem is not cholesterol itself but PUFA in lipoproteins undergoing peroxidation. Fifth, dietary cholesterol does not meaningfully raise blood cholesterol because the liver's production is feedback-regulated. Mainstream fear of dietary cholesterol was unfounded.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → ↓cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion → ↑blood cholesterol
- Thyroid supplementation (T3) → ↑conversion → ↓cholesterol (within days)
- ↓Cholesterol (<160) + ↓thyroid → ↓protective steroids → inflammation, cancer, cognitive decline
- ↓Cholesterol → ↓brain neurosteroid precursors → depression, violence, dementia risk
- ↑Cholesterol → normal if thyroid is adequate (being converted to steroids)
- Cholesterol + thyroid + vitamin A → pregnenolone → progesterone/DHEA/cortisol/testosterone
- Endotoxin → liver damage → ↓cholesterol synthesis → dangerously low levels
- PUFA in lipoproteins → oxidized cholesterol → arterial damage (the actual atherogenic agent)
- Sugar/fructose → liver substrate → adequate cholesterol synthesis
- Statins/PUFA/dietary restriction → ↓cholesterol → ↓steroid hormones → ↑cancer/degeneration

## Sex Differences

No sex-specific cholesterol claims found in corpus, though Peat's framework implies that women's higher estrogen (which impairs T4-to-T3 conversion) makes them more susceptible to the hypothyroid pattern of high cholesterol that mainstream medicine would treat with statins rather than thyroid support.

## Medication Effects

- **Statins:** Peat opposed statin therapy. By lowering cholesterol, statins deprive the body of steroid hormone precursors. The Hungarian study and others showed that cholesterol-lowering increased total mortality, cancer, and violent death.
- **Thyroid (T3, Armour, Cynoplus):** Rapidly lowers cholesterol by driving its conversion to steroid hormones. People with cholesterol of 400-500 can normalize in one week with hourly T3 doses.
- **PUFA/seed oils:** Lower cholesterol through thyroid suppression and increased cellular stress — the cholesterol-lowering effect is a sign of toxicity, not benefit.
- **Orange juice/sugar:** Can raise dangerously low cholesterol by providing substrate for hepatic synthesis.
