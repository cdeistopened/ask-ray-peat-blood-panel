# Total Cholesterol

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat considered cholesterol one of the most useful blood markers because it serves as a direct mirror of thyroid function
**Companion markers:** TSH, T3, T4, temperature, pulse, pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, vitamin A
**Peat's range:** 160-220 mg/dL (context-dependent; ~270 associated with longest survival in elderly; each 10 mg/dL increase in non-HDL cholesterol in 70s associated with 0.67 mortality odds ratio)
**Mainstream range:** <200 mg/dL (desirable), 200-239 (borderline high), >240 (high)

## Peat's Position

Cholesterol occupies a central place in Peat's metabolic framework as both a diagnostic marker and a steroid hormone precursor. He described cholesterol and thyroid as "mirror images" — removing the thyroid gland causes an immediate cholesterol rise; supplementing thyroid causes an immediate drop. They move in exact inverse relationship. This is because thyroid hormone drives the enzymatic conversion of cholesterol into pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, and all other steroid hormones. When thyroid is inadequate, this conversion stalls and cholesterol accumulates in the blood unconverted. High cholesterol, in Peat's view, is not a disease requiring treatment but a symptom of inadequate thyroid function.

Peat suggested keeping cholesterol in the range of 160-220, noting this requires enough thyroid to keep TSH low. He witnessed people with cholesterol of 400-500 mg/dL lower it to normal within a single week using frequent small doses of active T3 thyroid hormone — roughly 5 micrograms per hour. This dramatic response proved that the cholesterol-to-steroid pathway was simply blocked by inadequate thyroid, not that cholesterol itself was pathological. Conversely, orange juice consumption could raise dangerously low cholesterol from 130 to 160-170 in just a couple of weeks, since sugar is a necessary substrate for hepatic cholesterol synthesis. Custards, sweet fruits, and Haagen Dazs ice cream are safe ways to increase low cholesterol — these provide the combination of sugar (for liver cholesterol synthesis) and saturated fat needed to support production.

Peat was equally concerned about cholesterol that is too low. Below 160, combined with low thyroid, protective steroids are not produced in normal amounts, leading to inflammatory processes, connective tissue pain, waist fat, and constipation. Very low cholesterol (below 160-180) is associated with increased cancer, accidents, homicides, and suicide. Violent offenders are connected with cholesterol below 180. The brain produces neurosteroids at 10 times blood levels using cholesterol as the raw material, and the Framingham study showed that people over 50 whose cholesterol was below 200 were more likely to develop dementia. In rest home studies, the longest-lived residents had cholesterol around 270. In people in their 70s, each 10 mg/dL increase in non-HDL cholesterol was associated with a mortality odds ratio of 0.67 — higher cholesterol predicted lower death risk.

Cholesterol also serves a direct structural function beyond hormone synthesis. It protects red blood cells from disintegration — lowering it experimentally makes cells more rigid and fragile, while restoring cholesterol restores flexibility. It protects cells against hemolytic poisons including snake venom and chloroform by forming innocuous compounds with them. Aging is a state of cholesterol starvation — old skin has half the cholesterol of young skin, and the brain's cholesterol synthesis decreases with age. When skin's free cholesterol is increased experimentally, it regains its ability to shed dead superficial cells; when lowered with a statin, the skin takes on the structure and appearance of old skin.

The synthesis of pregnenolone from cholesterol requires three inputs: thyroid hormone, vitamin A, and cholesterol itself. Deficiency in any one reduces all downstream protective steroids. Additionally, bacterial endotoxin from the gut poisons the liver and stops it from making adequate cholesterol, representing another pathway to dangerously low levels. Peat acknowledged that no one is really sure of the single optimal cholesterol number, emphasizing that context — thyroid status, age, steroid hormone production — matters more than any absolute value.

A critical distinction the standard cholesterol panel misses: free cholesterol is protective, while cholesterol esters (especially with PUFA) are destructive. At birth, cholesterol ester proportion is very low. After age 20, cholesterol esters accumulate at 5x the rate of free cholesterol. After age 40, cholesterol esters become the main lipid in blood vessels. The esterification enzyme is activated by highly polyunsaturated fatty acids. Increased serum cholesterol esterification rate is a predictor of coronary heart disease and sudden death in the general population. The cholesterol esters found in atherosclerotic foam cells are specifically cholesteryl eicosapentaenoate, cholesteryl docosahexaenoate, cholesteryl arachidonate, and cholesteryl linoleate — all PUFA esters.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats total cholesterol above 200 as a risk factor requiring intervention (usually statins), with lower being better. Peat inverted this on multiple levels. First, he argued that high cholesterol is a symptom of hypothyroidism, not an independent disease — the correct treatment is thyroid support, not cholesterol reduction. Second, he cited the Hungarian study showing that artificially lowering cholesterol increased cancer mortality, total mortality, and death from accidents, homicide, and even heart disease and strokes. Third, he argued that cholesterol-lowering interventions of all kinds — whether drugs, PUFA, or dietary restriction — tend to promote cancer and degenerative disease by suppressing steroid hormone production. Fourth, the original atherosclerosis experiments used toxic oxidized cholesterol, not native cholesterol, and unoxidized cholesterol does not damage blood vessels — Lande and Sperry (1936) found no correlation between blood cholesterol content and atherosclerosis, regardless of age. Fifth, dietary cholesterol does not meaningfully raise blood cholesterol because the liver's production is feedback-regulated. Mainstream fear of dietary cholesterol was unfounded. Sixth, PUFA inactivates ABCA1 (the protein that removes excess cholesterol from arteries), promoting atherosclerosis — saturated fat does not do this.

## Sex Differences

In an ovary perfusion experiment, the amount of progesterone secreted varied directly with the amount of LDL cholesterol in the blood — LDL is the delivery vehicle for steroidogenesis. This means statin therapy or very low cholesterol directly reduces progesterone production in women. Peat's framework implies that women's higher estrogen (which impairs T4-to-T3 conversion) makes them more susceptible to the hypothyroid pattern of high cholesterol that mainstream medicine would treat with statins rather than thyroid support.

## Medication Effects

- **Statins:** Peat opposed statin therapy. By lowering cholesterol, statins deprive the body of steroid hormone precursors. Statins damage mitochondria directly — the amount of cholesterol in mitochondria corresponds to their ability to produce ATP. They cause rhabdomyolysis, birth defects, depression, and increased cancer. Exercise increases the risk of statin-induced muscle damage. The Hungarian study and others showed that cholesterol-lowering increased total mortality, cancer, and violent death.
- **Thyroid (T3, Armour, Cynoplus):** Rapidly lowers cholesterol by driving its conversion to steroid hormones. People with cholesterol of 400-500 can normalize in one week with hourly T3 doses.
- **PUFA/seed oils:** Lower cholesterol through thyroid suppression and increased cellular stress — the cholesterol-lowering effect is a sign of toxicity, not benefit. PUFA also activates the cholesterol esterification enzyme that produces the destructive cholesterol esters found in plaques.
- **Orange juice/sugar:** Can raise dangerously low cholesterol by providing substrate for hepatic synthesis. Switching from starchy vegetables to sweet fruits normalizes low cholesterol.
- **Progesterone:** Inhibits ACAT (the enzyme that forms toxic PUFA-cholesterol esters) in all tissues except the liver, where it promotes cholesterol export for steroid synthesis — a positive feedback loop for its own production.
