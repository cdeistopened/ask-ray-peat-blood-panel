# Calcium (Serum) — Including PTH Connection

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered serum calcium deeply misleading without PTH, vitamin D, and phosphate context; normal serum calcium can mask severe underlying calcium dysfunction
**Companion markers:** PTH, vitamin D (25-OH), phosphate/phosphorus, ionized calcium, bone density (DEXA), magnesium, progesterone, estrogen, sodium, T3
**Peat's range:** Serum calcium 9-10 mg/dL is healthy only if PTH is low; dietary calcium 1,500-2,000+ mg/day from dairy; vitamin D target 50 ng/mL; 2:1 Ca:P ratio safe, 1:1 probably safe
**Mainstream range:** Serum calcium 8.6-10.2 mg/dL; PTH 15-65 pg/mL

## Peat's Position

Calcium is one of the markers where Peat's interpretation diverges most dramatically from standard medicine. Serum calcium can appear normal or even elevated while the body is catastrophically calcium-deficient in bones and cells. Elevated serum calcium (11-13 mg/dL) driven by PTH actually reflects bone dissolution, not calcium abundance. The body may have too much calcium in the wrong places (arteries, kidneys, brain) and too little in the right places (bones, cells) simultaneously. A serum calcium reading without PTH context is nearly meaningless.

PTH is the central villain in Peat's calcium framework. When dietary calcium is inadequate or phosphate intake is excessive, PTH rises to maintain serum calcium by dissolving bone. But PTH does far more damage than bone loss: it blocks oxygen utilization in all tissues, shifting cells toward lactic acid production. The lactic acid then dissolves more bone mineral, releasing more calcium in a self-reinforcing destructive cycle. PTH belongs alongside cortisol, estrogen, prolactin, and serotonin as a stress-related hormone whose chronic elevation drives degenerative disease. PTH and aldosterone "collaborate to knock down energy production." PTH increases with age and in excess causes insomnia, seizures, dementia, psychosis, cancer, heart disease, respiratory distress, osteoporosis, sarcopenia, histamine release, inflammation, and soft tissue calcification. PTH ends the hair growth cycle — many people notice copious hair loss mainly in the morning, corresponding to the nocturnal PTH rise. A glass of milk at bedtime may protect hair as it protects bone.

CO2/bicarbonate regulates calcium metabolism. In hypothyroidism, CO2 is usually low, which disrupts calcium regulation. Calcium helps keep the metabolic rate high and steers tryptophan away from the serotonin pathway, making milk's tryptophan content less problematic than it would otherwise be. Calcium, sodium, and aspirin increase the ability to oxidize glucose, working alongside T3 to promote glucose utilization. Calcium and vitamin D reduce obesity and increase metabolic rate — the obesity epidemic that began ~40 years ago paralleled decreasing milk consumption.

The calcium-to-phosphate ratio is what determines PTH activity, not absolute calcium amounts. Modern diets are heavily skewed toward phosphate from meat, grains, beans, nuts, and processed foods. This high phosphate relative to calcium chronically elevates PTH. A 2:1 calcium-to-phosphorus ratio is safe; even a 1:1 ratio is probably safe, but the ideal has not been clearly defined. Peat recommended keeping this ratio high through dairy (milk, cheese) and fruit rather than meat and starches. Milk, cheese, and eggs are "better proteins" specifically because of their superior calcium-to-phosphate ratio.

Peat recommended "thousands of milligrams of dietary calcium per day" — specifically about 2 quarts of milk daily (approximately 2,400mg calcium), combined with vitamin D levels around 50 ng/mL. This combination effectively suppresses PTH and supports bone density. Powdered eggshells are the best calcium supplement, oyster shell calcium second. He rejected mainstream warnings about calcium supplementation causing heart disease: dietary calcium suppresses PTH, and PTH is what actually causes arterial calcification. More calcium prevents the problem mainstream medicine fears calcium causes.

High ferritin with adequate vitamin D may indicate insufficient calcium relative to phosphate, causing elevated PTH that produces generalized inflammation. PTH testing is recommended in this context. Deficiency of calcium or vitamin D increases PTH, which raises serum calcium and blood pressure.

The nocturnal dimension is clinically important. During sleep, blood calcium drops as the body goes hours without food, triggering PTH release that disturbs sleep, elevates blood pressure, and accelerates bone loss. Bone loss occurs almost entirely during the night due to nocturnal rise in PTH and cortisol. Drinking milk before bed prevents this nocturnal PTH surge. Calcium and vitamin D, sufficient to keep PTH low, combined with salty snacks and ice cream at bedtime, support restorative sleep and retard nocturnal brain aging.

## Mainstream Inversion

This is one of Peat's most dramatic inversions. Mainstream medicine warns against excessive calcium supplementation, citing concerns about cardiovascular calcification and kidney stones. Peat argued the exact opposite: inadequate calcium is what elevates PTH, and elevated PTH is what causes both arterial calcification AND kidney stones AND osteoporosis. These are unified expressions of PTH excess, not separate diseases caused by too much calcium. The solution is MORE dietary calcium to suppress PTH, not less.

Additionally, mainstream medicine attributes post-menopausal osteoporosis to declining estrogen. Peat argued that estrogen actually promotes calcium loss from bone and increases PTH secretion. Prolactin contributes to osteoporosis by mobilizing calcium from bones; since estrogen increases prolactin, using estrogen to "prevent" osteoporosis may actually worsen it through the prolactin pathway. The true bone-protective hormone is progesterone, which reduces PTH secretion.

## Sex Differences

The estrogen-calcium connection is a major sex difference. Mainstream medicine claims estrogen protects bone, but Peat argued estrogen promotes calcium loss and increases PTH. Post-menopausal osteoporosis is not caused by declining estrogen but by declining progesterone, which truly protects bone. Prolactin contributes to osteoporosis by mobilizing calcium from bones; estrogen increases prolactin. Women with estrogen dominance relative to progesterone would have higher PTH and worse calcium distribution. Calcium deficiency causes cramps, seizures, and bronchial spasms — symptoms more commonly reported in women, potentially reflecting the estrogen-PTH connection.

Rational therapy for osteoporosis includes thyroid, vitamin A, bright light, and either pregnenolone or a mixture of progesterone and DHEA, with a diet rich in protein and all salts: calcium, magnesium, sodium, and potassium. A bias toward magnesium rather than calcium would protect against many conditions of aging.
