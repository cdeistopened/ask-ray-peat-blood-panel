# Calcium (Serum) — Including PTH Connection

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered serum calcium deeply misleading without PTH, vitamin D, and phosphate context; normal serum calcium can mask severe underlying calcium dysfunction
**Companion markers:** PTH, vitamin D (25-OH), phosphate/phosphorus, ionized calcium, bone density (DEXA), magnesium, progesterone, estrogen
**Peat's range:** Serum calcium 9-10 mg/dL is healthy only if PTH is low; dietary calcium 1,500-2,000+ mg/day from dairy; vitamin D target 50 ng/mL
**Mainstream range:** Serum calcium 8.6-10.2 mg/dL; PTH 15-65 pg/mL

## Peat's Position

Calcium is one of the markers where Peat's interpretation diverges most dramatically from standard medicine. Serum calcium can appear normal or even elevated while the body is catastrophically calcium-deficient in bones and cells. Elevated serum calcium (11-13 mg/dL) driven by PTH actually reflects bone dissolution, not calcium abundance. The body may have too much calcium in the wrong places (arteries, kidneys, brain) and too little in the right places (bones, cells) simultaneously. A serum calcium reading without PTH context is nearly meaningless.

PTH is the central villain in Peat's calcium framework. When dietary calcium is inadequate or phosphate intake is excessive, PTH rises to maintain serum calcium by dissolving bone. But PTH does far more damage than bone loss: it blocks oxygen utilization in all tissues, shifting cells toward lactic acid production. The lactic acid then dissolves more bone mineral, releasing more calcium in a self-reinforcing destructive cycle. PTH belongs alongside cortisol, estrogen, prolactin, and serotonin as a stress-related hormone whose chronic elevation drives degenerative disease. PTH and aldosterone "collaborate to knock down energy production."

The calcium-to-phosphate ratio is what determines PTH activity, not absolute calcium amounts. Modern diets are heavily skewed toward phosphate from meat, grains, beans, nuts, and processed foods. This high phosphate relative to calcium chronically elevates PTH. Peat recommended keeping this ratio high through dairy (milk, cheese) and fruit rather than meat and starches. Milk, cheese, and eggs are "better proteins" specifically because of their superior calcium-to-phosphate ratio.

Peat recommended "thousands of milligrams of dietary calcium per day" — specifically about 2 quarts of milk daily (approximately 2,400mg calcium), combined with vitamin D levels around 50 ng/mL. This combination effectively suppresses PTH and supports bone density. Powdered eggshells are the best calcium supplement, oyster shell calcium second. He rejected mainstream warnings about calcium supplementation causing heart disease: dietary calcium suppresses PTH, and PTH is what actually causes arterial calcification. More calcium prevents the problem mainstream medicine fears calcium causes.

CO2 keeps calcium in solution in the blood and stable in bones. When respiration is impaired and CO2 drops, calcium tends to precipitate in tissues. This connects calcium metabolism to the broader metabolic framework: adequate oxidative metabolism (producing CO2) keeps calcium where it belongs, while glycolytic metabolism (producing lactic acid) dissolves bone and promotes ectopic calcification.

The nocturnal dimension is clinically important. During sleep, blood calcium drops as the body goes hours without food, triggering PTH release that disturbs sleep, elevates blood pressure, and accelerates bone loss. Drinking milk before bed prevents this nocturnal PTH surge.

Keeping the calcium-to-phosphate ratio very high in the diet has a "relaxing, anti-stress effect" — one of the most powerful dietary anti-stressors in Peat's view.

## Mainstream Inversion

This is one of Peat's most dramatic inversions. Mainstream medicine warns against excessive calcium supplementation, citing concerns about cardiovascular calcification and kidney stones. Peat argued the exact opposite: inadequate calcium is what elevates PTH, and elevated PTH is what causes both arterial calcification AND kidney stones AND osteoporosis. These are unified expressions of PTH excess, not separate diseases caused by too much calcium. The solution is MORE dietary calcium to suppress PTH, not less.

Additionally, mainstream medicine attributes post-menopausal osteoporosis to declining estrogen. Peat argued that estrogen actually promotes calcium loss from bone and increases PTH secretion. The true bone-protective hormone is progesterone, which reduces PTH secretion. The standard HRT rationale for bone health inverts the actual hormonal mechanism.

## Sex Differences

The estrogen-calcium connection is a major sex difference. Mainstream medicine claims estrogen protects bone, but Peat argued estrogen promotes calcium loss and increases PTH. Post-menopausal osteoporosis is not caused by declining estrogen but by declining progesterone, which truly protects bone. Women with estrogen dominance relative to progesterone would have higher PTH and worse calcium distribution. Calcium deficiency causes cramps, seizures, and bronchial spasms — symptoms more commonly reported in women, potentially reflecting the estrogen-PTH connection.

## Medication Effects

Calcium supplementation (eggshell powder preferred, oyster shell second) directly suppresses PTH. Vitamin D supplementation (2,000-6,000 IU winter) enhances calcium absorption and independently suppresses PTH. Vitamin K directs calcium to bones and away from arteries. Progesterone (natural, not synthetic progestins) reduces PTH secretion. Thyroid supplementation improves oxidative metabolism, increasing CO2 production, which helps keep calcium stable in bones. Statins and diuretics (common in the mainstream approach to cardiovascular disease) do nothing to address the underlying PTH-calcium disorder.
