# Glucose (Fasting Blood Sugar)

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat considered glucose useful but insisted that hypoglycemia is more dangerous than hyperglycemia, inverting the standard clinical priority
**Companion markers:** Free fatty acids, insulin, lactate, cortisol, adrenaline, TSH, T3, potassium, dietary carbohydrate type
**Peat's range:** ~90 mg/dL (stable, with stable blood pressure around 120/80); below 80 mg/dL associated with worse outcomes in brain injury
**Mainstream range:** 70-100 mg/dL (fasting normal); >126 mg/dL (diabetes)

## Peat's Position

Peat viewed blood glucose through the lens of oxidative metabolism rather than through the mainstream diabetes framework. Stable blood sugar around 90 mg/dL, accompanied by stable blood pressure around 120/80 and a pulse of 80-85, corresponds to good metabolic function and adequate thyroid. The key question is not whether glucose is within a reference range but whether cells are able to oxidize it — and this depends on factors that glucose measurement alone cannot reveal.

Peat considered hypoglycemia far more dangerous than hyperglycemia. Low blood sugar triggers cascading stress responses — cortisol rises (promoting catabolism), adrenaline surges (causing vasoconstriction and anxiety), and free fatty acids are mobilized from fat stores (activating the Randle cycle). This stress cascade damages tissues and suppresses metabolism, creating a degenerative spiral. Blood sugar normally falls during the hours of darkness, compensated by rising free fatty acids — the same pattern as stress. This nocturnal pattern explains night sweats, insomnia, and early-morning cortisol spikes. Eating carbohydrate at bedtime can prevent it. In patients hospitalized with subarachnoid hemorrhage, blood glucose below 80 mg/dL was associated with worse outcomes including vasospasm and cerebral infarction. The heart is more susceptible to infarction during fasting than after a meal.

The rising blood sugar in diabetes, Peat argued, reflects cells' inability to oxidize glucose (due to PUFA and the Randle cycle), not glucose overproduction. The body floods the system with glucose hoping some will get through to starving cells. The proof is that diabetics have very high lactic acid — showing glucose IS entering cells, but it cannot be used oxidatively because fatty acids are blocking the respiratory system. Lactate returns to the liver and is reconverted to glucose in a futile Cori cycle. The cause that interferes with glucose use should be concentrated on, not the blood sugar number itself.

Glucose is also essential for T4-to-T3 conversion in the liver — during fasting, there is a sharp decrease in metabolic rate because this conversion stops. Even 200-300 calories of carbohydrates can be added during a fast without causing fat storage, by restoring this conversion. Intravenous glucose restores the balance between arterial and venous muscle tone and has been used many times to bring patients out of shock.

Sugar actually protects against diabetes in Peat's framework. Dietary sugar suppresses lipolysis, keeping fatty acids stored rather than circulating, which prevents the Randle cycle activation that drives insulin resistance. Historical sugar therapy — before insulin was available — fed diabetics about three-quarters of a pound of sugar daily, and patients recovered from wasting disease in weeks. A two-day fast causes glucose tolerance to deteriorate in both diabetics and normal people, while a 75% carbohydrate diet improved insulin sensitivity compared to 44% carbohydrate. Fructose is particularly important because it enters cells and is metabolized without requiring insulin, and it limits insulin secretion while intensifying metabolic activity. Potassium plays a larger role in glucose disposal than insulin, and fruit juice (containing both sugar and potassium) requires far less insulin than bread with similar calories. Starch-fed rats gained more weight than sugar-eating rats, contradicting the assumption that sugar is uniquely fattening.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream diabetes management focuses on reducing blood glucose through insulin, metformin, and carbohydrate restriction. Peat inverted nearly every aspect of this approach. First, he argued that hyperglycemia is a symptom of impaired glucose oxidation (caused by PUFA and the Randle cycle), not excess glucose — the correct treatment addresses the oxidation block, not the glucose level. Second, sugar restriction worsens the problem by promoting lipolysis and free fatty acid release, which further blocks glucose oxidation. Third, insulin resistance is a protective adaptation, not pure pathology — cells that cannot oxidize glucose efficiently are protecting themselves from lactic acid overproduction. Fourth, diagnostic thresholds for Type 2 diabetes and gestational diabetes have been progressively lowered to expand the market for pharmaceutical intervention, capturing people with transient or stress-related glucose elevations. Fifth, any diabetes diagnosis is incomplete without assessing TSH, cortisol, and vitamin D, and T3 supplementation is always appropriate with diabetes.

## Sex Differences

Peat noted that gestational diabetes represents a medicalized normal variation. Pregnancy naturally shifts toward insulin resistance to direct glucose preferentially to the fetus. Progressively lowered diagnostic thresholds capture more pregnant women, creating a market for insulin intervention during a physiological adaptation.

## Medication Effects

- **Insulin (exogenous):** Peat considered insulin forcing in standard diabetes management potentially harmful — forcing glucose into cells that cannot oxidize it produces more lactic acid and metabolic stress.
- **T3 (thyroid):** Always appropriate with diabetes because it restores oxidative metabolism, addressing the underlying cause of glucose dysfunction. In diabetes, T4 builds up because cells cannot convert it to T3 without glucose — use combination T3+T4, not T4-only.
- **Niacinamide:** Lowers free fatty acids by inhibiting lipase, preventing Randle cycle activation and restoring glucose oxidation.
- **Aspirin:** Blocks inflammatory prostaglandins from PUFA, reduces metabolic interference with glucose use, increases glucose oxidation. 325 mg with meals twice daily with vitamin K supplementation.
- **Sugar/fruit juice:** Peat considered sugar therapeutic in diabetes — it suppresses lipolysis and prevents Randle cycle activation. Historical physicians used three-quarters of a pound daily.
- **Coffee (with food):** Stabilizes blood sugar and reduces inflammation through magnesium, niacin, and caffeine's antioxidant effects.
- **Vitamin B1:** 50-100 mg with meals improves glucose oxidation; watch for hypoglycemia in insulin-treated diabetics. Pantothenic acid (B5) 100-200 mg helps limit hypoglycemia.
