# Albumin

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat considered albumin one of the most informative blood markers, reflecting liver function, inflammatory status, thyroid activity, and biological aging
**Companion markers:** Total protein, ALT/AST, CRP, ferritin, fibrinogen, thyroid markers (TSH, T3, T4), estrogen, free fatty acids
**Peat's range:** 4.5-5.5 g/dL optimal; consistently above 5.0 reflects robust metabolic health; trend over time more informative than single values
**Mainstream range:** 3.5-5.5 g/dL (reference range); <3.5 = hypoalbuminemia

## Peat's Position

Albumin occupies a central position in Peat's diagnostic framework as perhaps the single best blood marker of overall metabolic vitality. Truly optimal albumin levels sit in the upper portion of the reference range: 4.5-5.5 g/dL. Values at the low end of normal (3.5-4.0) still indicate suboptimal metabolic function even though the lab will not flag them as abnormal. Albumin consistently above 5.0 reflects robust liver function, low inflammation, and good thyroid activity. The standard reference range (3.5-5.5) includes metabolically compromised individuals, making the low end of "normal" functionally abnormal.

Albumin is more sensitive than liver enzymes for detecting chronic metabolic stress. ALT and AST only elevate when liver cells are actually damaged. Albumin can decline with chronic liver stress before overt damage develops, providing an early warning signal. Low albumin with normal liver enzymes suggests chronic metabolic stress from hypothyroidism, excess estrogen, or inflammatory conditions — a pattern invisible to standard liver panels.

The albumin-thyroid relationship is bidirectional. Low thyroid impairs albumin synthesis because the liver requires thyroid hormone for the energy to sustain continuous albumin production (roughly 15g per day). Conversely, low albumin impairs thyroid hormone dynamics by reducing binding and buffering capacity. The two markers move together because both reflect fundamental metabolic capacity. Hypothyroidism typically presents with low-normal albumin.

Albumin is classified as a negative acute phase protein: its synthesis decreases during inflammation while positive acute phase proteins (CRP, ferritin, fibrinogen) increase. Chronic low-grade inflammation persistently suppresses albumin even when specific inflammatory markers appear normal. This means albumin can detect inflammation that CRP misses, particularly the chronic, low-grade variety.

The ratio between free fatty acids and albumin determines PUFA toxicity. Albumin binds and safely transports free fatty acids. When albumin is low or free fatty acids are high, unbound PUFA circulates freely, causing mitochondrial damage, thyroid suppression, and inflammation. Low albumin therefore exacerbates PUFA toxicity in a vicious cycle: inflammation lowers albumin, low albumin increases unbound PUFA, unbound PUFA increases inflammation.

Albumin also serves as a longevity biomarker. It declines with age, reflecting accumulated metabolic compromise: reduced liver function, chronic inflammation, declining thyroid, and PUFA accumulation. But this decline is not inevitable. Centenarians who maintain albumin comparable to middle-aged individuals demonstrate that age-related decline reflects lifestyle and metabolic factors, not aging itself. Gradually declining albumin over years, even within normal ranges, signals progressive metabolic deterioration.

## Mainstream Inversion

Standard medicine considers albumin "normal" down to 3.5 g/dL and only flags values below that as clinically significant. Peat argued that 3.5-4.0 already represents metabolic compromise. The mainstream approach also treats albumin primarily as a nutritional marker (low albumin = poor nutrition), while Peat saw it as a comprehensive metabolic indicator reflecting liver function, thyroid status, inflammatory load, and biological aging simultaneously. The mainstream focus on ALT/AST for liver assessment misses the chronic dysfunction that albumin detects earlier. Perhaps most importantly, the mainstream reference range includes sick individuals, so "normal" does not mean "optimal."

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → reduced liver energy → impaired albumin synthesis → low-normal albumin
- Low albumin → impaired thyroid hormone binding/buffering → worse thyroid dynamics (bidirectional)
- Chronic inflammation → liver redirects synthesis from albumin to acute phase proteins → albumin falls
- Low albumin → more unbound PUFA in circulation → increased oxidative damage → more inflammation (vicious cycle)
- Estrogen excess → increased inflammation + liver stress → suppressed albumin synthesis
- Aging → cumulative metabolic compromise → gradual albumin decline (but not inevitable)
- Adequate protein (80-100g/day from milk, eggs, cheese) + thyroid optimization → supports albumin production

## Sex Differences

No explicit sex-specific differences in albumin interpretation were noted in the extracted claims. However, given Peat's emphasis on estrogen's role in suppressing albumin (through liver stress and inflammation), women with estrogen dominance relative to progesterone would be expected to have lower albumin than women with adequate progesterone levels.

## Medication Effects

Thyroid supplementation (particularly T3) supports albumin synthesis by providing the energy the liver needs for continuous protein production. Anti-inflammatory interventions (aspirin, reducing PUFA intake) allow the liver to shift from acute phase protein production back to albumin synthesis. Adequate dietary protein from easily digested sources (milk, eggs, cheese) provides the amino acid substrate. Estrogen reduction (through progesterone support, carrot salad for intestinal estrogen binding, and liver detoxification support) removes a major suppressor of albumin production.
