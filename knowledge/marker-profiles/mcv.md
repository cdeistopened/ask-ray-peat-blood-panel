# MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat considered MCV useful for identifying B12 deficiency and macrocytic anemia but emphasized functional markers over the lab value alone
**Companion markers:** B12, methylmalonic acid, homocysteine, reticulocyte count, thyroid markers
**Peat's range:** Not specified beyond noting that elevated MCV (>100 fL) suggests B12 deficiency
**Mainstream range:** 80-100 fL; >100 fL suggests macrocytic anemia

## Peat's Position

Peat's discussion of MCV is primarily in the context of B12 deficiency. B12 is required for DNA synthesis in red blood cell precursors. Without adequate B12, these precursors cannot divide normally — they produce abnormally large, immature cells called megaloblasts instead of normal red blood cells. The result is macrocytic anemia characterized by elevated MCV: large, pale cells that cannot efficiently carry oxygen. An elevated MCV is therefore a functional flag for B12 status.

However, Peat cautioned against over-reliance on serum B12 levels, noting that they do not always correlate with functional status. He favored methylmalonic acid and homocysteine as more reliable functional markers of B12 adequacy. A person with a modestly low B12 blood level who eats animal foods and has no symptoms may not be truly deficient, while someone with neurological symptoms, unexplained anemia, or elevated homocysteine warrants attention regardless of their serum B12 number. MCV fits into this as a red flag: when it is elevated, it points toward a real utilization problem that warrants investigation.

## Mainstream Inversion

The mainstream approach and Peat largely agree that elevated MCV points to B12 (or folate) deficiency. The divergence is in what to do next: mainstream medicine typically supplements B12 and rechecks the lab. Peat would look at the broader metabolic context — thyroid function, iron utilization, dietary patterns — and emphasize that serum B12 levels alone are an unreliable guide to functional status. He also noted that the reference ranges for B12 have shifted over time without clear evidence, suggesting that some diagnoses of B12 deficiency are artifacts of changing lab norms rather than genuine clinical problems.

## Causal Connections

- B12 deficiency → impaired DNA synthesis in RBC precursors → megaloblasts → elevated MCV
- B12 deficiency → impaired homocysteine recycling → elevated homocysteine → cardiovascular/neurological risk
- B12 deficiency → impaired carotene-to-retinol conversion → functional vitamin A deficiency
- Folate deficiency → similar megaloblastic pathway → elevated MCV (folate and B12 share this endpoint)
