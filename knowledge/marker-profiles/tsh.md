# TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat considers TSH useful but dangerous without companion markers, since stress hormones can suppress TSH and mimic hyperthyroidism in hypothyroid people
**Companion markers:** Temperature, pulse, Free T3, cholesterol, cortisol, estrogen, Reverse T3
**Peat's range:** As close to zero as possible; below 0.4 in the healthiest populations
**Mainstream range:** 0.4-4.0 mIU/L (standard); 0.3-3.0 (AACE revised)

## Peat's Position

Peat's view of TSH is radically different from mainstream endocrinology. Rather than treating TSH as a passive indicator of thyroid status, he considered it an active pathological agent. TSH itself causes many of the symptoms blamed on hypothyroidism because it acts on bone marrow, the liver, and many other tissues, directly triggering inflammatory cytokines including TNF, IL-1, and IL-6. In Peat's framework, TSH is not merely a signal that thyroid is low — it is part of the disease.

For this reason, the goal of thyroid replacement in Peat's system is not merely to achieve normal tissue function but to suppress TSH to minimal levels, ideally near zero. He cited Broda Barnes's protocol of using 3-4 grains of Armour thyroid — enough to lower TSH to zero — for cancer prevention, and noted that in Barnes's 30 years of practice, no patient on this protocol died of heart disease. The healthiest population regarding thyroid disease had TSH levels below 0.4.

However, Peat recognized a critical limitation of TSH as a diagnostic tool: a low TSH does not always mean adequate thyroid. A hypothyroid person raises estrogen, cortisol, and adrenaline as compensatory stress hormones, and these suppress TSH, making it look like the person has too much thyroid when they actually have too little. This makes TSH alone unreliable — it must always be interpreted alongside temperature, pulse, cholesterol, and clinical symptoms. The gold-standard measure of thyroid adequacy, in Peat's view, was the Basal Metabolic Rate (oxygen consumption), which showed that 30% of Americans were hypothyroid in 1940 and approximately 60% are today — far more than the roughly 5% detected by TSH-based testing.

Peat also emphasized that TSH fluctuates with stress, illness, and time of day. A single TSH measurement provides a snapshot that may reflect transient stress rather than true thyroid status. The most reliable interpretation comes from tracking TSH alongside functional markers over time.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine interprets TSH as a reliable thermostat: high TSH means hypothyroid, low TSH means hyperthyroid. Peat inverted this in two ways. First, he argued that high TSH is not just a signal but an active contributor to disease — suppressing it is a therapeutic goal, not just a diagnostic target. Second, he argued that low TSH does not rule out hypothyroidism, because stress hormones (cortisol, estrogen, adrenaline) can suppress TSH independently of thyroid status. A person with low TSH and hypothyroid symptoms is likely stressed and hypothyroid, not hyperthyroid. Mainstream medicine's reliance on TSH as the primary diagnostic leads to massive underdiagnosis (60% hypothyroid by oxygen consumption vs. 5% by TSH) and dangerous misdiagnosis (calling stressed hypothyroid patients "hyperthyroid").

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → ↑TSH → ↑inflammatory cytokines (TNF, IL-1, IL-6) → tissue damage
- ↑TSH → overstimulation of thyroid gland → glandular protein leakage → ↑TPO/thyroglobulin antibodies
- ↑Estrogen/cortisol/adrenaline → ↓TSH (stress suppression) → false appearance of hyperthyroidism
- ↑TSH → ↑cholesterol (inverse mirror relationship)
- Thyroid supplementation → ↓TSH → ↓inflammation → ↓antibodies (within months)
- Low vitamin D → impaired thyroid handling → ↑Reverse T3 → ↑TSH
- TSH suppression (near zero) → reduced cancer risk, reduced heart disease (Barnes protocol)

## Sex Differences

Women are more vulnerable to TSH misinterpretation because they have higher baseline estrogen, which both suppresses TSH (masking hypothyroidism) and blocks hepatic T4-to-T3 conversion (making T4-only therapy fail). Peat noted that women are the main population with Hashimoto's, making T4-only therapy (which suppresses TSH but fails to provide T3) particularly dangerous for them. Men, with lower estrogen, have better T4-to-T3 conversion and are more likely to respond to T4-only therapy.

## Medication Effects

- **Synthroid (T4-only):** Suppresses TSH but can leave patients hypothyroid at the tissue level if T4-to-T3 conversion is impaired by estrogen. TSH normalization does not equal clinical improvement.
- **Cynoplus (T4+T3):** Peat's preferred synthetic — suppresses TSH while also providing active T3 directly.
- **Cynomel/Cytomel (T3-only):** Directly addresses the conversion problem; if enough T3 is provided, TSH suppresses and inflammatory damage from TSH ceases.
- **Armour Thyroid (desiccated):** Barnes used 2-4 grains to suppress TSH to zero. Modern reformulations may have bioavailability issues.
- **Estrogen/birth control:** Suppresses TSH independently of thyroid status, creating false appearance of adequate thyroid function while worsening tissue-level hypothyroidism.
- **Cortisol (stress):** Suppresses TSH, masking hypothyroidism in stressed individuals.
