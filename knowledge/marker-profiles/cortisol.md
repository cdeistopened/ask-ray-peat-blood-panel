# Cortisol

**Reliability:** Tier 3 -- Peat considered all cortisol tests unreliable without companion tests. Saliva reflects only unbound fraction, serum swings wildly with meals and venipuncture stress, and urinary cortisol reflects liver metabolism more than production.
**Companion markers:** Temperature, pulse, pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, testosterone, TSH, T3
**Peat's range:** No specific number. Ratio of cortisol to protective steroids matters more than absolute value. For a 35-year-old male, cortisol should be no higher than the middle of the range. Trending downward with interventions is the target.
**Mainstream range:** Morning serum: 6-23 mcg/dL; Evening serum: 2-14 mcg/dL; Morning saliva: 0.1-0.75 mcg/dL; 24-hour urinary free cortisol: 3.5-45 mcg/24h

## Peat's Position

Peat regarded cortisol as the body's emergency glucose-manufacturing hormone, not a marker to be managed in isolation. Its primary role is gluconeogenesis: converting amino acids from tissue proteins into glucose when oxidative metabolism has failed. The body breaks down thymus first, then muscle, skin, and bone to maintain blood sugar. Heart, lungs, and brain are spared longest because they concentrate protective androgens (testosterone, DHEA) that block cortisol's catabolic action -- but when those androgens are low, even vital organs sustain damage.

The central insight is that cortisol's destructiveness depends entirely on the ratio of cortisol to protective steroids. Even "normal" cortisol on a lab test is destructive if pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, and testosterone are low. Cortisol and progesterone are both synthesized from pregnenolone, and when metabolism falters, production shifts preferentially toward cortisol at the expense of progesterone. This is not "pregnenolone steal" but inadequate total production driven by insufficient thyroid, cholesterol, and vitamin A -- the three inputs required for pregnenolone synthesis.

Rather than trusting lab numbers, Peat assessed cortisol through metabolic signatures: waking body temperature (~97.8F rising to 98.6F after breakfast), resting pulse (~80-85 bpm), sleep quality (2-4 AM waking indicates nocturnal hypoglycemia triggering cortisol), muscle mass relative to abdominal fat, and skin thickness. He explicitly rejected the concept of "adrenal fatigue," noting that the adrenal cortex regenerates very well -- the problem is upstream inputs (thyroid, cholesterol, nutrients), not tired glands. Hypothyroid people often have very high adrenaline, cortisol, and other stress hormones during the night, causing temperature to be higher before breakfast than after — this inverted temperature pattern is diagnostic.

Cortisol also participates in self-amplifying cycles. It breaks down muscle, liberating tryptophan, which feeds serotonin synthesis. Serotonin is one of the most powerful activators of the cortisol stress system, acting on the hypothalamus to release CRH. This cortisol-tryptophan-serotonin loop creates escalating inflammation. Meanwhile, cortisol paradoxically deprives the brain of glucose by impairing neural glucose transport, even while breaking down peripheral proteins to produce glucose. The chronic increase of cortisol also increases estrogen production through fat cell aromatase activation, compounding the damage.

Two critical timing details from Peat's written work: darkness itself triggers cortisol to rise, regardless of whether the person is asleep or awake — artificial light and its absence directly determine the time at which cortisol begins to rise. And falling glucose is the specific signal for cortisol to rise — dreams are closely associated with the chemical-physiological mobilization in response to low blood glucose, with rising adrenaline probably mediating the cortisol response. Sleep minimizes the increase of cortisol through GABA and the sleep peptide's quieting anti-stress effects.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats cortisol as a binary: too high (Cushing's) or too low (Addison's), with everything between considered normal. Peat argued this misses the most clinically important situation -- "normal" cortisol with low protective steroids. A man with cortisol at 15 mcg/dL and testosterone at 250 ng/dL is in a catabolic state that mainstream medicine would not flag. Mainstream also endorses prolonged fasting and low-carb diets as therapeutic, which Peat identified as cortisol-maximizing: on a complete water fast, 80%+ of weight loss comes from muscle and skin destruction, and providing only protein and fat without carbohydrate forces cortisol-driven gluconeogenesis. Mainstream further treats "adrenal fatigue" as either a real condition requiring adrenal support or a myth -- Peat rejected both framings, locating the problem upstream in thyroid and nutrient status.

## Causal Connections

- Low thyroid -> impaired cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion -> low protective steroids -> high cortisol relative to protectors
- Low carbohydrate intake -> gluconeogenesis demand -> cortisol rises to convert protein to glucose
- Protein without carbohydrate -> cortisol surge -> amino acids wasted for energy instead of anabolism
- Darkness -> cortisol rises independent of sleep state -> nocturnal catabolic cascade
- Falling glucose -> adrenaline -> cortisol -> dreams as manifestation of stress mobilization
- Cortisol -> muscle breakdown -> liberated tryptophan -> serotonin synthesis -> more cortisol (self-amplifying)
- Cortisol -> tissue atrophy -> fat cell aromatase activation -> increased estrogen production
- Serotonin -> hypothalamic CRH -> pituitary ACTH -> adrenal cortisol
- Estrogen -> cortisol (acts at three levels: adrenals, pituitary, hypothalamus)
- Low calcium -> high PTH; low sodium -> high aldosterone -> both poison mitochondria -> cortisol dependence
- Cortisol + adrenaline + estrogen -> TSH suppression -> appears hyperthyroid when actually hypothyroid
- Fasting -> liver detox shutdown -> estrogen recirculation -> more cortisol
- Brief resistance exercise (20-30 seconds) -> muscle shifts from cortisol to testosterone production
- Sustained exertion -> T3 falls + serotonin/TSH/prolactin/CRH/cortisol all rise
- Aspirin -> lowers ACTH -> lowers aldosterone -> reduces cortisol cascade
- Raw carrot daily -> binds estrogen in intestine -> lower estrogen and cortisol within ~3 days
- Calcium in diet -> suppresses prolactin, cortisol, and serotonin
- Pregnenolone/progesterone supplementation -> body normalizes, cortisol falls

## Sex Differences

Women are more vulnerable to cortisol dominance because estrogen (higher in women) drives cortisol production through three simultaneous pathways and blocks T4-to-T3 conversion, creating functional hypothyroidism. Women have 5-10 times as much thyroid disease as men for this reason. Around age 40, most people reach their peak estrogen-to-progesterone ratio, making this the most cortisol-vulnerable age for both sexes, but especially women. In men, brief moderate resistance exercise can shift muscle from cortisol to testosterone production, but excessive exercise (even one hour of treadmill walking at mild intensity) can make both sexes functionally hypothyroid, triggering the cortisol cascade.

## Medication Effects

- **Pregnenolone/progesterone:** Safely lower cortisol by providing upstream substrate; body self-regulates downstream production. Cannot cause excess. Progesterone lowers cortisol synthesis by acting on brain, pituitary, and adrenal glands simultaneously.
- **Thyroid (T3/T4):** Corrects the upstream cause; adequate thyroid drives cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, reducing the body's reliance on cortisol.
- **Aspirin:** Blocks ACTH-driven aldosterone production, reducing the cortisol-aldosterone degenerative cycle. Also anti-inflammatory.
- **Calcium supplementation:** Suppresses prolactin, cortisol, and serotonin simultaneously.
- **Raw carrot fiber:** Binds estrogen in intestine, preventing reabsorption; lowers both estrogen and cortisol within about three days.
- **Statin drugs:** By lowering cholesterol, statins deplete the raw material for all protective steroids, potentially worsening the cortisol-to-protector ratio.
- **SSRIs:** Raise serotonin, which is one of the most powerful cortisol activators. May worsen the cortisol cascade.
- **Birth control pills:** Chronic estrogen elevation drives cortisol through all three HPA pathways.
- **Fasting/low-carb diets:** Maximally activate cortisol-driven gluconeogenesis; Peat recommended >100g carbohydrate daily (preferably sucrose) to prevent this.
