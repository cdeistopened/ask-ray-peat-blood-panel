## Peat's Position

Peat's discussion of white blood cells centered not on counting them but on understanding what makes them work. The foundation is Metchnikoff's phagocyte theory: cellular immunity via phagocytes — white blood cells that physically engulf and destroy foreign material — is the older, more fundamental immune mechanism. This phagocytic system does not require prior exposure to a pathogen. It recognizes damaged and foreign material on energetic and structural grounds, making it a metabolic function rather than a learned one.

The killing capacity of immune cells depends on oxidative bursts — the same oxidative metabolism that defines cellular health generally. WBCs kill pathogens through reactive oxygen species, which requires functional mitochondrial metabolism. Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) in WBC membranes impair this capacity, working "by exactly the same mechanism that X-rays do: creating an immune deficiency." Hypothyroidism reduces WBC metabolic competence by the same logic: cells that cannot produce energy efficiently cannot mount effective immune responses. This means that optimizing thyroid function and reducing PUFA intake are foundational immune interventions.

Peat also identified a hormonal axis affecting white blood cell function. Nitric oxide turns off steroid synthesis starting with pregnenolone in white blood cells. Serotonin, histamine, and nitric oxide all suppress the protective steroid cascade at its origin. The thymus gland, which trains T-lymphocytes, atrophies with aging because estrogen elevates cortisol, and cortisol is directly toxic to thymic tissue. This explains why immune function declines with aging in parallel with rising estrogen and cortisol — and why autoimmune diseases increase as the thymus shrinks.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine interprets WBC counts primarily as infection/inflammation markers (high count = infection, low count = immunosuppression). Peat reframed immune function as fundamentally metabolic: the number of white blood cells matters less than their oxidative capacity, which depends on thyroid function and PUFA content. A person with a normal WBC count but high tissue PUFA and low thyroid may have profoundly impaired immune function that no standard lab test would detect. Additionally, mainstream medicine does not connect PUFA intake to immune suppression or recognize that PUFA impairs WBC killing capacity through the same mechanism as radiation.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → reduced WBC oxidative metabolism → impaired phagocytic killing
- PUFA in WBC membranes → suppressed oxidative burst → immune deficiency
- Estrogen → elevated cortisol → thymic atrophy → impaired T-cell training → autoimmunity
- Nitric oxide / serotonin / histamine → blocked pregnenolone synthesis in WBCs → reduced local protective steroids
- Adequate thyroid + low PUFA → robust WBC oxidative capacity → effective innate immunity

## Sex Differences

Estrogen's role in thymic atrophy via cortisol elevation implies that women with higher estrogen levels (relative to progesterone) may experience earlier and more severe thymic involution. This connects to the higher incidence of autoimmune disease in women compared to men.

## Medication Effects

No specific medication effects on WBC counts were discussed in the extracted claims. However, Peat's framework implies that thyroid supplementation should improve WBC function (by supporting oxidative metabolism), and that reducing PUFA intake restores WBC killing capacity over time as membrane composition changes.
