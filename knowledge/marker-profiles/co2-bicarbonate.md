# CO2 / Bicarbonate

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat considered CO2 production the single most important indicator of metabolic health, though he typically assessed it through functional signs (temperature, pulse, breathing rate) rather than lab values
**Companion markers:** Lactate, temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, end-tidal CO2, blood pH, calcium, magnesium, potassium
**Peat's range:** Bicarbonate higher in range = better oxidative metabolism; end-tidal CO2 ~40 mmHg; respiratory rate 8-12 breaths/min at rest; RQ approaching 1.0 = optimal
**Mainstream range:** CO2 (bicarbonate on BMP) 22-28 mEq/L; end-tidal CO2 35-45 mmHg; respiratory rate 12-20 breaths/min

## Peat's Position

CO2 is arguably the most important metabolic marker in Peat's entire framework — the molecule where his theory of health comes into sharpest focus. CO2 production is both a marker and a product of efficient oxidative metabolism. High CO2 indicates active mitochondrial respiration. Low CO2 indicates glycolytic metabolism and cellular stress. CO2 is simultaneously anti-inflammatory, vasodilatory, and metabolically protective. It is not a waste product to be eliminated but a critically important metabolic regulator.

The bicarbonate value on a standard basic metabolic panel (BMP) reflects the balance between oxidative metabolism (which produces CO2) and glycolytic metabolism (which produces lactic acid). Low bicarbonate may indicate metabolic acidosis from excessive lactic acid production — a sign that cells are failing to complete oxidative metabolism and are instead fermenting glucose. This is the Warburg effect writ large: cancer cells and inflamed tissues are acidic precisely because they produce lactic acid instead of CO2. The shift from CO2-producing oxidation to lactate-producing glycolysis is the fundamental metabolic shift underlying degenerative disease.

CO2's role in oxygen delivery via the Bohr effect is foundational. Hemoglobin releases oxygen to tissues in proportion to local CO2 concentration. Without sufficient CO2, oxygen stays bound to hemoglobin — a condition of functional hypoxia despite normal blood oxygen saturation. Hyperventilation (blowing off CO2) is therefore one of the most immediately destructive metabolic behaviors. Many chronically ill people breathe 18-22 times per minute with no pause between breaths — chronic hyperventilation that constantly depletes their CO2 and impairs oxygen delivery.

Normal resting breathing should be 8-12 breaths per minute with comfortable pauses after exhalation. End-tidal CO2 should be around 40 mmHg. Peat recommended bag breathing (rebreathing into a paper bag) as an acute CO2 intervention: it quickly raises tissue CO2, improves oxygen delivery, and breaks the hyperventilation-anxiety cycle. People often feel immediate warmth, calmness, and mental clarity — revealing how much of their distress came from low CO2 rather than the psychological causes they assumed.

The respiratory quotient (RQ) — CO2 produced divided by O2 consumed — indicates fuel selection. An RQ of 1.0 means pure glucose oxidation, which Peat considered the metabolically optimal state because glucose oxidation produces more CO2 per oxygen consumed than fat oxidation. An RQ of 0.7 indicates fat oxidation. Peat rejected the mainstream concept of "metabolic flexibility" (ability to switch between fuels) as a virtue, arguing instead that chronic fat oxidation is a stress state, not an adaptation.

CO2 keeps calcium in solution in blood and stable in bones. When CO2 drops, calcium precipitates in soft tissues. CO2 retention also helps cells retain magnesium and potassium. Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) in water provides both sodium and bicarbonate, supporting oxidative metabolism and opposing lactic acid accumulation. Salt (sodium chloride) has direct effects on mitochondria, increasing energy and CO2 production.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine views CO2 primarily as a metabolic waste product. Peat viewed it as a critical metabolic regulator and protectant. Mainstream medicine considers fat oxidation ("metabolic flexibility") a sign of metabolic health. Peat considered chronic fat oxidation a stress state. The mainstream normal respiratory rate (12-20 breaths/min) includes many people who are chronically hyperventilating by Peat's standard (8-12 is optimal). Most importantly, mainstream medicine does not interpret the bicarbonate value on a BMP as a metabolic vitality indicator — it is checked primarily for acid-base balance in acute care. Peat's framework makes it a window into the most fundamental metabolic question: are cells producing energy through oxidation (CO2) or fermentation (lactic acid)?

## Causal Connections

- Adequate thyroid → oxidative metabolism → CO2 production → oxygen delivery (Bohr effect) → cellular energy
- Hypothyroidism → glycolytic metabolism → lactic acid instead of CO2 → metabolic acidosis → tissue hypoxia
- Hyperventilation → CO2 loss → impaired Bohr effect → functional hypoxia + platelet serotonin release + calcium precipitation
- Low CO2 → calcium precipitates in soft tissues → ectopic calcification (arteries, kidneys, brain)
- CO2 retention → helps cells retain magnesium and potassium → cellular stability
- Cancer / inflammation → Warburg effect → lactic acid production → low CO2 → acidic microenvironment
- Glucose oxidation (RQ 1.0) → maximum CO2 per O2 → optimal metabolic state
- Fat oxidation (RQ 0.7) → less CO2 per O2 → chronic stress metabolism
- Sodium (salt, baking soda) → supports mitochondrial function → increases CO2 production
- PTH elevation → blocks oxidative metabolism → shifts cells to lactic acid → lower CO2

## Sex Differences

No explicit sex-specific differences in CO2/bicarbonate interpretation were found in the extracted claims. However, given that estrogen promotes inflammation and impairs oxidative metabolism in Peat's framework, women with estrogen dominance may tend toward lower CO2 production and higher lactic acid output, potentially showing lower bicarbonate values on metabolic panels.

## Medication Effects

Thyroid supplementation (T3) directly increases oxidative metabolism and CO2 production — this is the foundational intervention. Bag breathing acutely raises tissue CO2 and can break the hyperventilation-anxiety cycle. Baking soda provides exogenous bicarbonate and can support acid-base balance. Adequate salt intake supports mitochondrial energy and CO2 production. Aspirin reduces inflammation that shifts metabolism toward glycolysis. Pharmaceutical diuretics deplete sodium, magnesium, and potassium, potentially worsening the metabolic conditions that reduce CO2 production.
