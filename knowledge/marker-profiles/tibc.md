# TIBC / Transferrin Saturation

**Reliability:** Tier 1 -- Peat explicitly endorsed transferrin saturation percentage as "probably the most useful single iron test." It is more reliable than ferritin because ferritin is too easily distorted by inflammation. This is the iron marker Peat gave a specific numeric target for.
**Companion markers:** Ferritin (secondary), hemoglobin (low end of normal), quantity of transferrin, copper/ceruloplasmin, estrogen/progesterone
**Peat's range:** Transferrin saturation should be under 30%. "A little under 30" is ideal.
**Mainstream range:** Transferrin saturation: 20-50% (normal); <20% suggests iron deficiency; >45% suggests overload. TIBC: 250-370 mcg/dL.

## Peat's Position

Transferrin saturation percentage is the only iron marker for which Peat gave a specific numeric target: under 30%, ideally a little under. This makes it the cornerstone of iron assessment in his framework, replacing ferritin as the primary test. Transferrin is the protein that transports iron safely through the bloodstream, and the saturation percentage indicates how loaded the transferrin molecule is with iron. High saturation means excessive circulating iron available to catalyze oxidative damage; low saturation means iron is being adequately bound and transported rather than circulating freely.

The total quantity of transferrin can be tested separately from the saturation percentage, and together these give a more complete picture than either alone. High transferrin with low saturation can indicate the body is expanding transport capacity in response to iron deficiency. Low transferrin with high saturation indicates overwhelmed transport -- a dangerous state where unbound iron is available to feed pathogens and catalyze free radical damage.

A critical insight from the corpus: iron overload is fundamentally a condition of estrogen excess. Estrogen subtracts copper from the system, promotes iron retention, and blocks bone marrow production of red cells. The iron "overload" is a downstream effect of hormonal dysfunction, not a primary mineral problem. Estrogen lowers hepcidin, causing greater dietary iron retention. Under stress, women retain several times as much dietary iron as men. This creates vicious circles: inflammation drives heme oxygenase, which drives estrogen, which drives more inflammation and iron retention.

Copper deficiency creates a particularly deceptive pattern: when copper is deficient, ceruloplasmin cannot oxidize ferrous (Fe2+) to ferric (Fe3+) iron for binding to transferrin. Iron accumulates in tissues in reduced toxic form while failing to reach sites where it is needed. Blood tests show apparent iron deficiency (low serum iron, low hemoglobin) but the real problem is inability to use iron, not lack of iron. Supplementing iron worsens the tissue burden without correcting the functional deficiency.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine uses transferrin saturation as part of the iron panel but often relies more heavily on ferritin for screening. Peat inverted this priority: transferrin saturation is the primary test, ferritin is secondary and unreliable. Mainstream considers transferrin saturation of 20-50% normal; Peat's target of under 30% is below the mainstream midpoint, reflecting his view that lower circulating iron is safer.

The most significant inversion is Peat's claim that iron overload is fundamentally hyperestrogenemia. Mainstream treats iron overload (hemochromatosis) as a genetic or acquired mineral metabolism disorder. Peat viewed it as a downstream consequence of hormonal dysfunction -- estrogen excess depletes copper, impairs ceruloplasmin, causes iron retention, and lowers hemoglobin simultaneously. Treating the iron without treating the estrogen misses the root cause.

Mainstream also treats copper deficiency and iron deficiency as separate conditions. Peat showed they are deeply intertwined: copper deficiency produces anemia indistinguishable from iron deficiency, and supplementing iron without correcting copper makes the problem worse.

## Causal Connections

- Transferrin saturation >30% -> excessive circulating iron -> oxidative damage risk
- Estrogen excess -> copper depletion -> impaired ceruloplasmin -> iron cannot bind transferrin properly
- Estrogen -> lowers hepcidin -> increased dietary iron retention
- Copper deficiency -> iron trapped in ferrous (Fe2+) form -> tissue overload + apparent blood deficiency
- Iron + PUFA -> severe lipid peroxidation (far worse than either alone)
- Free iron -> feeds pathogens, cancer (anything growing fast needs iron)
- Calcium deficiency -> high PTH -> intracellular alkaline shift -> increased protein affinity for iron and heavy metals
- CO2 -> keeps iron in safe ferric state; lactic acid -> activates reactive ferrous iron
- Inflammation -> both transferrin and ferritin increase in blood
- Iron in respiratory enzymes substitutes for copper when copper is deficient -> dysfunctional cytochrome oxidase

## Sex Differences

Women's iron metabolism is dominated by the estrogen-progesterone balance. Premenopausal women lose iron through menstruation (protective), but estrogen simultaneously causes tissue iron retention. After menopause, iron retention accelerates: progesterone fails, estrogen becomes unopposed (from peripheral aromatization), menstrual loss ceases, and women begin accumulating iron like men. This convergence partly explains the postmenopausal rise in cardiovascular disease.

Men accumulate iron progressively with no excretory pathway. Blood donation is the closest equivalent to menstrual iron loss and provides a mildly rejuvenating effect beyond just iron removal -- it also removes "stressins" (stress-related circulating substances).

For both sexes, the transferrin saturation target remains under 30%. Hemoglobin at the low end of the normal range (approximately 12-13 g/dL for women, 14-15 g/dL for men) is the safest monitoring complement.

## Medication Effects

- **Iron supplements:** Should not be given based on ferritin alone. Only consider if transferrin saturation is extremely low AND hemoglobin is genuinely deficient. Even then, address copper and thyroid first.
- **Coffee:** Peat's preferred safe iron chelator. Reduces heavy metals in liver, kidneys. Drink with iron-rich meals (liver, red meat).
- **Chocolate/cocoa:** Equally effective at inhibiting iron absorption. Consume with iron-rich meals.
- **Tea:** Tannins bind iron in gut, reducing absorption.
- **Aspirin + Vitamin E:** Protect against iron-catalyzed oxidative damage.
- **Vitamin D:** Lowers PTH, reducing cellular affinity for iron accumulation in soft tissues.
- **Copper supplementation (or copper-rich foods):** Oysters, liver, chocolate provide copper that enables ceruloplasmin function and proper iron metabolism.
- **Blood donation:** Recommended for iron management, especially for men and postmenopausal women.
- **Pharmaceutical chelators (deferoxamine):** Dangerous -- can relocate iron to worse places. Avoid in favor of coffee, diet modification.
- **Liver (dietary):** Once weekly with coffee to block iron absorption while getting vitamin A, B vitamins, and copper.
