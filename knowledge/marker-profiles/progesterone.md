# Progesterone

**Reliability:** Tier 2 -- Peat found serum progesterone useful but with the caveat that brain concentrations are ~10x blood levels, so serum underrepresents the most important site of action. Saliva tests are unreliable because progesterone is nearly insoluble in water.
**Companion markers:** Estradiol (the E2:P4 ratio is the real marker), cortisol, thyroid, temperature, pulse
**Peat's range:** Women endogenous luteal: ~30 mg/day production (serum 5-20 ng/mL). Therapeutic: 10-30 mg general; 100 mg for acute migraine; 10-20 mg dissolved in vitamin E is ~20x more potent. Men: 5-10 mg safe. Pregnancy: 800-900 mg/day (demonstrates safety margin).
**Mainstream range:** Follicular: <1.5 ng/mL; Luteal: 5-20 ng/mL; Pregnancy third trimester: 65-290 ng/mL; Male: 0.3-1.2 ng/mL

## Peat's Position

Progesterone was the centerpiece of Peat's therapeutic framework -- the most protective hormone in the body. Peat suggested it should not even be called a hormone — like cholesterol, it is a cell-stabilizing substance produced by cells, especially brain cells. Its concentration in the brain corresponds to brain quality and quality of life. The brain has the highest concentration of progesterone of any tissue, approximately ten times higher than blood levels, making it an essential brain-stabilizing molecule. A healthy late pregnancy produces 800-900 mg of progesterone daily from the placenta, demonstrating an enormous safety margin. Katharina Dalton's treated patients (working-class mothers, average IQ 90) produced babies averaging 130 IQ, with intelligence correlating dose-dependently with the amount of progesterone given. In single-substance experiments, progesterone increased brain growth in rats — glucose or glycine had similar effects, increasing brain development by increasing energy supply or the ability to use energy effectively.

Peat's central claim about progesterone is that it opposes estrogen through roughly ten simultaneous mechanisms: it destroys the estrogen receptor when it binds to the progesterone receptor, turns off estrogen production, changes estrogen metabolism toward detoxification, and activates clearance enzymes. It also degrades estrogen-binding proteins and inhibits enzymes that release estrogen from sulfates and glucuronides. This is not a gentle modulation but an absolute anti-estrogenic effect. If continuous estrogen exposure is interrupted by periodic progesterone, cancer does not develop. Lipschutz called pregnenolone and progesterone the "anti-fibromatogenic steroids" -- substances that inhibit fibrous overproduction and protect against tumors.

A key pharmacological insight: progesterone stimulates its own synthesis — taking it does not suppress the body's ability to produce it, unlike cortisol. Sometimes one dose or a few doses can restore the body's ability to produce enough on its own. Progesterone will not turn into estrogen — along with thyroid and aspirin, it reduces estrogen. It also lowers cortisol synthesis by acting on brain, pituitary, and adrenal glands simultaneously. It can also cause a temporarily enlarged thyroid gland to begin secreting, producing mild hyperthyroid symptoms for a few weeks while the gland unloads excess colloid and returns to normal size.

Progesterone provides stress protection equivalent to cortisol but without the catabolic tissue destruction. Pregnant rats with adrenals removed survived stresses that killed non-pregnant adrenalectomized rats -- progesterone alone provided complete stress protection without any cortisol. This is possible because progesterone supports oxidative metabolism directly, stabilizes membranes, and blocks the inflammatory cascade at multiple points. It has antihistamine effects, anti-serotonin effects, steadies heart rhythm the way digitalis does, and blocks aldosterone-driven cardiac fibrosis. It protects against intestinal inflammation during brain injury and systemic stress — inhibiting nitric oxide synthesis, prostaglandins, and reducing free fatty acid formation. It even protects against radiation injury because radiation's cellular effects parallel estrogen's.

Progesterone's role in epilepsy, depression, mania, heart failure, dementia, osteoporosis, neuropathies, myopathies, hypertension, pulmonary arterial hypertension, fibrosis, autoimmunity, and glaucoma has been neglected because of its classification as a "pregnancy hormone."

The critical distinction in the corpus is between natural progesterone and synthetic progestins (medroxyprogesterone). Synthetic progestins block conversion to the natural range of downstream steroids and interfere with brain function. The naming causes dangerous confusion -- progestins are anti-progesterone, not progesterone.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats estrogen as the primary female hormone and progesterone as secondary -- mainly relevant to pregnancy and menstrual regulation. Peat inverted this completely: progesterone is the primary protective hormone for both sexes, while estrogen is a stress hormone. Mainstream prescribes synthetic progestins interchangeably with progesterone; Peat considered progestins to be anti-progesterone agents that interfere with brain function and block the natural steroid cascade.

Mainstream endocrinology also treats menopausal symptoms as estrogen deficiency. Peat argued the real deficiency is progesterone: in rat studies, ovariectomy restores normal estrogen levels within a week (from adrenal/peripheral sources), but progesterone disappears. The "estrogen deficiency" model led to HRT with estrogen, which Peat considered adding fuel to the fire.

In the 1930s, progesterone deficiency was recognized as a diagnostic sign of hypothyroidism. This connection has been largely forgotten by modern medicine.

The large NIH-funded ProTECT trial ($28M, 6 years) for progesterone in traumatic brain injury failed because the progesterone was dissolved in soybean oil intralipid emulsion. The PUFA in intralipid is toxic to the brain, increasing inflammation and blocking energy metabolism — negating progesterone's benefit. The study tested "progesterone + brain toxin" vs placebo.

## Sex Differences

For women, progesterone is the critical hormone to assess. A woman produces about 30 mg/day during the luteal phase. Fertility requires 50-100 times as much progesterone as estrogen. Women who have been pregnant eight times have higher residual progesterone levels and live much longer. If progesterone supplements are used continuously all month, the liver upregulates excretion over two weeks; the natural cycle's two-week rest allows enzyme reset. Peat recommended cycling progesterone two weeks on, two weeks off for premenopausal women. Post-menopausal women also benefit from cyclic use (imitating the menstrual cycle), unless using it to control a specific symptom. When thyroid function is good, cycling is even more effective.

For men, 5-10 mg of progesterone is in the normal range and reduces estrogen production, making testosterone's effect more potent. Higher doses (30 mg) can antagonize testosterone in men, so the dose must stay low. Because testosterone can be converted to estrogen via aromatase, progesterone is actually more protective than testosterone in aging -- it cannot be aromatized. Progesterone will not turn into estrogen.

In both sexes, progesterone's brain concentration is ~10x blood levels, making serum measurements a significant underestimate of neurological progesterone status.

## Medication Effects

- **Natural progesterone dissolved in vitamin E:** ~20x more potent than other preparations because it enters the chylomicron pathway, bypassing the liver's first-pass metabolism. 10-20 mg is often an effective dose. For general effects: 10-30 mg; for acute migraine: 100 mg (complete reversal in 1-2 minutes). Women cycle 2 weeks on, 2 weeks off. Men use 5-10 mg. In extreme cases (severe addiction withdrawal): up to 1000 mg in the first night was used successfully.
- **Natural progesterone (Prometrium, compounded):** Standard oral preparations require higher doses due to hepatic first-pass. Enormous safety margin (800-900 mg/day in pregnancy).
- **Synthetic progestins (medroxyprogesterone/Provera):** Not progesterone. Block natural steroid conversion, interfere with brain function. Avoid entirely.
- **Birth control pills:** Estrogen component raises prolactin and cortisol; progestin component is anti-progesterone. Net effect is estrogen dominance.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Required for progesterone synthesis. In the 1930s, progesterone deficiency was a diagnostic sign of hypothyroidism.
- **Pregnenolone:** Upstream precursor; converts to progesterone according to the body's need. The safest first-line intervention.
- **Vitamin A:** Required for cholesterol-to-pregnenolone-to-progesterone conversion. Deficiency causes progesterone deficiency.
- **Statins/low-cholesterol diets:** Deplete the raw material for all steroid synthesis including progesterone.
