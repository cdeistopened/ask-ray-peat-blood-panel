# LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein)

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered the standard LDL classification as "bad cholesterol" fundamentally wrong; LDL is the delivery vehicle for steroid hormone precursors, and its composition matters far more than its count
**Companion markers:** Total cholesterol, HDL, triglycerides, PUFA intake, progesterone, pregnenolone, DHEA, thyroid markers
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically; low LDL increases cancer and dementia risk. LDL directly determines progesterone secretion rate.
**Mainstream range:** <100 mg/dL (optimal), <70 mg/dL (high-risk patients)

## Peat's Position

Peat rejected the classification of LDL as "bad cholesterol." In his framework, LDL is the delivery vehicle for steroid hormone precursors — it carries cholesterol to tissues throughout the body where it is converted to protective hormones like pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, and testosterone. The enrichment data provides a striking experimental demonstration: in an ovary perfusion experiment, the amount of progesterone secreted varied directly with the amount of LDL cholesterol in the blood. LDL is not merely transporting cholesterol — it is the rate-limiting delivery system for steroidogenesis. Lowering LDL reduces this delivery and deprives steroid-producing glands (ovaries, adrenals) of essential raw material.

When LDL is driven too low, the consequences extend beyond hormone production. Cancer risk and dementia risk increase because cells — and especially the brain — are starved of cholesterol needed for neurosteroid synthesis and membrane integrity. Cholesterol starvation arrests cell division in a tetraploid state (double chromosomes), creating unstable cells prone to becoming aneuploid cancer cells. The mitotic spindle that sorts chromosomes during cell division requires adequate cholesterol. Without it, cells cannot complete mitosis and are arrested in a precancerous state.

The danger attributed to LDL comes not from native LDL but from oxidized LDL. It is the PUFA incorporated into LDL particles that undergoes peroxidation, producing reactive aldehydes that damage arteries and trigger inflammatory, atherogenic processes. Unoxidized native cholesterol does not damage blood vessels. The foam cells found in atherosclerotic plaques contain cholesterol esters of specifically highly unsaturated fatty acids — cholesteryl eicosapentaenoate (from EPA), cholesteryl docosahexaenoate (from DHA), cholesteryl arachidonate, and cholesteryl linoleate. These PUFA-cholesterol esters are the actual atherogenic material, not native cholesterol.

PUFA also inactivates ABCA1, the protein responsible for removing excess cholesterol from arteries — a direct mechanism by which PUFA promotes plaque formation. Saturated fat does not have this effect. Progesterone inhibits ACAT (the enzyme that forms toxic cholesterol esters) in all tissues except the liver, where it promotes cholesterol export for steroid synthesis — making progesterone a natural protector against the atherogenic process.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine classifies LDL as "bad cholesterol" and sets progressively lower targets (below 100, below 70 for high-risk patients), treating LDL reduction as a primary therapeutic goal. Peat inverted this completely: LDL is a protective delivery system for cholesterol, and driving it too low increases cancer and dementia risk by starving cells of steroid hormone precursors and cholesterol needed for mitosis. The ovary perfusion experiment demonstrates that progesterone production is directly proportional to LDL availability — lowering LDL with statins reduces protective steroid production.

The conventional framing that "lower LDL = better" ignores that the fat composition within the particles, not the particle count, determines whether LDL is harmful. LDL containing saturated fat is metabolically stable; LDL containing PUFA is susceptible to peroxidation and becomes the oxidized LDL that actually damages arteries. Foam cells contain specifically PUFA-derived cholesterol esters (EPA, DHA, arachidonic acid, linoleate). Mainstream lipid panels do not distinguish between native and oxidized LDL, and they do not assess the PUFA content of particles.

## Causal Connections

- LDL delivers cholesterol → steroid-producing glands convert to pregnenolone/progesterone/DHEA/testosterone
- LDL availability directly determines progesterone secretion rate (ovary perfusion experiment)
- ↓LDL → ↓cholesterol delivery → ↓steroid hormones → ↑cancer, ↑dementia risk
- ↓LDL → cholesterol starvation → cell division arrested in tetraploid state → aneuploid cancer cells
- PUFA in LDL particles → lipid peroxidation → oxidized LDL → arterial damage and atherosclerosis
- PUFA → inactivates ABCA1 → cholesterol cannot exit macrophages → foam cell formation → plaque
- Saturated fat in LDL particles → metabolically stable → no oxidative damage
- Progesterone → inhibits ACAT → prevents toxic cholesterol ester formation in tissues
- Hypothyroidism → ↑total cholesterol → ↑LDL (accumulation of unconverted precursor)
- Thyroid supplementation → ↑cholesterol-to-steroid conversion → ↓LDL (converted to hormones)

## Sex Differences

The ovary perfusion experiment directly demonstrates that LDL availability determines progesterone production in women. Lowering LDL with statins in women of reproductive age would reduce progesterone synthesis, potentially worsening estrogen dominance. In men, the same mechanism applies to testosterone and DHEA production via adrenal LDL uptake.

## Medication Effects

- **Statins:** Directly lower LDL, which in Peat's framework reduces the delivery of protective cholesterol to steroid-producing tissues and increases cancer and dementia risk. Also deprive mitochondria of cholesterol needed for ATP production.
- **PUFA/seed oils:** Increase the PUFA content of LDL particles, making them more susceptible to oxidative damage. Also inactivate ABCA1, promoting plaque formation.
- **Thyroid (T3):** Lowers LDL by driving cholesterol conversion to steroid hormones — the appropriate way to reduce cholesterol rather than blocking its delivery.
- **Progesterone:** Inhibits ACAT in peripheral tissues, preventing formation of toxic PUFA-cholesterol esters. Protective against atherosclerosis.
- **Fish oil (EPA/DHA):** Despite mainstream promotion, foam cells contain specifically EPA and DHA cholesterol esters — supplementing these may increase atherogenic material in LDL particles.
