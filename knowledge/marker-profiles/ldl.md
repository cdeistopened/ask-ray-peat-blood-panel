# LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein)

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered the standard LDL/HDL ratio interpretation oversimplified and backwards; what matters is the composition of the particles, not the count
**Companion markers:** Total cholesterol, HDL, triglycerides, PUFA intake, oxidized LDL, steroid hormones
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically; low LDL increases cancer and dementia risk
**Mainstream range:** <100 mg/dL (optimal), <70 mg/dL (high-risk patients)

## Peat's Position

Peat rejected the classification of LDL as "bad cholesterol." In his framework, LDL is the delivery vehicle for steroid hormone precursors — it carries cholesterol to tissues throughout the body where it is converted to protective hormones like pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, and testosterone. Lowering LDL reduces this delivery and deprives tissues of essential raw material. When LDL is low, cancer risk and dementia risk increase, because cells and especially the brain are starved of cholesterol needed for neurosteroid synthesis and membrane integrity.

The danger attributed to LDL comes not from native LDL but from oxidized LDL. It is the cholesterol in LDL that has been oxidized — specifically when polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) incorporated into LDL particles undergo peroxidation — that damages arteries and triggers inflammatory, atherogenic processes. Unoxidized native cholesterol does not damage blood vessels. The original atherosclerosis experiments used toxic oxidized cholesterol, not native cholesterol, to produce disease in animals. This means the composition of fats within LDL particles (saturated vs. PUFA) determines their biological effect far more than the particle count itself.

Peat also noted that when cholesterol is very low, thyroid supplementation alone may be insufficient to produce adequate steroid hormones because there is not enough substrate. In such cases, one must also supplement pregnenolone, progesterone, or DHEA directly.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine classifies LDL as "bad cholesterol" and sets progressively lower targets (below 100, below 70 for high-risk patients), treating LDL reduction as a primary therapeutic goal. Peat inverted this completely: LDL is a protective delivery system for cholesterol, and driving it too low increases cancer and dementia risk. The conventional framing that "lower LDL = better" ignores that the fat composition within the particles, not the particle count, determines whether LDL is harmful. LDL containing saturated fat is metabolically stable; LDL containing PUFA is susceptible to peroxidation and becomes the oxidized LDL that actually damages arteries. Mainstream lipid panels do not distinguish between native and oxidized LDL, and they do not assess the PUFA content of particles.

## Causal Connections

- LDL delivers cholesterol → tissues convert to pregnenolone/progesterone/DHEA/testosterone
- ↓LDL → ↓cholesterol delivery to tissues → ↓steroid hormones → ↑cancer, ↑dementia risk
- PUFA in LDL particles → lipid peroxidation → oxidized LDL → arterial damage and atherosclerosis
- Saturated fat in LDL particles → metabolically stable → no oxidative damage
- ↓Total cholesterol → ↓LDL substrate → may need direct pregnenolone/progesterone supplementation
- Hypothyroidism → ↑total cholesterol → ↑LDL (accumulation of unconverted precursor)
- Thyroid supplementation → ↓cholesterol → ↓LDL (converted to steroid hormones)

## Medication Effects

- **Statins:** Directly lower LDL, which in Peat's framework reduces the delivery of protective cholesterol to tissues and increases cancer and dementia risk.
- **PUFA/seed oils:** Increase the PUFA content of LDL particles, making them more susceptible to oxidative damage — the actual mechanism of arterial harm.
- **Thyroid (T3):** Lowers LDL by driving cholesterol conversion to steroid hormones, which Peat considered the appropriate way to reduce cholesterol rather than blocking its delivery.
