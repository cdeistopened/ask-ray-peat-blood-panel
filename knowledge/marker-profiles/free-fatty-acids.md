# Free Fatty Acids (FFA / NEFA)

**Reliability:** Tier 1 -- Peat considered FFA one of his gold-standard markers, rarely tested but "extremely important." Placed alongside CO2, transferrin saturation, PTH, albumin, and cholesterol as a test that directly reflects metabolic state.
**Companion markers:** Albumin (FFA:albumin ratio determines toxicity), glucose, insulin, cortisol, adrenaline, thyroid (T3/TSH), PUFA dietary history
**Peat's range:** As low as possible. No specific numeric target stated, but elevation indicates stress-driven lipolysis. The composition matters as much as the quantity -- PUFA-type FFAs are far more toxic than saturated FFAs.
**Mainstream range:** 0.1-0.45 mmol/L (rarely included in standard panels)

## Peat's Position

Free fatty acids are, in Peat's framework, the most dangerous and most neglected blood lipid marker. While mainstream medicine fixates on cholesterol and LDL, Peat argued that circulating free fatty acids -- not cholesterol, not triglycerides -- are the single most destructive lipid fraction in blood. They block glucose oxidation through the Randle cycle, suppress thyroid receptor binding, stimulate glucagon, and generate inflammatory prostaglandins. "Free fatty acids are the dangerous form of fats in the blood; they have multiple interferences, for example with oxidative metabolism and the use of sugar."

The damage is not uniform across fatty acid types. Saturated free fatty acids can be oxidized cleanly by mitochondria. Polyunsaturated free fatty acids -- primarily linoleic acid liberated from adipose tissue -- damage mitochondria directly through lipid peroxidation, suppress thyroid function, and generate inflammatory prostaglandins that saturated fats cannot. "Only polyunsaturated fatty acids have the terrible anti-energy effects."

FFAs rise when the body mobilizes stored fat under stress. Cortisol and adrenaline trigger lipolysis as an emergency fuel source when glucose oxidation is failing. This makes elevated FFA a direct marker of the stress state -- the body has shifted from efficient glucose burning to emergency fat mobilization. Free fatty acids in turn activate adrenaline, ACTH, cortisol, TSH, and glucagon, creating a self-amplifying stress loop.

The ratio of free fatty acids to albumin determines metabolic toxicity. Albumin binds FFAs, rendering them metabolically inert during transport to the liver for detoxification. When FFAs exceed albumin's binding capacity, unbound FFAs circulate freely and poison cellular metabolism. "Keeping the free fatty acids low in relation to albumin, they will largely be carried bound to the albumin to the liver." This is why albumin status (target 4.9-5.0+) matters for FFA interpretation.

A particularly striking demonstration of FFA toxicity: intravenous injection of emulsified soy oil (which floods the blood with PUFA free fatty acids) produces hyperglycemia within 15 minutes. This demonstrates the Randle cycle in real time -- PUFA fatty acids immediately block pyruvate dehydrogenase, preventing glucose oxidation, causing blood glucose to rise even without any dietary sugar.

Stored body fat becomes more PUFA-rich with age, making each episode of fat mobilization progressively more toxic. Fat cells preferentially oxidize saturated fatty acids, so PUFA concentrates in stores over time. This is why aggressive weight loss in older adults (through fasting or extreme dieting) can be metabolically devastating -- it floods the system with decades of accumulated PUFA.

The nocturnal FFA rise is a key clinical connection. Blood sugar normally drops at night, compensated by rising free fatty acids. This nocturnal FFA rise represents a stress pattern that explains night sweats, insomnia, and early-morning cortisol spikes. Eating carbohydrate at bedtime prevents this by maintaining liver glycogen through the night.

## Mainstream Inversion

Free fatty acids are rarely tested in standard metabolic panels. Mainstream medicine focuses on LDL cholesterol as the primary lipid risk marker, with triglycerides as a secondary concern. FFAs are largely absent from routine screening -- most patients and many physicians have never heard of the test.

Peat considered this a fundamental error. The Randle cycle -- the competition between fatty acids and glucose for cellular oxidation -- explains insulin resistance, Type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease more coherently than the cholesterol hypothesis. When FFAs are high (especially PUFA), cells burn fat preferentially, blocking glucose oxidation and lowering CO2 production. This creates the "diabetic" picture: high blood glucose, high insulin, cells starving despite glucose abundance. The mainstream response -- lowering blood sugar with drugs or restricting dietary sugar -- misses the root cause entirely. The FFA-driven Randle cycle is the mechanism, not glucose excess.

Niacin's dual effectiveness against both heart disease and diabetes makes sense through this lens: it lowers free fatty acids. "It's recognized for years that niacin is effective for not only heart disease, but diabetes simply because it lowers the free fatty acids." Mainstream medicine knows niacin works but has not connected it to FFA as the unifying mechanism.

## Causal Connections

- Stress (cortisol, adrenaline) -> lipolysis -> FFA release -> Randle cycle activation -> glucose oxidation blocked
- PUFA-type FFAs -> mitochondrial damage via lipid peroxidation -> impaired oxidative metabolism
- PUFA FFAs -> thyroid receptor suppression -> functional hypothyroidism
- FFAs -> activate adrenaline, ACTH, cortisol, TSH, glucagon -> self-amplifying stress loop
- Estrogen -> activates insulin AND FFA release simultaneously -> Randle cycle -> intracellular glucose deprivation
- Aging -> stored fat becomes more PUFA-rich -> each stress-driven fat mobilization is more toxic
- Nocturnal blood sugar drop -> FFA rise -> explains insomnia, night sweats, 2-4 AM waking
- Low albumin -> reduced FFA binding capacity -> unbound FFAs more toxic
- Niacinamide -> inhibits lipase -> lowers FFA release from stores
- Aspirin -> blocks inflammatory prostaglandins from PUFA FFAs
- Sugar/carbohydrate -> suppresses cortisol/adrenaline -> suppresses lipolysis -> FFAs stay stored
- Coffee (with food) -> stabilizes blood sugar -> reduces FFA release via magnesium, niacin, caffeine antioxidant effects

## Sex Differences

No sex-specific FFA claims were extracted from the corpus. However, the estrogen connection is relevant: estrogen activates both insulin and FFA release simultaneously. Through the Randle cycle, these FFAs block glucose oxidation, creating intracellular glucose deprivation even when blood sugar is normal. This mechanism is more pronounced in women with higher estrogen (premenopausal, on oral contraceptives, or in estrogen-dominant states around age 40). The practical implication: women with high estrogen-to-progesterone ratios may have higher circulating FFAs and more Randle cycle interference, contributing to the higher rates of insulin resistance seen in estrogen-dominant states.

## Medication Effects

- **Niacinamide (Vitamin B3):** Peat's primary recommendation for lowering FFAs. Inhibits the lipase that liberates fats from adipose stores. "Niacinamide and aspirin as the most practical tools for lowering free fatty acids and stopping the stress reaction that elevates blood sugar."
- **Aspirin:** Blocks inflammatory prostaglandin synthesis from PUFA-type FFAs. Does not lower total FFA but reduces the inflammatory damage they cause.
- **Sugar / Carbohydrate:** Suppresses cortisol and adrenaline-driven lipolysis, keeping FFAs stored rather than circulating. "Sugar is one of the things that can reduce the flow of polyunsaturated fats out of your tissues into your bloodstream."
- **Niacin (nicotinic acid):** Recognized for decades as effective for both heart disease and diabetes. The mechanism: lowering FFAs. "Niacin is effective for not only heart disease, but diabetes simply because it lowers the free fatty acids."
- **Coffee (with food):** Contains magnesium, niacin, and B vitamins. Caffeine has antioxidant effects. "As long as you use it with food, it tends to stabilize blood sugar and reduce inflammation."
- **Thyroid supplementation:** By restoring oxidative metabolism, thyroid reduces the body's reliance on stress-driven fat mobilization, lowering FFAs indirectly.
- **Statins:** Lower cholesterol but do not address FFA. In Peat's framework, statins remove the raw material for protective steroid synthesis without addressing the actual lipid problem (circulating FFAs).
- **Low-carb / ketogenic diets:** Dramatically increase FFA mobilization by design. In Peat's framework, these diets elevate the most dangerous lipid fraction while lowering the least dangerous one (blood glucose).
- **Fasting:** Maximally activates cortisol-driven lipolysis. Liberates stored PUFA into circulation. "On a complete water fast for 10 days, they found that 80% or more of the weight loss came from the destruction of muscle and skin" -- not from safe fat burning.
