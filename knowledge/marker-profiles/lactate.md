# Lactate (Lactic Acid)

**Reliability:** Tier 1 — Peat considered lactate one of the most informative metabolic markers, providing better objective evidence of metabolic health than many conventional markers
**Companion markers:** Temperature, pulse, CO2/bicarbonate, thyroid function (T3/TSH), free fatty acids, glucose
**Peat's range:** <1.5 mmol/L (normal resting); 2-4 (metabolic stress); >4 (significant dysfunction)
**Mainstream range:** 0.5-2.2 mmol/L (varies by lab); >4 mmol/L = lactic acidosis

## Peat's Position

Lactate occupies a privileged position in Peat's diagnostic hierarchy because it directly reflects the ratio of oxidative to glycolytic metabolism — the central variable in his entire health framework. Normal resting blood lactate should be below 1.5 mmol/L. Levels of 2-4 indicate metabolic stress, meaning a significant proportion of cells are resorting to glycolysis (fermentation) instead of completing oxidative metabolism. Levels above 4 indicate significant metabolic dysfunction. Tracking lactate provides better objective evidence of metabolic improvement than cholesterol, body weight, or other commonly tracked metrics.

The diseases Peat considered most important — cancer, Alzheimer's, heart disease, and aging itself — all share the metabolic signature of elevated lactate. Cancer cells famously produce lactate via the Warburg effect (aerobic glycolysis). Neuronal glycolysis in Alzheimer's produces excess lactate. Cardiac inefficiency in heart disease produces lactate. All of these conditions share the deeper pattern of elevated fatty acids, impaired glucose oxidation, and compensatory stress responses — and all are reflected in elevated resting lactate.

Peat was particularly concerned about exercise-induced lactate. When lactic acid appears during exercise, blood sugar is falling and active thyroid hormone (T3) production drops. If nutrition is not ideal, a single episode of lactic acid-producing exercise can knock a person into a lower metabolic state. Eastern European exercise physiologists discovered they could improve athletic performance by having athletes stop exercising before lactic acid accumulated. Brief, sub-threshold exercise makes muscles produce testosterone and DHEA, while crossing the lactate threshold shifts muscle into glycolysis and suppresses steroid production. Peat recommended checking blood lactate levels in people with cancer, as tumors produce large amounts of lactic acid.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine tests lactate primarily in emergency/critical care settings (sepsis, shock, lactic acidosis) and largely ignores it as a routine metabolic health marker. Peat argued that resting lactate should be a standard part of metabolic assessment because it directly measures what matters — whether cells are performing oxidative metabolism (health) or glycolysis (disease). The mainstream fitness paradigm also inverts Peat's view: conventional exercise science treats lactate accumulation during training as normal and even desirable ("no pain, no gain"), while Peat considered exercise that produces lactate potentially harmful, capable of knocking a person into a lower metabolic state. Eastern European physiologists proved that sub-lactate-threshold exercise produces better results. Mainstream medicine also does not routinely check lactate in cancer patients, despite tumors being major lactate producers.

## Causal Connections

- PUFA → blocks pyruvate dehydrogenase (Randle cycle) → glucose cannot be oxidized → ↑lactate
- ↓T3 (hypothyroidism) → impaired oxidative metabolism → cells resort to glycolysis → ↑lactate
- ↑Lactate → returns to liver (Cori cycle) → reconverted to glucose → futile hyperglycemia
- Exercise above lactate threshold → ↓blood sugar + ↓T3 production → lower metabolic state
- Sub-lactate-threshold exercise → muscle produces testosterone + DHEA → improved performance
- Cancer (Warburg effect) → ↑lactate production → detectable in blood lactate
- ↑Lactate + ↑free fatty acids + ↓glucose oxidation = shared metabolic signature of cancer, Alzheimer's, heart disease, aging
- ↓CO2 production ↔ ↑lactate production (inverse markers of oxidative vs. glycolytic metabolism)
- Adequate thyroid + ↓PUFA → restored oxidative metabolism → ↓lactate

## Medication Effects

- **T3 (thyroid):** Restores oxidative metabolism, shifting cells from glycolysis to oxidative phosphorylation, which lowers resting lactate.
- **Niacinamide:** Lowers free fatty acids, preventing Randle cycle activation that drives glycolysis and lactate production.
- **Aspirin:** Blocks inflammatory prostaglandins from PUFA, supporting oxidative metabolism.
- **Sugar/fruit juice:** Provides substrate for oxidative metabolism and suppresses lipolysis, reducing the free fatty acids that block glucose oxidation and drive lactate production.
