## Peat's Position

The Peat corpus does not contain extensive direct commentary on homocysteine as a blood marker. What exists places homocysteine within the context of methionine metabolism and the broader amino acid balance Peat emphasized. Homocysteine accumulates when B12 is deficient (impaired recycling) or methionine intake is excessive (overwhelming recycling capacity). Elevated homocysteine is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and cognitive decline. Glycine helps clear homocysteine by supporting the transsulfuration pathway.

Peat was deeply interested in the methionine-glycine ratio: he recommended limiting methionine-rich muscle meats and supplementing glycine-rich gelatin to balance amino acid intake. Homocysteine as a methylation intermediate fits directly into this concern — excess methionine without adequate glycine, B12, folate, and B6 would elevate homocysteine as a predictable consequence. His emphasis on B vitamins (particularly B6 for prolactin control and general metabolic support, at doses around 10 mg) would also support homocysteine clearance.

The entity graph links homocysteine from methionine, confirming that Peat's interest was within the framework of amino acid metabolism rather than as an independent cardiovascular or inflammation marker. His emphasis on adequate protein from gelatin-rich sources, liver health, and B vitamin adequacy would all affect homocysteine levels, but these connections are inferred from the broader framework rather than stated as direct claims about homocysteine.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine uses homocysteine as a cardiovascular risk marker and indicator of B vitamin status. Elevated homocysteine is treated with B6, B12, and folate supplementation. Without extensive direct Peat commentary, we cannot identify specific mainstream inversions. However, Peat's general framework suggests he would emphasize the metabolic context — thyroid function, liver health, methionine-glycine balance — rather than simply supplementing B vitamins to lower a number. He would likely point to the methionine excess from muscle-meat-heavy diets (without gelatin) as a more fundamental driver than B vitamin deficiency alone.

## Causal Connections

- Methionine metabolism → homocysteine as intermediate → requires B12/folate/B6 for recycling
- B12 deficiency → impaired homocysteine recycling → elevated homocysteine → cardiovascular/neurological risk
- B6 deficiency → impaired transsulfuration → homocysteine accumulation (Peat recommended B6 at ~10 mg for prolactin control)
- High methionine diet (muscle meats without gelatin) → overwhelming recycling capacity → elevated homocysteine
- Glycine/gelatin supplementation → supports transsulfuration pathway → helps clear homocysteine
- Liver health → methylation capacity → homocysteine clearance
