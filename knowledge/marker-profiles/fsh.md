# FSH / LH (Gonadotropins)

**Reliability:** Tier 3 -- Peat found these useful only in context. Doctors worry about low FSH/LH, but Peat argued that low values can indicate appropriate suppression (not pathology) when downstream hormones are adequate. High values under stress reflect pituitary dysfunction, not gonadal health.
**Companion markers:** Prolactin, TSH, testosterone, progesterone, estrogen, thyroid (T3/T4)
**Peat's range:** Low FSH/LH can be appropriate if downstream hormones are adequate. Stress raises all pituitary hormones except LH.
**Mainstream range:** FSH men: 1.5-12.4 mIU/mL; FSH women: varies by cycle phase (follicular 3.5-12.5, mid-cycle 4.7-21.5, luteal 1.7-7.7, postmenopausal 25.8-134.8); LH men: 1.8-8.6 mIU/mL; LH women: varies by cycle phase

## Peat's Position

Peat's view of the gonadotropins centers on one remarkable observation: stress induces all pituitary hormones except luteinizing hormone. LH is uniquely suppressed by stress, while FSH, TSH, prolactin, ACTH, and growth hormone all rise. Since LH is the hormone that stimulates testosterone and progesterone production, this means stress simultaneously amplifies the stress-reactive pituitary outputs while shutting down the one signal that produces protective steroids. This single mechanism explains much of the hormonal deterioration Peat associated with chronic stress.

Doctors typically worry when pituitary hormones are low, preferring to see FSH, LH, and TSH in a "certain range." But Peat argued that if thyroid is supplemented and TSH drops, that represents appropriate suppression -- not pathology. The same principle applies to FSH and LH: if downstream hormones (testosterone, progesterone) are adequate, low gonadotropins indicate a well-functioning system, not a deficient one. High pituitary hormones indicate stress, not health.

Perhaps the most provocative claim in the corpus: experimenters removed animals' pituitary glands entirely and replaced only cortisol and thyroid (not LH, GH, or ACTH). These animals lived twice as long or more -- comparable to animals that naturally lack growth hormone through genetic mutation. This suggests that the pituitary hormones (including FSH and LH) may be stress-adaptive rather than essential, and that their chronic elevation with aging is part of the degenerative process, not the body's attempt at repair.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine interprets elevated FSH as a primary indicator of ovarian failure or menopause, and elevated LH in context with FSH for PCOS diagnosis or pituitary assessment. Low gonadotropins in a symptomatic patient prompt investigation for pituitary insufficiency. Peat inverted this: high pituitary hormones indicate stress, and low ones (with adequate downstream hormones) indicate health. The postmenopausal rise in FSH, which mainstream treats as the expected response to ovarian failure, Peat would interpret as part of the degenerative pituitary stress response that accompanies aging.

The hypophysectomy experiments suggesting pituitary hormones are dispensable for longevity directly contradict the mainstream endocrine framework, which treats all pituitary axes as essential for health.

## Causal Connections

- Stress -> all pituitary hormones rise EXCEPT LH -> protective steroid production falls
- Low thyroid -> TSH rises -> prolactin co-rises -> LH is suppressed
- Serotonin -> drives all pituitary hormones -> stress-induced pituitary hyperfunction
- LH suppression -> less testosterone and progesterone production -> estrogen dominance
- Estrogen -> further pituitary stress -> more prolactin, less LH
- Thyroid supplementation -> TSH drops -> pituitary stress decreases -> LH may normalize
- Pituitary removal + cortisol/thyroid replacement -> doubled lifespan (animal studies)

## Sex Differences

In women, FSH and LH are conventionally used to assess menstrual cycle phase, ovarian reserve, and menopausal status. Peat's framework reinterprets high postmenopausal FSH not as the expected response to ovarian failure but as part of the pituitary stress pattern that also elevates prolactin and TSH. In PCOS, the elevated LH:FSH ratio is conventionally the diagnostic marker; Peat would look upstream to the stress, estrogen, and thyroid factors driving the pituitary response.

In men, low LH with low testosterone prompts mainstream investigation for secondary hypogonadism. Peat would assess whether the system is stressed (prolactin high, cortisol high) and address thyroid, diet, and protective steroid status rather than prescribing gonadotropin therapy.

## Medication Effects

- **Thyroid supplementation:** Reduces pituitary stress; TSH drops and LH may normalize as the pituitary stress burden decreases.
- **Clomiphene (Clomid):** Standard medical treatment to raise LH/FSH. Peat's corpus does not address it directly, but the mechanism (blocking estrogen negative feedback) is consistent with the framework -- it artificially raises LH by blocking the estrogen signal, which is a workaround rather than a systemic fix.
- **GnRH agonists/antagonists:** Used in fertility treatment and prostate cancer. Peat's framework does not directly address these.
- **Birth control pills:** Suppress FSH/LH through exogenous estrogen/progestin. In Peat's view, this replaces the body's own regulation with synthetic stress signals.
- **Anti-serotonin agents (cyproheptadine):** By lowering serotonin's drive on all pituitary hormones, may normalize pituitary output including FSH/LH balance.
