Doctors worry about low FSH/LH, but Peat argued that low values can indicate appropriate suppression (not pathology) when downstream hormones are adequate. Enrichment confirms serotonin as the master pituitary stimulator driving all pituitary hormones including FSH and LH.
**Companion markers:** Prolactin, TSH, testosterone, progesterone, estrogen, thyroid (T3/T4), serotonin

**Mainstream range:** FSH men: 1.5-12.4 mIU/mL; FSH women: varies by cycle phase (follicular 3.5-12.5, mid-cycle 4.7-21.5, luteal 1.7-7.7, postmenopausal 25.8-134.8); LH men: 1.8-8.6 mIU/mL; LH women: varies by cycle phase

## Peat's Position

Peat's view of the gonadotropins centers on one remarkable observation: stress induces all pituitary hormones except luteinizing hormone. LH is uniquely suppressed by stress, while FSH, TSH, prolactin, ACTH, and growth hormone all rise. Since LH is the hormone that stimulates testosterone and progesterone production, this means stress simultaneously amplifies the stress-reactive pituitary outputs while shutting down the one signal that produces protective steroids. This single mechanism explains much of the hormonal deterioration Peat associated with chronic stress.

The enrichment data reveals the upstream driver of this pituitary activation: serotonin. Peat identified serotonin, along with estrogen, as the major promoter of all pituitary hormones — prolactin, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, GH, MSH, POMC, vasopressin, and oxytocin. Stresses increase serotonin and all these hormones, leading to adaptive changes at the cost of inflammation and degeneration. This makes serotonin the master switch for pituitary hyperfunction during aging — and anti-serotonin interventions (cyproheptadine, thyroid support, reduced PUFA) potentially therapeutic for normalizing the entire pituitary axis.

Doctors typically worry when pituitary hormones are low, preferring to see FSH, LH, and TSH in a "certain range." But Peat argued that if thyroid is supplemented and TSH drops, that represents appropriate suppression — not pathology. The same principle applies to FSH and LH: if downstream hormones (testosterone, progesterone) are adequate, low gonadotropins indicate a well-functioning system, not a deficient one. High pituitary hormones indicate stress, not health.

Perhaps the most provocative claim in the corpus: experimenters removed animals' pituitary glands entirely and replaced only cortisol and thyroid (not LH, GH, or ACTH). These animals lived twice as long or more — comparable to animals that naturally lack growth hormone through genetic mutation. This suggests that the pituitary hormones (including FSH and LH) may be stress-adaptive rather than essential, and that their chronic elevation with aging is part of the degenerative process, not the body's attempt at repair.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine interprets elevated FSH as a primary indicator of ovarian failure or menopause, and elevated LH in context with FSH for PCOS diagnosis or pituitary assessment. Low gonadotropins in a symptomatic patient prompt investigation for pituitary insufficiency. Peat inverted this: high pituitary hormones indicate stress (driven by serotonin), and low ones (with adequate downstream hormones) indicate health. The postmenopausal rise in FSH, which mainstream treats as the expected response to ovarian failure, Peat would interpret as part of the serotonin-driven degenerative pituitary stress response that accompanies aging.

The hypophysectomy experiments suggesting pituitary hormones are dispensable for longevity directly contradict the mainstream endocrine framework, which treats all pituitary axes as essential for health.

## Causal Connections

- Stress → ↑serotonin → all pituitary hormones rise EXCEPT LH → protective steroid production falls
- Serotonin → master pituitary stimulator → ↑prolactin, ↑TSH, ↑ACTH, ↑FSH, ↑LH, ↑GH → aging acceleration
- Low thyroid → TSH rises → prolactin co-rises → LH is suppressed
- LH suppression → less testosterone and progesterone production → estrogen dominance
- Estrogen → further serotonin elevation → more pituitary stress → more prolactin, more TSH
- Thyroid supplementation → TSH drops → pituitary stress decreases → LH may normalize
- Anti-serotonin interventions (cyproheptadine) → reduced pituitary stimulation → normalized hormone balance
- Pituitary removal + cortisol/thyroid replacement → doubled lifespan (animal studies)

## Sex Differences

In women, FSH and LH are conventionally used to assess menstrual cycle phase, ovarian reserve, and menopausal status. Peat's framework reinterprets high postmenopausal FSH not as the expected response to ovarian failure but as part of the serotonin-driven pituitary stress pattern that also elevates prolactin and TSH. In PCOS, the elevated LH:FSH ratio is conventionally the diagnostic marker; Peat would look upstream to the stress, estrogen, serotonin, and thyroid factors driving the pituitary response.

In men, low LH with low testosterone prompts mainstream investigation for secondary hypogonadism. Peat would assess whether the system is stressed (prolactin high, serotonin high, cortisol high) and address thyroid, diet, and protective steroid status rather than prescribing gonadotropin therapy.

## Medication Effects

- **Thyroid supplementation:** Reduces pituitary stress; TSH drops and LH may normalize as the pituitary stress burden decreases.
- **Cyproheptadine:** Anti-serotonin — by blocking serotonin's drive on all pituitary hormones, may normalize the entire pituitary axis including FSH/LH balance.
- **Clomiphene (Clomid):** Standard medical treatment to raise LH/FSH. Blocks estrogen negative feedback, artificially raising LH — a workaround rather than a systemic fix.
- **Birth control pills:** Suppress FSH/LH through exogenous estrogen/progestin. In Peat's view, this replaces the body's own regulation with synthetic stress signals.
- **Anti-serotonin agents generally:** By lowering serotonin's drive on all pituitary hormones, may normalize pituitary output including FSH/LH balance.
