# Free T4

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered T4 a precursor hormone whose blood level tells you little without knowing the T4:T3 conversion ratio and functional markers
**Companion markers:** Free T3, TSH, T4:T3 ratio, temperature, pulse, liver function, estrogen, blood glucose
**Peat's range:** Not specified as a standalone number; the T4:T3 ratio matters more (normal 3:1 to 4:1); T4 half-life is ~2 weeks so wait at least 2 weeks between dose adjustments
**Mainstream range:** 0.8-1.8 ng/dL

## Peat's Position

In Peat's framework, T4 (thyroxine) is not the active thyroid hormone — it is a precursor that must be converted to T3 in the liver and other tissues to exert metabolic effects. A "normal" or even elevated Free T4 level can coexist with severe functional hypothyroidism if the conversion to T3 is impaired. This is the central problem with T4-focused thyroid assessment: it measures the supply of raw material without measuring whether that material is being processed into its active form. Women often have above-average T4 with symptoms of hypothyroidism, because T4 is not being converted to T3. Before using a T3 supplement, it might be possible to solve the problem with diet alone: fruit or juice between meals, adequate animal protein.

Peat emphasized that the T4:T3 ratio reveals the truth that Free T4 alone conceals. In healthy thyroid glands (pig and cow), the ratio of T4 to T3 is about 3:1 to 4:1. But in hypothyroid people, the blood serum ratio can reach 50:1 or even 100:1 — meaning the body is awash in inactive T4 while starving for active T3. The factors that cause this conversion failure include estrogen excess (which blocks hepatic deiodinase), PUFA consumption (which damages conversion enzymes), liver disease, and nutrient deficiencies in selenium, zinc, and vitamin A. Free fatty acids are also a direct inhibitor of T4-to-T3 conversion.

When conversion is blocked, T4 can be shunted toward Reverse T3 instead of active T3. Reverse T3 occupies the thyroid receptor without activating it, functioning as a brake on metabolism. Reverse T3 is induced by stress, with cortisol inducing the type of deiodinase that makes inactive rT3 — a low sugar diet can cause chronically high cortisol, feeding this loop. Vitamin D deficiency specifically promotes this pathological shunting. The net result is a patient whose Free T4 looks normal or high, whose TSH may be suppressed (by the T4 feedback on the pituitary), but who is profoundly hypothyroid at the tissue level.

T4 has a half-life of about two weeks in the body, so the effect of any daily dose increases with time. It is important to stay on a certain dose for at least two or three weeks, watching effects, then adjust. In diabetes, when cells are not getting enough glucose, T4 cannot be converted to the active T3, and it can build up to levels that interfere with metabolism — but the advantage of a T4/T3 combination is that T4 inhibits TSH.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream endocrinology uses Free T4 as a primary diagnostic marker, interpreting it alongside TSH to determine thyroid status. High Free T4 with low TSH equals hyperthyroidism; low Free T4 with high TSH equals hypothyroidism. Peat argued this framework misses the most common form of thyroid failure: adequate T4 production with impaired T4-to-T3 conversion. A patient on Synthroid (T4-only) can show "perfect" labs — normal TSH, normal Free T4 — while being profoundly hypothyroid at the tissue level because the T4 is not being converted to active T3. Mainstream medicine rarely calculates the T4:T3 ratio and does not routinely test Reverse T3, leaving this conversion failure invisible. When thyroxine was declared "the thyroid hormone" and displaced desiccated thyroid, it led to two misconceptions: that only 5% of the population is thyroid-deficient, and that thermogenesis/metabolic rate are not important effects of the hormone.

## Causal Connections

- ↑Free T4 + ↓Free T3 → conversion failure → functional hypothyroidism despite "normal" labs
- ↑Estrogen → ↓hepatic deiodinase → T4 accumulates unconverted
- ↑PUFA → damaged conversion enzymes → T4 shunted to Reverse T3
- ↑Free fatty acids → direct inhibition of T4-to-T3 conversion
- Low vitamin D → T4 → Reverse T3 (instead of T3)
- ↑Cortisol (from stress or low-sugar diet) → induces rT3-producing deiodinase → T4 shunted to rT3
- T4 supplementation (Synthroid) → ↓TSH → suppressed endogenous thyroid → ↓T3 (if conversion impaired)
- ↑T4 unconverted → ↑Reverse T3 → blocks thyroid receptor → worsened hypothyroidism
- Adequate liver function + selenium + zinc + vitamin A → T4 → T3 (healthy conversion)
- Liver glycogen depletion (fasting) → ↓T4-to-T3 conversion
- Diabetes (glucose starvation) → T4 cannot convert to T3 → T4 accumulates → interferes with metabolism

## Sex Differences

Women are more susceptible to T4 conversion failure because of higher estrogen levels. Women often have above-average T4 with symptoms of hypothyroidism. Peat stated that T4-only therapy can be reasonable to try, especially in men, but since women are the main people with thyroid disease, symptoms of worsening hypothyroidism on T4 are a signal to switch to a product containing T3. The higher the estrogen burden (from endogenous production, birth control, or environmental exposure), the more likely T4-only therapy is to fail by creating a high-T4, low-T3 state. US doctors do not correct hypothyroidism because they prescribe only T4, which fails to be converted because hypothyroid women's livers are not efficient — women "treated for hypothyroidism" with T4 are still hypothyroid, and hypothyroid women are much more likely to get cancer.

## Medication Effects

- **Synthroid (T4-only):** Directly increases Free T4 and suppresses TSH, but if conversion is impaired, T4 accumulates while T3 remains low. Peat warned this can produce an "increasingly hypothyroid state" while labs appear normal. The best known brand was for a long time 30% below labeled potency, while Armour thyroid USP was reliably within 1%.
- **Cynoplus (T4+T3):** Provides T4 plus direct T3 in a 4:1 ratio, preventing the conversion-failure trap. T4 component provides TSH suppression benefit.
- **Armour Thyroid (desiccated):** Contains T4 and T3 in physiological ratio, plus minor thyroid hormones. Historically reliably within 1% of labeled potency from biological standardization.
- **Estrogen/birth control:** Impairs T4-to-T3 conversion, increasing the T4:T3 ratio and promoting functional hypothyroidism.
- **Mainstream advice to take T4 on an empty stomach:** Peat recommended the opposite — take thyroid with food, particularly sugar or fruit juice, because liver glycogen supports T4-to-T3 conversion and prevents adrenaline spikes.
