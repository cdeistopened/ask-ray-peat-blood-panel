# DHEA

**Reliability:** Tier 2 -- Peat considered DHEA useful to test, especially as a marker of overall protective steroid status. DHEA-S (sulfated form) is the standard lab test and reflects adrenal production. The caveat is that brain DHEA is ~10x blood levels.
**Companion markers:** Pregnenolone, testosterone, cortisol, thyroid, estrogen, temperature
**Peat's range:** No specific serum target. ~2% annual decline after mid-twenties is the expected trajectory. Deficiency common by age 40. Supplement: 10 mg safe for men; women should prefer pregnenolone.
**Mainstream range:** DHEA-S: 280-640 mcg/dL (men 20-29), declining with age; 65-380 mcg/dL (women 20-29)

## Peat's Position

DHEA is the most abundant steroid hormone in the body, and its decline with aging -- roughly 2% per year after the mid-twenties -- parallels the broader pattern of protective hormone loss that Peat associated with metabolic deterioration. By the forties, it is very common for people to become deficient in both pregnenolone and DHEA, usually because of an imbalance between thyroid and estrogen. Low thyroid impairs cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, and since DHEA is downstream of pregnenolone, the entire cascade suffers.

DHEA serves multiple protective functions. It is anabolic for muscles and activates heat production -- it causes the body to "waste" heat, but this thermogenesis keeps nerve function up and inflammation down. DHEA is also an activator of fat oxidation specifically in muscle tissue. Everything that increases muscle testosterone and DHEA while lowering cortisol favors safe fat burning in muscles. Together with testosterone, DHEA protects the heart, lungs, and brain from cortisol-driven catabolism by concentrating in those vital organs.

Like testosterone, DHEA can be converted to estrogen through aromatase. Anything causing stress, irritation, or inflammation activates this conversion, turning a protective hormone into a harmful one. This is why Peat's supplementation hierarchy places pregnenolone first -- it converts to either progesterone or DHEA according to need, and the body's own regulation determines direction.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine largely dismisses DHEA supplementation, considering it unproven or risky. Some anti-aging medicine embraces it but treats it as a standalone supplement without Peat's context of the steroid cascade. Peat's view differs from both: DHEA is genuinely protective but must be understood within the hierarchy (pregnenolone first, then DHEA, then testosterone) and within the metabolic context (thyroid must be adequate, or DHEA may aromatize to estrogen). The mainstream tendency to test DHEA-S as an isolated value misses the critical ratio relationships with cortisol, estrogen, and thyroid that determine whether DHEA is functioning protectively or being converted to estrogen.

## Causal Connections

- Low thyroid -> impaired pregnenolone synthesis -> DHEA deficiency (common by age 40)
- DHEA -> muscle anabolism + thermogenesis -> anti-inflammatory
- DHEA -> fat oxidation in muscle -> body composition improvement
- DHEA + testosterone -> protect heart, lungs, brain from cortisol catabolism
- Stress/inflammation -> aromatase activation -> DHEA converted to estrogen
- Pregnenolone -> converts to DHEA (or progesterone) according to body's need
- DHEA -> promotes restorative sleep (alongside progesterone and testosterone)
- Cortisol opposes DHEA: high cortisol = low DHEA (both compete for pregnenolone)

## Sex Differences

For men, 10 mg of DHEA is "pretty safe" and converts to testosterone. For women, DHEA can have masculinizing effects (acne, facial hair, deepened voice) because it converts to testosterone. Peat recommended that women use pregnenolone instead, letting the body regulate how much becomes DHEA vs. progesterone. If a woman does use DHEA, the dose should be lower than for men, and thyroid function must be adequate to prevent aromatase-driven conversion to estrogen.

Both sexes experience the ~2% annual decline, but women's DHEA decline is complicated by the simultaneous decline in progesterone around age 40, creating a double vulnerability to estrogen dominance.

## Medication Effects

- **DHEA supplementation:** 10 mg safe for men. Women should use pregnenolone instead due to masculinizing risk. Always ensure thyroid is adequate first.
- **Pregnenolone:** Preferred upstream precursor. 100 mg pregnenolone + 5 mg DHEA for recovery after exogenous testosterone.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Required for DHEA production. Without adequate thyroid, DHEA synthesis is impaired at the pregnenolone step.
- **Statins/low-cholesterol diets:** Lower cholesterol, cutting off raw material for the entire steroid cascade including DHEA.
- **SSRIs:** Raise serotonin, which activates cortisol and shifts production away from DHEA.
- **Cortisol (exogenous or endogenous excess):** Competes with DHEA for pregnenolone substrate. Chronic stress depletes DHEA.
