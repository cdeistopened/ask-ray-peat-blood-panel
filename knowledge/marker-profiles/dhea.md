# DHEA

**Reliability:** Tier 2 -- Peat considered DHEA useful to test, especially as a marker of overall protective steroid status. DHEA-S (sulfated form) is the standard lab test and reflects adrenal production. The caveat is that brain DHEA is ~10x blood levels.
**Companion markers:** Pregnenolone, testosterone, cortisol, thyroid, estrogen, temperature
**Peat's range:** No specific serum target. Peak production: 12-15 mg/day in teens, declining to near-zero by age 90. Supplement: 5-10 mg safe for middle-aged men; women should prefer pregnenolone. Use 2:1 pregnenolone:DHEA ratio to mitigate estrogen conversion risk.
**Mainstream range:** DHEA-S: 280-640 mcg/dL (men 20-29), declining with age; 65-380 mcg/dL (women 20-29)

## Peat's Position

DHEA is the most abundant steroid hormone in the body, and its decline with aging -- roughly 2% per year after the mid-twenties -- parallels the broader pattern of protective hormone loss that Peat associated with metabolic deterioration. Production peaks at 12-15 mg/day in the teens and decreases to nearly zero by age 90. By the forties, it is very common for people to become deficient in both pregnenolone and DHEA, usually because of an imbalance between thyroid and estrogen. Low thyroid impairs cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, and since DHEA is downstream of pregnenolone, the entire cascade suffers.

DHEA serves multiple protective functions. It is anabolic for muscles and activates heat production -- it causes the body to "waste" heat, but this thermogenesis keeps nerve function up and inflammation down. DHEA is also an activator of fat oxidation specifically in muscle tissue. Everything that increases muscle testosterone and DHEA while lowering cortisol favors safe fat burning in muscles. Together with testosterone, DHEA protects the heart, lungs, and brain from cortisol-driven catabolism by concentrating in those vital organs.

Like testosterone, DHEA can be converted to estrogen through aromatase. Anything causing stress, irritation, or inflammation activates this conversion, turning a protective hormone into a harmful one. If thyroid is very low, be cautious with DHEA because stress hormones can cause it to convert to estrogen. A 2:1 ratio of pregnenolone to DHEA helps mitigate this risk — pregnenolone protects against the stress-driven aromatization. Thyroid and aspirin also help prevent the conversion of DHEA to estrogen by reducing inflammation that drives aromatase.

Applied topically in vitamin E, DHEA caused a large, growing mole to rapidly shrink and resolve within days. A large red keloid tumor also quickly faded with topical DHEA in vitamin E. Copper, vitamin A, pregnenolone, progesterone, vitamin E, and DHEA are all intimately involved with both respiration and pigment metabolism — skin changes in aging may reflect steroid deficiency, making topical application especially appropriate.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine largely dismisses DHEA supplementation, considering it unproven or risky. Some anti-aging medicine embraces it but treats it as a standalone supplement without Peat's context of the steroid cascade. Peat's view differs from both: DHEA is genuinely protective but must be understood within the hierarchy (pregnenolone first, then DHEA, then testosterone) and within the metabolic context (thyroid must be adequate, or DHEA may aromatize to estrogen). The mainstream tendency to test DHEA-S as an isolated value misses the critical ratio relationships with cortisol, estrogen, and thyroid that determine whether DHEA is functioning protectively or being converted to estrogen. Mainstream supplement doses (25-50 mg) are much higher than Peat recommended (5-10 mg).

## Causal Connections

- Low thyroid -> impaired pregnenolone synthesis -> DHEA deficiency (common by age 40)
- DHEA -> muscle anabolism + thermogenesis -> anti-inflammatory
- DHEA -> fat oxidation in muscle -> body composition improvement
- DHEA + testosterone -> protect heart, lungs, brain from cortisol catabolism
- Stress/inflammation -> aromatase activation -> DHEA converted to estrogen (especially with low thyroid)
- Pregnenolone -> converts to DHEA (or progesterone) according to body's need
- 2:1 pregnenolone:DHEA ratio -> protects against stress-driven aromatization
- Thyroid + aspirin -> anti-inflammatory -> prevent DHEA-to-estrogen conversion
- DHEA -> promotes restorative sleep (alongside progesterone and testosterone)
- Cortisol opposes DHEA: high cortisol = low DHEA (both compete for pregnenolone)
- Topical DHEA in vitamin E -> local tissue normalization (moles, keloids)
- DHEA + copper + vitamin A + vitamin E -> respiration + pigment metabolism in skin

## Sex Differences

For men, 5-10 mg of DHEA is safe and converts to testosterone. 5 mg with olive oil or butter can have a noticeable effect on mood and muscle tone in a few hours. For women, DHEA can have masculinizing effects (acne, facial hair, deepened voice) because it converts to testosterone. Peat recommended that women use pregnenolone instead, letting the body regulate how much becomes DHEA vs. progesterone. If a woman does use DHEA, the dose should be lower than for men, and thyroid function must be adequate to prevent aromatase-driven conversion to estrogen.

Both sexes experience the ~2% annual decline, but women's DHEA decline is complicated by the simultaneous decline in progesterone around age 40, creating a double vulnerability to estrogen dominance.

## Medication Effects

- **DHEA supplementation:** 5-10 mg safe for middle-aged men, taken with olive oil or butter for absorption. Women should use pregnenolone instead due to masculinizing risk. Always ensure thyroid is adequate first. Use 2:1 pregnenolone:DHEA ratio to protect against estrogen conversion.
- **Pregnenolone:** Preferred upstream precursor. 100 mg pregnenolone + 5 mg DHEA for recovery after exogenous testosterone.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Required for DHEA production. Without adequate thyroid, DHEA synthesis is impaired at the pregnenolone step. Also prevents DHEA-to-estrogen conversion.
- **Aspirin:** Anti-inflammatory, helps prevent DHEA-to-estrogen conversion by suppressing aromatase.
- **Topical DHEA in vitamin E:** For skin lesions (moles, keloids). Promotes local immune response and tissue normalization.
- **Statins/low-cholesterol diets:** Lower cholesterol, cutting off raw material for the entire steroid cascade including DHEA.
- **SSRIs:** Raise serotonin, which activates cortisol and shifts production away from DHEA.
- **Cortisol (exogenous or endogenous excess):** Competes with DHEA for pregnenolone substrate. Chronic stress depletes DHEA.
