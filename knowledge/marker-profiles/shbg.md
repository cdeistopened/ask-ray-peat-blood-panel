# SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin)

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat found SHBG useful as context for interpreting testosterone and estrogen, but challenged the fundamental concept of "free" vs. bound hormones that gives SHBG its clinical importance. Enrichment confirms SHBG must be interpreted alongside cortisol and estrogen for male hormonal assessment.
**Companion markers:** Estrogen, testosterone, cortisol, albumin, breast cancer risk markers, vitamin D
**Peat's range:** Not specified. Context-dependent: high SHBG is protective against estrogen (lowers breast cancer risk) but may limit testosterone availability. For men, testosterone must be interpreted in relation to cortisol, estrogen, and SHBG together.
**Mainstream range:** Men: 10-57 nmol/L; Women: 18-144 nmol/L

## Peat's Position

Peat's view of SHBG was nuanced and somewhat contrarian. He identified SHBG as "the body's defense against estrogen" — when SHBG is high, a woman has lower breast cancer risk because SHBG keeps estrogen out of cells. This protective function means high SHBG should not automatically be treated as a problem, even though it also limits testosterone availability.

However, Peat questioned the deeper premise that gives SHBG its clinical significance: the distinction between "free" and "bound" hormones. Several experiments have shown that hormones covalently attached to albumin — so they cannot get loose — still work, entering cells effectively. This means the clinical construct of "free testosterone" (unbound to SHBG or albumin) may not reflect biological reality. What is called "protein-bound" hormone may be fully functional. Peat also noted that progesterone is very hard to dissolve in water, raising questions about what saliva tests actually measure as "free" progesterone. If the fundamental premise of the free hormone hypothesis is wrong, then SHBG's clinical interpretation needs rethinking.

The enrichment data adds specificity to SHBG's role in male hormonal interpretation. Peat stated that testosterone must be interpreted in relation to cortisol, estrogen, and SHBG together. For a 35-year-old male, the ideal is cortisol no higher than mid-range, estrogen below the middle, and testosterone above the middle. A vitamin D supplement often helps improve this hormone balance. This means SHBG cannot be interpreted in isolation — it must be read as part of the cortisol-estrogen-testosterone triad where the ratios between protective and catabolic hormones determine health, not absolute values.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats SHBG as a modulator that determines how much "free" (bioavailable) testosterone and estrogen are circulating. Low SHBG is associated with insulin resistance, PCOS, and metabolic syndrome; high SHBG is associated with hyperthyroidism, anorexia, and estrogen use. The standard approach is to calculate "free testosterone" based on total testosterone and SHBG.

Peat challenged this framework at its foundation: if albumin-bound hormones are biologically active (which experiments show they are), then the entire free-hormone construct overstates SHBG's functional importance. Additionally, mainstream treats high SHBG in men as a problem (limiting testosterone); Peat would weigh this against its anti-estrogenic protection. The clinical picture depends on which effect matters more for the individual patient — and critically, on the broader cortisol-estrogen-testosterone context.

## Causal Connections

- Estrogen excess → liver produces more SHBG (compensatory defense)
- High SHBG → binds estrogen (protective: lower breast cancer risk)
- High SHBG → binds testosterone (potentially limiting: reduced androgen activity)
- Hypothyroidism → lower SHBG production
- Hyperthyroidism → higher SHBG production
- Insulin resistance → lower SHBG
- SHBG interpretation depends on whether "free hormone" theory is valid (Peat questions it)
- Testosterone must be interpreted alongside cortisol, estrogen, and SHBG — ratios matter more than absolutes
- Vitamin D supplementation → improves overall hormone balance including testosterone-SHBG relationship

## Sex Differences

In women, high SHBG is protective against breast cancer by sequestering estrogen. In the context of estrogen dominance, rising SHBG is the body's attempt to buffer excess estrogen. Low SHBG in women (associated with PCOS, insulin resistance) means more free estrogen reaching tissues — a metabolically dangerous state.

In men, low SHBG is conventionally associated with higher "free" testosterone (seemingly good) but also with metabolic syndrome (bad). High SHBG in men is conventionally treated as limiting testosterone action. In Peat's framework, this needs to be weighed against the estrogen-binding benefit, especially in older men with rising estrogen. The ideal for a 35-year-old male is cortisol at or below mid-range, estrogen below mid-range, and testosterone above mid-range — with SHBG as a modulating variable.

## Medication Effects

- **Thyroid supplementation:** Increases SHBG production by the liver. This is generally protective (more estrogen binding) but may concern conventional practitioners who see reduced "free" testosterone.
- **Birth control pills:** Estrogen component increases SHBG dramatically. This is one mechanism by which oral contraceptives reduce "free" testosterone, contributing to low libido.
- **Testosterone replacement:** May lower SHBG, increasing the ratio of free estrogen — potentially counterproductive.
- **Insulin-sensitizing drugs (metformin):** May increase SHBG by improving insulin resistance.
- **Vitamin D:** Helps improve the overall hormone balance, potentially normalizing SHBG in context.
