# Pregnenolone

**Reliability:** Tier 2 -- Peat considered pregnenolone useful to test as a marker of the body's steroid-synthetic capacity. The primary caveat: brain concentrations are about 10x blood levels, so serum testing underrepresents neurological status. Deficiency is common by the forties.
**Companion markers:** Cholesterol (raw material), thyroid/TSH (drives conversion), Vitamin A (cofactor), progesterone, DHEA, cortisol, all downstream steroids
**Peat's range:** Serum: 10-200 ng/dL (mainstream). No specific Peat target, but declining levels after mid-twenties signal metabolic deterioration. Supplement: 10-50 mg general support; 100 mg + 5 mg DHEA for recovery after exogenous testosterone.
**Mainstream range:** 10-200 ng/dL (serum); rarely tested in routine panels

## Peat's Position

Pregnenolone is the "mother of all steroid hormones" -- the first compound synthesized from cholesterol, and the precursor to every protective steroid in the body: progesterone, DHEA, testosterone, cortisol, aldosterone. Its production requires three inputs working together: cholesterol (the raw material), thyroid hormone (the metabolic driver), and Vitamin A (the cofactor). When any one of these three is deficient, pregnenolone synthesis collapses and all downstream steroids fall with it. This three-input requirement makes pregnenolone a sensitive indicator of overall metabolic health.

The brain is one of the biggest steroid-forming glands. Brain concentrations of pregnenolone, DHEA, and progesterone are about 10 times higher than blood levels. This means serum pregnenolone underrepresents the brain's most important neuroprotective steroid by an order of magnitude. Peat considered pregnenolone essential for brain function -- it stabilizes neurons, protects against excitotoxicity, and supports memory. The neurological consequences of low pregnenolone (brain fog, memory impairment, anxiety, depression) can be severe even when serum levels appear borderline.

Pregnenolone converts to either progesterone or DHEA according to the body's need. This makes it the safest first-line steroid supplement because it allows the body's own regulatory mechanisms to direct production to the most deficient pathway. Unlike supplementing a downstream hormone (testosterone, cortisol, estrogen) which bypasses the body's regulation, pregnenolone feeds the top of the cascade and lets the body decide. "It turns into either [progesterone or DHEA], according to need."

Lipschutz called pregnenolone and progesterone the "anti-fibromatogenic steroids" -- substances that inhibit fibrous overproduction and protect against tumors. This anti-cancer property reflects pregnenolone's fundamental role in opposing the estrogen-driven proliferative state that Peat associated with aging, cancer, and degenerative disease.

It is very common for people in their forties to become deficient in both pregnenolone and DHEA, usually because of an imbalance of thyroid and estrogen. Low thyroid impairs the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone, and rising estrogen further blocks steroidogenesis. This creates a vicious cycle: low pregnenolone means less progesterone to oppose estrogen, which allows estrogen to further suppress pregnenolone synthesis.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine rarely tests pregnenolone and does not consider it a standard marker. Anti-aging medicine has embraced pregnenolone supplementation but often treats it as a standalone supplement without understanding the three-input requirement (cholesterol + thyroid + Vitamin A) that governs its production. Functional medicine practitioners may test pregnenolone but typically interpret it in isolation rather than as a window into the entire steroid cascade.

Peat's key inversion: high cholesterol is not a disease requiring statins -- it is often a symptom of impaired pregnenolone synthesis. When thyroid is low or Vitamin A is deficient, cholesterol accumulates because it cannot be converted to pregnenolone and downstream steroids. The body compensates by increasing cholesterol production. Treating with statins removes the raw material for the entire protective steroid cascade, worsening the underlying problem. "When you force your cholesterol down, you're unable to make progesterone, pregnenolone, DHEA, and the whole range of protective steroids." The correct intervention is thyroid support and Vitamin A, not cholesterol-lowering drugs.

The concept of "pregnenolone steal" (popular in functional medicine) -- where the body preferentially converts pregnenolone to cortisol under stress, depleting progesterone -- is partially correct in Peat's framework but misnamed. The problem is not "stealing" from one pathway to another; the problem is inadequate total pregnenolone production due to insufficient thyroid, cholesterol, or Vitamin A. "Thyroid hormone, vitamin A, and cholesterol support the formation of pregnenolone, and the well nourished body is able to make large adjustments in these, to minimize the need for cortisol."

## Causal Connections

- Low thyroid -> impaired cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion -> all downstream steroids decline
- Low Vitamin A -> impaired pregnenolone synthesis (cofactor requirement)
- Low cholesterol (statins, low-fat diet) -> insufficient raw material -> pregnenolone collapse -> brain suffers most
- Nitric oxide (chronic inflammation) -> directly turns off pregnenolone synthesis -> entire protective steroid cascade collapses
- High cholesterol + low temperature -> diagnostic pattern: cholesterol accumulating because it is not being converted to pregnenolone
- Pregnenolone -> progesterone (OR) DHEA -> body's regulatory mechanisms determine direction based on need
- Low pregnenolone -> low progesterone -> unopposed estrogen -> estrogen further suppresses steroidogenesis -> vicious cycle
- Low pregnenolone -> low DHEA -> reduced muscle anabolism, thermogenesis, and fat oxidation
- Low pregnenolone -> brain concentrations fall (10x blood levels) -> cognitive impairment, memory loss, anxiety
- Pregnenolone + progesterone = "anti-fibromatogenic steroids" -> cancer protection
- Cortisol tests help gauge pregnenolone interventions: declining cortisol while symptoms improve = successful upstream support

## Sex Differences

In women, pregnenolone's primary downstream product is progesterone, making pregnenolone deficiency particularly dangerous because it leaves estrogen unopposed. The estrogen-to-progesterone ratio peaks around age 40 -- precisely when pregnenolone deficiency becomes common. Pregnenolone supplementation in women is preferred over DHEA because DHEA can have masculinizing effects (acne, facial hair) by converting to testosterone.

In men, pregnenolone converts preferentially to DHEA and then to testosterone. Peat recommended trying pregnenolone first, then DHEA, before direct testosterone supplementation. "Pure testosterone on the skin is safe if the diet and thyroid function are good, but it's better to try supplements of pregnenolone first, and then DHEA." This upstream-first approach allows the body to produce what it needs without the aromatization risk of direct testosterone supplementation. If a man supplements testosterone daily, the liver adapts over 1-2 weeks by upregulating testosterone-destroying enzymes, and at medical doses (50-100 mg), the liver may convert as much testosterone to estrogen as it preserves.

For both sexes, the underlying principle is identical: fix the upstream problem (thyroid, cholesterol, Vitamin A), supplement the upstream steroid (pregnenolone), and let the body regulate downstream production.

## Medication Effects

- **Pregnenolone supplementation:** 10-50 mg for general support. 100 mg + 5 mg DHEA for testicular recovery after exogenous testosterone. "Pregnenolone can always be used with thyroid to guarantee an easy adrenal response." The safety margin is enormous -- "the more pregnenolone or progesterone you take, the more normal you become."
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Required for pregnenolone production. Without adequate thyroid, supplemental cholesterol and Vitamin A cannot be converted. Fix thyroid FIRST, then assess pregnenolone.
- **Vitamin A:** Essential cofactor. Must be preformed retinol (liver, eggs, dairy), not beta-carotene, because hypothyroid individuals cannot convert carotene to Vitamin A.
- **Statins:** Directly reduce the raw material for pregnenolone synthesis. In Peat's framework, statins are among the most harmful drug classes because they collapse the entire protective steroid cascade. Brain suffers most due to its high steroid requirement.
- **Low-fat diets:** Same mechanism as statins -- inadequate cholesterol supply for steroid synthesis. Cholesterol below ~100 requires caution before starting thyroid because thyroid will further deplete cholesterol by converting it to steroids.
- **Cortisol (endogenous excess):** When metabolism falters, production shifts from progesterone toward cortisol. This is not "pregnenolone steal" but inadequate total production. Supplementing pregnenolone restores the balance by increasing total substrate.
- **SSRIs / Serotonin:** Serotonin is one of the most powerful activators of the cortisol stress system. By driving cortisol demand, chronic serotonin excess indirectly depletes pregnenolone.
- **Nitric oxide donors:** NO directly inhibits pregnenolone synthesis. Chronic inflammation (which produces NO) actively shuts down the beginning of the steroid cascade.
- **Progesterone supplementation:** When pregnenolone alone is insufficient, direct progesterone can be added. For women: 10-30 mg general, 100 mg for acute migraine. For men: 5-10 mg (higher doses may antagonize testosterone). Use cyclically in women -- two weeks on, two weeks off -- because continuous use induces hepatic clearance enzymes.
