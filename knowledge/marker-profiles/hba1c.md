# HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)

**Reliability:** Tier 4 — Peat explicitly distrusted HbA1c because the glycation blamed on glucose is actually caused more powerfully by PUFA peroxidation products. Enrichment adds that oral contraceptives mask metabolic disruption by not changing HbA1c despite causing insulin resistance.
**Companion markers:** PUFA intake, lipid peroxidation markers, fasting glucose, free fatty acids, dietary fat composition, oral contraceptive use
**Peat's range:** Not specified
**Mainstream range:** <5.7% (normal), 5.7-6.4% (prediabetes), >6.5% (diabetes)

## Peat's Position

Peat fundamentally questioned the premise of HbA1c as a glucose-damage marker. The glycated proteins (advanced glycation end products, or AGEs) typically blamed on high blood sugar in diabetics are actually caused more powerfully by polyunsaturated fatty acids than by glucose itself. PUFA peroxidation products — particularly reactive aldehydes like malondialdehyde — are more potent glycating agents than glucose. This means that an elevated HbA1c may reflect high PUFA exposure and lipid peroxidation rather than chronic hyperglycemia, fundamentally undermining the test's assumed mechanism.

This finding fits within Peat's broader argument that diabetes is not primarily a disease of glucose excess but of impaired glucose oxidation caused by PUFA-driven Randle cycle activation. If the tissue damage attributed to "high blood sugar" is actually caused by PUFA peroxidation, then the entire diagnostic and therapeutic framework built on HbA1c — including the progressive lowering of diagnostic thresholds to capture more patients — is addressing the wrong variable. The correct intervention would be reducing PUFA intake, not reducing glucose.

The enrichment data adds a specific clinical example demonstrating HbA1c's unreliability. Oral contraceptive users had higher 2-hour plasma glucose, higher fasting plasma insulin, and higher triglycerides — clear markers of insulin resistance and metabolic disruption — yet their fasting plasma glucose and glycated hemoglobin A1c "did not significantly differ" from controls. This means HbA1c missed the metabolic damage caused by oral contraceptives entirely, providing false reassurance while actual insulin resistance progressed silently. This is precisely the kind of diagnostic failure Peat predicted: HbA1c measures a secondary variable (glycation) while the primary metabolic disruption (insulin resistance from estrogen-driven fatty acid changes) goes undetected.

Peat also noted that when asked if A1c is a reliable metric for sugar intake, his response focused on iron and hemoglobin rather than A1c's validity — suggesting he considered the test's premise too flawed to address as a glucose metric.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats HbA1c as the gold standard for long-term glycemic control, interpreting elevated levels as proof of chronic hyperglycemia causing tissue damage through protein glycation. Peat inverted this by arguing that PUFA peroxidation products are more potent glycating agents than glucose, meaning the test may primarily reflect PUFA toxicity rather than glucose damage. An elevated HbA1c in a person consuming seed oils may tell you more about their fat intake than their sugar metabolism. The oral contraceptive data demonstrates the inverse failure: real metabolic damage (insulin resistance, elevated triglycerides) that HbA1c completely misses.

The mainstream therapeutic response to high HbA1c — glucose restriction and insulin sensitization — may miss the actual cause of the glycation if PUFA peroxidation is the dominant glycating agent. And the reassuring "normal" HbA1c in patients on estrogen may mask genuine metabolic damage.

## Causal Connections

- PUFA intake → lipid peroxidation → reactive aldehydes (malondialdehyde) → protein glycation → ↑HbA1c
- Glucose → protein glycation (slower, less potent than PUFA-derived aldehydes)
- PUFA → Randle cycle activation → impaired glucose oxidation → ↑blood sugar → secondary glucose glycation
- ↓PUFA intake → ↓lipid peroxidation → ↓glycation → potentially ↓HbA1c (independent of glucose changes)
- Oral contraceptives → ↑insulin resistance + ↑triglycerides + ↑2-hour glucose BUT unchanged HbA1c → false reassurance
- Progressive threshold lowering → expanded diabetic population → expanded pharmaceutical market

## Sex Differences

The oral contraceptive study demonstrates a sex-specific limitation of HbA1c: women on hormonal contraception may have normal HbA1c despite active insulin resistance and metabolic disruption. This makes HbA1c particularly unreliable in premenopausal women using oral contraceptives.

## Medication Effects

- **Metformin/insulin:** Standard HbA1c-lowering drugs. Peat's framework suggests these address a secondary variable (glucose) while the primary glycating agent (PUFA peroxidation products) remains unaddressed.
- **Oral contraceptives:** Cause insulin resistance and metabolic disruption that HbA1c fails to detect. Normal HbA1c provides false reassurance.
- **Dietary PUFA reduction:** In Peat's framework, reducing seed oil intake would address the primary cause of protein glycation more effectively than glucose-lowering drugs.
- **Aspirin:** Blocks inflammatory prostaglandins from PUFA, potentially reducing the peroxidation that drives glycation.
- **Niacinamide:** Lowers free fatty acids, reducing the substrate for peroxidation-driven glycation.
