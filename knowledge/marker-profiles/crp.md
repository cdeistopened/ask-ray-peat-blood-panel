# CRP (C-Reactive Protein)

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat recognized CRP as a useful positive acute phase protein but considered it secondary to albumin; its real value is confirming the acute phase pattern when paired with albumin and ferritin
**Companion markers:** Albumin (inverse — the most important companion), ferritin (co-elevated), fibrinogen (co-elevated), serum amyloid, thyroid, endotoxin markers
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically. Very low CRP is cancer-protective (naked mole rats have extremely low CRP and never develop cancer). Should always be interpreted alongside albumin.
**Mainstream range:** <1.0 mg/L (low cardiovascular risk); 1.0-3.0 mg/L (moderate risk); >3.0 mg/L (high risk)

## Peat's Position

CRP occupies a specific place in Peat's framework as one member of the acute phase protein triad — CRP, ferritin, and fibrinogen all rise during inflammation as the liver redirects protein synthesis from maintenance proteins (albumin) to emergency inflammatory proteins. This reciprocal relationship is the key interpretive insight: CRP rising while albumin falls confirms that the liver is locked in acute phase mode, diverting its protein-making capacity from albumin (which supports transport, binding, and oncotic pressure) toward inflammatory markers.

Peat went further than mainstream medicine in identifying the most common driver of this chronic acute phase state. He argued that endotoxin absorbed from bowel bacteria is "probably the commonest reason for increased production of these proteins," and suggested they might better be called "chronic inflammation proteins" rather than "acute phase proteins," since they are associated not with acute infection but with the degenerative diseases — diabetes, cancer, and heart disease. This reframes CRP elevation from a cardiovascular risk marker to a marker of chronic endotoxin-driven hepatic inflammation.

The naked mole rat observation provides a striking natural experiment. These animals have never been known to develop cancer, and their serum CRP is extremely low, indicating resistance to inflammation. In Peat's framework, this is not coincidental — cancer immunity reflects a metabolic and inflammatory environment that never permits the respiratory failure that cancer requires. Very low CRP therefore signals an organism whose inflammatory machinery is not chronically activated, and whose liver is dedicating its synthetic capacity to protective proteins like albumin rather than defensive ones like CRP.

The practical value of CRP in Peat's framework is as confirmation: if albumin is low-normal and CRP is elevated, the body is in a chronic inflammatory state even if other markers appear unremarkable. Albumin is Peat's preferred single marker of metabolic vitality, but CRP provides the inflammatory side of the same picture.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine uses high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) as a cardiovascular risk marker, with values above 3.0 mg/L indicating high risk. This is broadly consistent with Peat's view that elevated CRP reflects systemic inflammation damaging the cardiovascular system. However, the divergence is in etiology and response. Mainstream medicine treats CRP as an independent risk factor to be managed pharmacologically — statins lower CRP, which is cited as part of their benefit. Peat would interpret CRP elevation as part of a broader metabolic picture involving endotoxin, estrogen, thyroid, and PUFA, and would object to lowering CRP with statins because statins also deplete cholesterol needed for protective steroid synthesis.

Mainstream medicine does not routinely pair CRP with albumin in outpatient settings, despite this pairing being recognized in critical care (the Glasgow Prognostic Score). Peat's framework treats the CRP-albumin inverse relationship as the standard interpretive lens. Additionally, Peat's identification of gut-derived endotoxin as the primary driver of chronic CRP elevation shifts the therapeutic focus entirely: instead of statins or anti-inflammatories, the intervention is reducing endotoxin absorption through raw carrot salad, adequate fruit sugar for liver energy, and thyroid support.

## Causal Connections

- Chronic endotoxin absorption → liver in perpetual acute-phase mode → ↑CRP, ↑ferritin, ↑fibrinogen, ↓albumin
- CRP + ferritin + fibrinogen + serum amyloid rise together as positive acute phase proteins
- Estrogen → reduces albumin formation → worsens the CRP-albumin imbalance
- Hypothyroidism → liver energy deficit → impaired albumin synthesis → CRP appears relatively more elevated
- Chronic stress → sustained acute phase response → chronically elevated CRP
- COVID-19 → severe acute phase response → CRP + ferritin + D-dimer elevation (iron dysregulation, not virus directly)
- Very low CRP (naked mole rat model) → cancer-protective metabolic state
- Improving endotoxin status (raw carrot, fruit juice, thyroid) → liver exits acute phase → albumin rises, CRP falls

## Sex Differences

Women's higher baseline estrogen promotes more inflammation through aromatase activation, iron retention, and albumin suppression, which would be expected to elevate CRP. Postmenopausal women on estrogen replacement therapy would have particularly elevated CRP due to estrogen's effect on hepatic acute phase protein synthesis. Men's progressive iron accumulation also drives chronic inflammation reflected in CRP, but through a different mechanism (iron-catalyzed lipid peroxidation rather than estrogen-driven acute phase activation).

## Medication Effects

- **Statins:** Lower CRP (mainstream rationale for their cardiovascular benefit), but also lower cholesterol needed for protective steroid synthesis. Peat would consider this addressing a downstream marker while worsening upstream metabolic capacity.
- **Aspirin:** Anti-inflammatory; lowers prostaglandin-driven inflammation that contributes to CRP elevation. Also reduces endotoxin damage directly.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Supports liver energy and albumin production; may normalize the CRP-albumin relationship by allowing the liver to exit acute-phase mode.
- **Raw carrot salad / fruit juice:** Reduce endotoxin absorption (carrot fiber binds endotoxin; fructose provides liver energy), allowing the liver to shift from acute phase defense to normal albumin synthesis.
- **Niacinamide:** Lowers free fatty acids, reducing inflammatory substrate. Supports liver function.
