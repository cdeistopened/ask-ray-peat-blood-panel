# CRP (C-Reactive Protein)

**Reliability:** Tier 2 -- Peat did not extensively discuss CRP as a standalone marker, but recognized it as a useful positive acute phase protein. Its value comes from interpreting it alongside albumin (inverse relationship) and ferritin (co-elevation). CRP tells you the liver is in inflammatory mode.
**Companion markers:** Albumin (inverse -- the most important companion), ferritin (co-elevated), fibrinogen (co-elevated), thyroid, liver function
**Peat's range:** Not specified. Lower is better. Should be interpreted alongside albumin -- CRP rising while albumin falls confirms the acute phase inflammatory response.
**Mainstream range:** <1.0 mg/L (low cardiovascular risk); 1.0-3.0 mg/L (moderate risk); >3.0 mg/L (high risk)

## Peat's Position

CRP appears in Peat's framework primarily as a member of the acute phase protein triad: CRP, ferritin, and fibrinogen all rise during inflammation as the liver redirects protein synthesis from maintenance proteins (albumin) to emergency inflammatory proteins. This reciprocal relationship is the key insight -- CRP and albumin should always be interpreted together. CRP rising while albumin falls confirms that the liver is locked in acute phase mode, diverting its protein-making capacity from albumin (which supports transport, binding, and oncotic pressure) toward inflammatory markers.

While Peat did not devote extensive attention to CRP as an isolated marker (he was more interested in albumin as the functional inflammation indicator), its elevation was part of the inflammatory cascade he described in conditions ranging from chronic stress to COVID-19. In severe COVID, elevated inflammatory markers including CRP and ferritin correlated with poor outcomes -- but Peat emphasized that the underlying mechanism was iron dysregulation, clotting activation (D-dimer), and inflammatory cascades rather than the virus itself.

The practical value of CRP in Peat's framework is as confirmation: if albumin is low-normal and CRP is elevated, the body is in a chronic inflammatory state even if other markers appear unremarkable. Albumin is Peat's preferred single marker of metabolic vitality, but CRP provides the inflammatory side of the same picture.

## Key Quotes

> "Albumin is classified as a negative acute phase protein -- its synthesis decreases during inflammation while positive acute phase proteins (C-reactive protein, ferritin, fibrinogen) increase." -- Albumin wiki article

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine uses high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) as a cardiovascular risk marker, with values >3.0 mg/L indicating high risk. This is broadly consistent with Peat's view -- elevated CRP reflects systemic inflammation that damages the cardiovascular system. However, Peat would interpret CRP elevation as part of a broader metabolic picture involving estrogen, thyroid, endotoxin, and PUFA rather than treating it as an independent cardiovascular risk factor to be managed with statins (which lower CRP but also deplete cholesterol needed for protective steroid synthesis).

Mainstream does not routinely pair CRP with albumin as Peat's framework suggests. The CRP-albumin inverse relationship, while recognized in critical care medicine (Glasgow Prognostic Score), is not widely used in outpatient medicine. Peat would argue it should be standard.

## Causal Connections

- Inflammation -> liver redirects protein synthesis -> CRP rises, albumin falls
- CRP + ferritin + fibrinogen rise together as positive acute phase proteins
- Endotoxin -> liver stress -> acute phase response -> CRP elevation
- Estrogen -> reduces albumin formation -> worsens the CRP-albumin imbalance
- Hypothyroidism -> liver energy deficit -> impaired albumin synthesis -> CRP may appear relatively more elevated
- Chronic stress -> sustained acute phase response -> chronically elevated CRP
- COVID-19 -> severe acute phase response -> CRP + ferritin + D-dimer elevation
- Improving endotoxin status (raw carrot, fruit juice) -> liver exits acute phase -> albumin rises, CRP falls

## Sex Differences

The extractions contain limited sex-specific information about CRP. In general, women's higher baseline estrogen promotes more inflammation (through aromatase activation, iron retention, and albumin suppression), which would be expected to elevate CRP. Postmenopausal women on estrogen replacement therapy would have particularly elevated CRP due to estrogen's effect on hepatic acute phase protein synthesis. Men's progressive iron accumulation also drives chronic inflammation reflected in CRP.

## Medication Effects

- **Statins:** Lower CRP (mainstream rationale for their use), but also lower cholesterol needed for protective steroid synthesis. Peat would consider this addressing a downstream marker while worsening upstream causes.
- **Aspirin:** Anti-inflammatory; lowers prostaglandin-driven inflammation that contributes to CRP elevation.
- **Thyroid supplementation:** Supports liver energy and albumin production; may normalize the CRP-albumin relationship.
- **Dietary interventions (raw carrot, fruit juice):** Reduce endotoxin absorption, allowing the liver to exit the acute phase response. Peat reported seeing albumin rise from below 3.0 to above 4.0 in weeks -- CRP would be expected to fall correspondingly.
- **The extractions contain limited CRP-specific medication data. CRP is best understood as part of the albumin-ferritin-inflammation triad rather than managed independently.**
