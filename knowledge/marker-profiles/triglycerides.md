# Triglycerides

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered triglycerides "pretty innocuous" on their own and unreliable without knowing their fatty acid composition (saturated vs. PUFA)
**Companion markers:** Free fatty acids (the actually dangerous form), PUFA intake, dietary carbohydrate type, thyroid function, blood sugar
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically; composition matters more than the number
**Mainstream range:** <150 mg/dL (normal), 150-199 (borderline), 200-499 (high), >500 (very high)

## Peat's Position

Peat regarded triglycerides as one of the most misunderstood markers on a standard lipid panel. He described them as "pretty innocuous" — evidence that you are under stress and not using your sugar properly, but not a primary disease driver. Heart failure is actually less of a problem when triglycerides are high, and research cited by Peat showed that triglycerides protect against infective agents and are anti-inflammatory. Both LDL and HDL have protective roles in this regard.

The critical distinction Peat drew was between triglycerides and free fatty acids. Medical focus on triglyceride levels misses the real danger: free fatty acids are the dangerous form of fats in the blood, with multiple interferences with oxidative metabolism and the use of sugar. Triglycerides are stored, bound fats — relatively inert during transport. Free fatty acids are unbound and directly block glucose oxidation via the Randle cycle, suppress thyroid function, and generate inflammatory prostaglandins. The standard lipid panel measures triglycerides but rarely measures free fatty acids, focusing attention on the wrong target.

The harm of elevated triglycerides depends entirely on their fatty acid composition. Saturated triglycerides — produced by the liver through de novo lipogenesis from excess sugar — are protective to the heart and metabolically stable. PUFA triglycerides, containing polyunsaturated fatty acids from seed oils, are susceptible to peroxidation and interfere with cholesterol function. A high sugar diet can increase triglycerides and liver fat deposition, but because these are saturated and monounsaturated fats, they actually lower free radical toxicity and have an anti-diabetic effect by displacing toxic PUFA. Starches, by contrast, are strong stimulators of problematic triglyceride synthesis.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats elevated triglycerides as an independent cardiovascular risk factor and sets a target below 150 mg/dL. Peat inverted this in several ways. First, triglycerides are "pretty innocuous" and even protective against infection and inflammation — elevated levels are a stress signal, not a cause of disease. Second, the truly dangerous blood lipid is free fatty acids, which standard panels do not measure. Third, not all triglycerides are equal: saturated triglycerides from de novo lipogenesis (produced from sugar) are protective, while PUFA triglycerides (from seed oils) are harmful. The standard panel makes no distinction. Fourth, sugar-induced triglyceride elevation is actually anti-diabetic because it produces metabolically stable fats that displace toxic PUFA. Mainstream advice to restrict sugar to lower triglycerides may worsen the underlying metabolic problem.

## Causal Connections

- Metabolic stress (not using sugar properly) → ↑triglycerides (a symptom, not a cause)
- Sugar/fructose intake → ↑saturated triglycerides via de novo lipogenesis → protective
- PUFA/seed oil intake → ↑PUFA triglycerides → susceptible to peroxidation → harmful
- Starch → strong stimulation of triglyceride synthesis (more problematic than sucrose)
- Free fatty acids (unbound) → Randle cycle activation → ↓glucose oxidation → ↑blood sugar
- Triglycerides (bound) → relatively inert during transport → not directly pathogenic
- Triglycerides → protective against bacterial endotoxin and infection
- ↑Thyroid function → better sugar utilization → ↓triglycerides (normalized metabolism)
- Orange juice/fruit → supports thyroid → normalizes triglycerides

## Medication Effects

- **Niacin/Niacinamide:** Lowers free fatty acids (the actually dangerous fraction) by inhibiting lipase. This is the mechanism Peat valued, rather than triglyceride reduction per se.
- **Sugar/fructose:** Can raise triglycerides but produces the protective saturated form via de novo lipogenesis. Peat considered this beneficial, not harmful.
- **Thyroid (T3):** Normalizes triglycerides by restoring proper glucose utilization and metabolic efficiency.
- **Fibrates (mainstream triglyceride drugs):** No specific claims found in corpus.
