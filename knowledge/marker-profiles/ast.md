# AST / ALT (Liver Enzymes)

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered liver enzymes unreliable as standalone markers because they only show cell damage, not chronic dysfunction; albumin is the better liver marker
**Companion markers:** Albumin, total cholesterol, thyroid markers (TSH, T3, T4), ferritin, estrogen, GGT
**Peat's range:** Not specified
**Mainstream range:** ALT 7-56 U/L; AST 10-40 U/L

## Peat's Position

Peat's view of liver enzymes was dismissive as a primary diagnostic tool. ALT and AST elevate when liver cells are actually damaged — they are markers of acute cell death, not markers of how well the liver is functioning. A person can have severely compromised liver synthetic capacity while liver enzymes remain normal, because the liver has not yet reached the point of cell destruction. Albumin, by contrast, declines with chronic liver stress before overt damage develops, making it an earlier and more sensitive indicator of liver health. Low albumin with normal liver enzymes suggests chronic metabolic stress from hypothyroidism, excess estrogen, or inflammatory conditions — a pattern standard medicine would call "normal."

The deeper story is that the liver is where most of Peat's metabolic framework converges. The liver converts T4 to T3 (active thyroid hormone), synthesizes albumin and cholesterol, conjugates bilirubin and estrogen for excretion, and detoxifies endotoxin. When the liver is metabolically impaired — often from hypothyroidism — all of these functions suffer simultaneously. T3 directly restores liver function: in one case Peat described, T3 given to a man with serious liver cirrhosis caused him to "wake without a hangover for the first time." But when the liver is impaired, it cannot convert T4 to T3 on its own, creating a vicious cycle that standard labs may not detect.

Low cholesterol despite no statin use is another liver function marker Peat valued more than enzymes. The liver synthesizes most circulating cholesterol. When liver function is impaired, cholesterol production drops. Very low cholesterol without medication signals a weak liver — a functional marker more meaningful than ALT/AST levels alone.

The PUFA-iron-estrogen connection damages livers through a common pathway. Alcohol shifts the redox balance in a way that activates ferrous iron, and estrogen acts as a redox catalyst between NADH and NADP systems. Both generate free radicals that attack PUFA in liver cell membranes. This pathway explains why alcoholic liver disease involves both iron overload and estrogen excess, and why PUFA in the liver provides the substrate for lipid peroxidation regardless of alcohol intake.

## Mainstream Inversion

Standard medicine relies on ALT/AST as primary indicators of liver health, and "normal" liver enzymes are often taken as reassurance that the liver is fine. Peat argued this creates a dangerous blind spot: chronic liver dysfunction from hypothyroidism, estrogen excess, or PUFA accumulation may never register on liver enzymes until damage becomes severe. Albumin, cholesterol, and the T4:T3 conversion ratio are more sensitive markers of liver function. The mainstream pattern of checking liver enzymes and declaring the liver healthy misses the entire category of chronic metabolic liver stress.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → impaired liver T4-to-T3 conversion → vicious cycle of worsening liver and thyroid function
- Hypothyroidism → reduced albumin synthesis + reduced cholesterol synthesis → low-normal albumin + low cholesterol
- PUFA + iron (ferrous) + estrogen → free radical production → liver cell lipid peroxidation → elevated ALT/AST (only at damage stage)
- Alcohol → redox shift → activated ferrous iron + estrogen amplification → accelerated liver damage
- Impaired liver → reduced glucuronidation → impaired estrogen clearance → estrogen accumulates → further liver damage
- T3 supplementation → restored liver enzyme activity → improved detoxification + protein synthesis

## Sex Differences

Women's higher estrogen levels create a greater baseline burden on the liver's glucuronidation pathway. Estrogen acts as a redox catalyst in the liver, amplifying oxidative damage when combined with iron and PUFA. This may explain why women are more susceptible to certain forms of liver stress even at lower alcohol intake levels. The liver's role in estrogen clearance means that impaired liver function allows estrogen to accumulate, which further impairs the liver — a self-reinforcing cycle more pronounced in women.

## Medication Effects

T3 (active thyroid hormone) directly restores liver function. In cases where the liver cannot convert T4 to T3, supplementing T3 directly bypasses the bottleneck and can dramatically improve liver capacity. Synthetic T4 (Synthroid) may fail to help liver patients precisely because the damaged liver cannot perform the conversion. Aspirin reduces endotoxin damage to the liver. Coffee has direct hepatoprotective effects. Statins, by reducing cholesterol synthesis, may mask one of the best functional markers of liver health (cholesterol level) while doing nothing to address the underlying metabolic dysfunction.
