## Peat's Position

In Peat's framework, platelets are far more important for what they carry than for how many there are. Platelets serve as the body's serotonin transporters and destroyers. Ninety-five percent of serotonin is produced in the intestine, not the brain. When the intestine is irritated — by endotoxin, PUFA breakdown products, or bacterial overgrowth — it overproduces serotonin. Platelets bind and sequester this serotonin, keeping it from reaching the brain and other tissues where it causes harm. If the lungs and platelets fail to contain serotonin, it gets to the brain and "causes anxiety, aggression, and depression." This reframes platelets from passive clotting factors to active participants in serotonin metabolism.

The CO2 connection is critical. Hyperventilation — which displaces CO2 — causes platelets to become "leaky," unable to contain serotonin. The released serotonin constricts blood vessels, promotes inflammation, and worsens the hyperventilation cycle. Serotonin also promotes platelet aggregation (a positive feedback loop: activated platelets release serotonin, which activates more platelets) and increases vascular permeability, promoting edema. This cascade connects breathing patterns directly to cardiovascular risk, mood, and inflammatory status through the platelet-serotonin axis.

Endotoxin absorption from the gut amplifies this system. When endotoxin exceeds the liver's processing capacity, LPS spills into systemic circulation and triggers serotonin release from platelets, histamine release from mast cells, and widespread inflammatory cascades. The gut-platelet-serotonin axis is therefore a central node in Peat's model of how intestinal health drives systemic disease.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine tracks platelet counts primarily for clotting disorders (thrombocytopenia/thrombocytosis) and rarely considers the serotonin transport function in routine lab interpretation. Peat's framework makes the platelet's serotonin role central to understanding anxiety, depression, cardiovascular risk, and inflammatory disease — conditions that mainstream medicine typically does not connect to platelet function. The idea that hyperventilation causes platelets to leak serotonin, producing psychiatric and cardiovascular symptoms, is absent from standard platelet interpretation.

## Causal Connections

- Gut irritation → excess intestinal serotonin production → overwhelms platelet containment → systemic serotonin → anxiety/depression/vasoconstriction
- Hyperventilation → low CO2 → platelets release serotonin → vasoconstriction + inflammation → worsened hyperventilation (vicious cycle)
- Endotoxin absorption → liver overwhelmed → systemic LPS → platelet degranulation → serotonin + inflammatory cascade
- Serotonin released from platelets → further platelet aggregation (positive feedback) → clotting risk + vascular damage
- Aspirin → blocks COX → reduces thromboxane A2 → reduced platelet aggregation + reduced endotoxin damage

## Medication Effects

Aspirin reduces platelet aggregation by blocking inflammatory prostaglandins (specifically thromboxane A2). Peat suggested 300-500mg+ of aspirin when needed, significantly higher than the standard low-dose 81mg baby aspirin. He noted aspirin's effects on circulation come mainly from reducing inflammation and improving cellular respiration, not from direct vasodilation. Aspirin also reduces endotoxin damage and serotonin release, making it protective across the entire gut-platelet-serotonin axis.
