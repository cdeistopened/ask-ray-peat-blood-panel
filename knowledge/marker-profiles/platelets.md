## Peat's Position

In Peat's framework, platelets are far more important for what they carry than for how many there are. Platelets serve as the body's serotonin transporters and destroyers. Ninety-five percent of serotonin is produced in the intestine, not the brain. When the intestine is irritated — by endotoxin, PUFA breakdown products, or bacterial overgrowth — it overproduces serotonin. Platelets bind and sequester this serotonin, keeping it from reaching the brain and other tissues where it causes harm. If the lungs and platelets fail to contain serotonin, it gets to the brain and "causes anxiety, aggression, and depression." This reframes platelets from passive clotting factors to active participants in serotonin metabolism.

The CO2 connection is critical. Carbon dioxide causes cells to bind serotonin; loss of carbon dioxide causes serotonin to be released. Hyperventilation — which displaces CO2 — causes platelets to become "leaky," unable to contain serotonin. The released serotonin constricts blood vessels, promotes inflammation, makes blood vessels more permeable (leaking fluid and serum proteins into surrounding spaces), and worsens the hyperventilation cycle. In the lungs, this serotonin-driven permeability is especially dangerous — what Peat connected to "shock lung." Serotonin also promotes platelet aggregation (a positive feedback loop: activated platelets release serotonin, which activates more platelets) and increases vascular permeability, promoting edema. This cascade connects breathing patterns directly to cardiovascular risk, mood, and inflammatory status through the platelet-serotonin axis.

The enrichment data adds an important dimension connecting platelets to iron and PUFA. Lipid peroxidation affects platelet membrane fluidity, with implications extending to Alzheimer's disease. Iron in lipofuscin deposits interacts with PUFA in platelet membranes, altering their behavior and contributing to abnormal clotting and neurodegeneration. This means iron accumulation and PUFA tissue content jointly determine platelet function — a connection that standard medicine does not make when interpreting platelet counts or function tests.

Endotoxin absorption from the gut amplifies the entire system. When endotoxin exceeds the liver's processing capacity, LPS spills into systemic circulation and triggers serotonin release from platelets, histamine release from mast cells, and widespread inflammatory cascades. The gut-platelet-serotonin axis is therefore a central node in Peat's model of how intestinal health drives systemic disease.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine tracks platelet counts primarily for clotting disorders (thrombocytopenia/thrombocytosis) and rarely considers the serotonin transport function in routine lab interpretation. Peat's framework makes the platelet's serotonin role central to understanding anxiety, depression, cardiovascular risk, and inflammatory disease — conditions that mainstream medicine typically does not connect to platelet function. The idea that hyperventilation causes platelets to leak serotonin, producing psychiatric and cardiovascular symptoms, is absent from standard platelet interpretation. The connection between platelet membrane PUFA content, iron-catalyzed peroxidation, and neurodegenerative disease is also not part of standard hematology.

## Causal Connections

- Gut irritation → excess intestinal serotonin production → overwhelms platelet containment → systemic serotonin → anxiety/depression/vasoconstriction
- Hyperventilation → low CO2 → platelets release serotonin → vasoconstriction + vascular permeability + inflammation → worsened hyperventilation (vicious cycle)
- CO2 loss → serotonin release → blood vessel permeability → fluid leakage into surrounding spaces → "shock lung" in severe cases
- Endotoxin absorption → liver overwhelmed → systemic LPS → platelet degranulation → serotonin + inflammatory cascade
- Serotonin released from platelets → further platelet aggregation (positive feedback) → clotting risk + vascular damage
- Iron + PUFA in platelet membranes → lipid peroxidation → altered membrane fluidity → abnormal clotting → neurodegeneration (Alzheimer's link)
- Aspirin → blocks COX → reduces thromboxane A2 → reduced platelet aggregation + reduced endotoxin damage

## Medication Effects

- **Aspirin:** Reduces platelet aggregation by blocking inflammatory prostaglandins (specifically thromboxane A2). Peat suggested 300-500mg+ when needed, significantly higher than the standard low-dose 81mg baby aspirin. Also reduces endotoxin damage and serotonin release, making it protective across the entire gut-platelet-serotonin axis.
- **Cyproheptadine:** Anti-serotonin drug that blocks serotonin receptors, potentially reducing serotonin-driven platelet aggregation and vascular permeability.
- **SSRIs:** Block serotonin reuptake by platelets, which in Peat's framework would leave more serotonin in circulation — potentially worsening the very symptoms they are prescribed to treat.
- **CO2 support (bag breathing, reduced hyperventilation):** Restores platelet serotonin binding, reducing the inflammatory cascade from serotonin release.
