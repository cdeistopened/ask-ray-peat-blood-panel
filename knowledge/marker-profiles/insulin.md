# Insulin

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat considered insulin measurement useful but argued that insulin resistance is a protective adaptation, not pure pathology, and that diagnostic thresholds have been artificially lowered
**Companion markers:** Fasting glucose, free fatty acids, lactate, PUFA intake, thyroid (T3/TSH), potassium, dietary carbohydrate type, estrogen
**Peat's range:** Not specified numerically; lower insulin demand from diet = better; hyperinsulinemia itself accelerates aging
**Mainstream range:** 2.6-24.9 mcIU/mL (fasting)

## Peat's Position

Peat's view of insulin departs from mainstream endocrinology at a fundamental level. He argued that insulin resistance is not pure pathology but a protective adaptation. When cells cannot oxidize glucose efficiently — due to PUFA accumulation in mitochondrial membranes, mitochondrial damage, or inflammatory cytokines — forcing more glucose into those cells through high insulin levels would simply produce more lactic acid and metabolic stress. Insulin resistance limits this damage by reducing glucose uptake into metabolically compromised cells.

The cause of impaired glucose oxidation, in Peat's framework, is primarily polyunsaturated fatty acids. PUFA activates the Randle cycle by blocking pyruvate dehydrogenase, preventing cells from completing the oxidation of glucose. Intravenous injection of emulsified soy oil produces hyperglycemia within 15 minutes — a real-time demonstration of PUFA-driven insulin resistance. PUFA is also specifically toxic to pancreatic beta cells, creating cumulative chronic damage to the insulin-producing tissue itself. This is distinct from the temporary Randle effect of any fatty acid; PUFA peroxidation products directly destroy beta cells over time. Chronic PUFA accumulation also increases estrogen production, and PUFA free fatty acids intensify estrogen's actions — creating insulin resistance through an estrogen-mediated pathway.

Hyperinsulinemia itself accelerates aging. Decreasing insulin-like receptor activity extends lifespan in animal models, suggesting that minimizing insulin demand (by eating fruit sugar over starch) has anti-aging effects.

Peat emphasized that potassium plays a larger role in glucose disposal than insulin. Fruit juice, containing both sugar and potassium, requires far less insulin than bread with similar calories. Fructose enters cells and is metabolized without requiring insulin, making fruit and sucrose fundamentally different from starch in their insulin effects. This is why starch-fed rats gained more weight than sugar-eating rats despite similar caloric content — fructose in sucrose limits insulin secretion while intensifying metabolic activity, whereas pure glucose from starch requires more insulin and promotes more fat storage.

Peat also critiqued the progressive expansion of diabetes diagnoses. The expansion of the diabetic population beyond those with genuine metabolic disease was achieved by progressively lowering diagnostic thresholds, creating a market for pharmaceutical intervention. Gestational diabetes diagnostic thresholds have been similarly lowered to capture more pregnant women, when pregnancy naturally shifts toward insulin resistance to direct glucose preferentially to the fetus — an evolutionary adaptation, not a disease.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats insulin resistance as the primary pathology in Type 2 diabetes and seeks to overcome it with insulin sensitizers (metformin) or exogenous insulin. Peat argued that insulin resistance is a protective adaptation — cells that cannot oxidize glucose are limiting further metabolic damage. Forcing glucose in with more insulin produces more lactic acid, worsening the underlying problem. The correct approach is to restore oxidative capacity (through thyroid support, PUFA reduction, and adequate sugar/fructose) rather than overriding the protective resistance. Mainstream diagnostic thresholds for diabetes and gestational diabetes have been progressively lowered to expand pharmaceutical markets, capturing people with transient or stress-related glucose elevations or normal pregnancy adaptations. Oral contraceptives increase triglycerides, fasting insulin, and 2-hour glucose while fasting glucose and HbA1c appear normal — masking the metabolic disruption in standard screening.

## Sex Differences

Pregnancy naturally shifts metabolism toward insulin resistance to direct glucose preferentially to the fetus. Peat considered gestational diabetes a medicalized normal variation in most cases. Progressively lowered diagnostic thresholds capture more pregnant women into pharmaceutical treatment during what is an evolutionary adaptation, not a disease. Oral contraceptives produce measurable insulin resistance (higher fasting insulin, higher 2-hour glucose, higher triglycerides) without changing fasting glucose or HbA1c — masking the disruption.

## Medication Effects

- **Exogenous insulin:** Peat considered insulin-forcing potentially harmful when cells cannot oxidize glucose — it produces more lactic acid and metabolic stress in compromised cells.
- **Metformin:** Standard insulin sensitizer. Peat's framework suggests it addresses a secondary mechanism (insulin resistance) while leaving the primary cause (PUFA-driven Randle cycle) unaddressed.
- **T3 (thyroid):** Always appropriate with diabetes because it restores the oxidative metabolism that insulin resistance is compensating for. Any diabetes diagnosis should include TSH assessment. In diabetes, use combination T3+T4, not T4-only (T4 accumulates when glucose is insufficient for conversion).
- **Niacinamide:** Inhibits lipase, reducing free fatty acid release and preventing Randle cycle activation. One of Peat's most practical anti-diabetic tools.
- **Aspirin:** Blocks inflammatory prostaglandins from PUFA, reducing metabolic interference.
- **Sugar/fruit juice:** Suppresses lipolysis (keeping PUFA stored), provides fructose (bypasses insulin), and supplies potassium (insulin-independent glucose disposal).
- **Oral contraceptives:** Increase insulin resistance through estrogen-mediated pathway; masked by normal fasting glucose.
