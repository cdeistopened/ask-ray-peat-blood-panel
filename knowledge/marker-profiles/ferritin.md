# Ferritin

**Reliability:** Tier 4 -- Peat explicitly distrusted ferritin as a measure of iron status. He said it is "not meaningful enough to worry about." It is a positive acute phase reactant that rises with inflammation regardless of actual iron stores. Elevated serum ferritin means cells are stressed and leaking, not that iron stores are known.
**Companion markers:** Transferrin saturation (the preferred iron test), hemoglobin, CRP, albumin, liver biopsy (gold standard), TSH, thyroid markers, interleukin-18
**Peat's range:** Not specified as a target. >400 ng/mL warrants concern. The only certain way to assess iron stores is biopsy.
**Mainstream range:** 20-200 ng/mL (women); 20-500 ng/mL (men); <12 ng/mL considered deficient; >500 ng/mL triggers investigation

## Peat's Position

Ferritin was one of the lab values Peat was most dismissive of. He stated plainly: "I don't think ferritin is meaningful enough to worry about." His reasoning was that ferritin is properly an intracellular storage form that should be invisible to blood tests. When it appears elevated in serum, it means cells are stressed, injured, or overloaded with iron and are leaking their contents. Serum ferritin therefore reflects cellular distress as much as -- or more than -- iron storage. High ferritin does not directly imply high iron stores — it has a defensive effect and can be increased by inflammation alone.

Ferritin is classified alongside CRP and fibrinogen as a positive acute phase protein. During inflammation, the liver redirects protein synthesis away from maintenance proteins (albumin) toward emergency inflammatory proteins (CRP, ferritin, fibrinogen). This is the same mechanism that causes albumin to drop during chronic illness. Any inflammatory condition will raise ferritin independent of actual iron stores: chronic stress activates heme oxygenase, which breaks down heme groups and liberates free iron atoms, raising both tissue iron and serum ferritin. TSH promotes inflammation and increases interleukin-18 (IL-18), which is associated with liver damage and increased ferritin — so abnormal thyroid status can affect ferritin level without necessarily affecting actual iron load. Always check thyroid status when interpreting ferritin.

The only certain way to diagnose iron overload or deficiency, Peat argued, is to biopsy bone marrow or liver. Very often, when blood levels suggest deficiency, bone marrow and liver contain more than normal amounts of iron. Conversely, blood ferritin can rise from inflammation without true iron overload. Only when circulating iron approaches one-hundredth of healthy levels can a fair guess at true deficiency be made. For routine monitoring, Peat preferred watching hemoglobin at the low end of the normal range and transferrin saturation under 30%.

High ferritin with adequate vitamin D may indicate insufficient calcium relative to phosphate, causing elevated PTH that produces generalized inflammation. PTH testing is recommended in this context.

A ferritin of 800 (twice the upper limit) indicates a serious situation, but pharmaceutical iron chelators like deferoxamine are dangerous because they can relocate iron to worse places (kidneys, brain) rather than truly excreting it. Coffee is Peat's preferred safe chelator for iron management.

Iron itself has a cumulative danger: removing iron from food explains much of the life-extending effect of caloric restriction. The shape of the iron accumulation curve (minimum at age 12, rising steadily to death) is "amazingly similar" to the curves for death rate in general and death rate from cancer. Vitamin E should be taken (~100 mg/day) to counteract iron's oxidative damage, but NOT at the same time as iron-rich food because iron reacts with it in the stomach. The requirement for vitamin E increases with age as iron stores increase.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine uses ferritin as the primary screening test for iron deficiency and overload. Low ferritin (<12 ng/mL) is treated as proof of iron deficiency, prompting iron supplementation. Peat argued that very low ferritin in a woman with normal circulating iron may reflect thyroid dysfunction impairing copper-dependent iron metabolism rather than true deficiency -- thyroid is required for absorbing copper, which is needed for integrating iron with the blood. Supplementing iron in this scenario worsens tissue iron burden without correcting the functional deficiency.

Iron supplements during pregnancy have been associated with increased jaundice in newborns and may contribute to increased childhood cancer. Women who did not take iron supplements during pregnancy had healthy babies without jaundice. Even heavy menstruation involves the loss of only 30-40 mg of iron per month (about 1 mg/day), and it is practically impossible NOT to get that amount from diet. Women absorb 3x more iron from food than men; pregnant women absorb 9x more — estrogen increases iron absorption.

High ferritin in mainstream medicine triggers evaluation for hemochromatosis, liver disease, or malignancy. Peat agreed that high ferritin is concerning but for a broader reason: it reflects the inflammatory cascade (heme oxygenase activation, cellular stress, acute phase response) rather than simply "too much iron in the body." The COVID-19 correlation -- high ferritin and hepcidin correlating with poor outcomes -- supports Peat's view that ferritin elevation reflects systemic inflammatory state.

## Sex Differences

Women traditionally have lower ferritin due to menstrual iron loss. Peat considered this protective: women menstruate, losing iron regularly, while men accumulate it progressively. Even heavy menstruation involves loss of only ~1 mg iron/day, easily replaced by diet. Women absorb 3x more iron from food than men; pregnant women absorb 9x more — estrogen increases iron absorption. After menopause, iron retention begins in earnest because progesterone production fails, estrogen (which causes iron retention) becomes unopposed, and menstrual iron loss ceases. Post-menopausal women's iron load catches up with men's within a few years, coinciding with when heart attack incidence equalizes. This convergence partly explains why post-menopausal women's disease rates approach men's.

For premenopausal women with low ferritin, the reflex to prescribe iron supplements is often wrong. Low ferritin may reflect inflammation-driven shifts in iron distribution (not true deficiency), or it may reflect thyroid/copper dysfunction preventing proper iron utilization. If hemoglobin is adequate and transferrin saturation is not extremely low, iron supplementation may be harmful.

Men, who have no regular iron excretory pathway, accumulate iron throughout life. Blood donation provides the closest equivalent to menstrual iron loss and is recommended.

## Medication Effects

- **Iron supplements:** Often prescribed for low ferritin. Peat considered this frequently harmful -- it adds to tissue iron burden without correcting the underlying copper, thyroid, or inflammation problem. For 100 years, doctors prescribed iron to women with low hemoglobin, making them worse. Iron supplements during pregnancy associated with newborn jaundice and childhood cancer.
- **Coffee:** Peat's preferred safe iron chelator. Reduces bound heavy metals in liver, kidneys. Heavy coffee drinkers have extremely low incidence of all liver diseases. Drink with iron-rich meals.
- **Aspirin:** One of the most protective things against iron toxicity, alongside coffee and vitamin E. May act as antioxidant and iron binder.
- **Vitamin E:** Protects against iron-catalyzed lipid peroxidation. ~100 mg/day, timed away from iron-rich meals (iron reacts with it in the stomach). Requirement increases with age as iron stores increase.
- **Vitamin D:** Lowers iron retention indirectly by suppressing PTH, which reduces cellular affinity for iron and heavy metals.
- **Pharmaceutical chelators (deferoxamine):** Dangerous -- can relocate iron to worse places (kidneys, brain) and paradoxically worsen toxicity.
- **Chocolate/cocoa:** Inhibits iron absorption as effectively as coffee when consumed with iron-rich meals.
- **Tea:** Tannins bind iron in the gut, reducing absorption.
