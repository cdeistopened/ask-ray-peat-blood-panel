# Free T3

**Reliability:** Tier 3 — Peat considered the concept of "free" T3 vague and contradictory because the dialysis measurement method misses hormone traveling in red blood cells and bound to albumin
**Companion markers:** Temperature, pulse, Free T4, TSH, estrogen, liver function, Reverse T3
**Peat's range:** Not specified (distrusted the measurement method itself)
**Mainstream range:** 2.3-4.1 pg/mL

## Peat's Position

T3 (triiodothyronine) is the active thyroid hormone in Peat's framework — the molecule that actually drives oxidative metabolism at the cellular level. While T4 is the more abundant circulating form, T3 is what cells use. The ratio of T4 to T3 in healthy thyroid glands (pig and cow) is about 3:1 to 4:1, but in hypothyroid people, the blood serum ratio can reach 50:1 or even 100:1, revealing a massive failure of T4-to-T3 conversion in the liver. This conversion failure is the central problem in Peat's thyroid framework.

However, Peat was skeptical of the "Free T3" test as performed by laboratories. He pointed out that the concept of a "free" hormone is vague and contradictory. The standard dialysis method measures what passes through a dialysis membrane and calls it "free," but in living blood, thyroid hormones actually travel with proteins and fats and in and on blood cells. Albumin-bound thyroid hormone freely enters cells, meaning it is functionally "free" but is not captured by the test. Red blood cells carry thyroid hormone that is also missed. This means the measured "Free T3" value is an incomplete picture of how much active hormone is reaching tissues.

Despite this measurement limitation, Peat considered T3 the most important thyroid hormone for health. T3 is the main anti-inflammatory agent of the thyroid system. It replaces stress hormones (adrenaline, cortisol) as the primary metabolic driver. In extremely adrenergic states, supplemental T3 causes both temperature and heart rate to paradoxically decrease as it restores metabolic efficiency and suppresses compensatory stress hormones. T3 availability determines whether cells oxidize glucose efficiently (producing CO2) or resort to glycolysis (producing lactate).

The factors that impair T4-to-T3 conversion are central to Peat's model of disease: estrogen blocks hepatic deiodinase, PUFA damages the conversion enzymes, liver disease reduces conversion capacity, and even moderate exercise (pulse above 120 bpm) can suppress T3 production within an hour. Vitamin D deficiency shifts T4 processing toward Reverse T3 rather than active T3.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine treats Free T3 as a reliable measurement and uses it primarily to confirm hyperthyroidism (high Free T3) or to screen for T3 thyrotoxicosis. Peat challenged this on two fronts. First, the measurement itself is unreliable because it misses biologically active hormone traveling with albumin and red blood cells. Second, mainstream interpretation focuses on whether T3 is within the reference range, whereas Peat's concern was the ratio of T4 to T3 — a hypothyroid person can have a "normal" Free T3 result while having a grossly abnormal T4:T3 ratio (50:1 to 100:1 instead of the physiological 3:1 to 4:1). Mainstream endocrinology does not routinely calculate or interpret this ratio.

## Causal Connections

- ↑PUFA intake → damaged conversion enzymes → ↓T3 production

- Low vitamin D → T4 processed to Reverse T3 instead of T3

- Adequate T3 → replaces stress hormones → paradoxical ↓pulse + ↑temperature
- ↓T3 → ↑cholesterol (unconverted to steroid hormones)

## Sex Differences

Women are disproportionately affected by T3 deficiency because estrogen directly impairs the hepatic deiodinase enzyme responsible for T4-to-T3 conversion. Peat noted that women are the main population with thyroid disease, and that T4-only therapy (Synthroid) frequently fails women specifically because estrogen blocks the conversion step. Men, with lower estrogen levels, have better T4-to-T3 conversion and are more likely to benefit from T4-only therapy.

## Medication Effects

- **Synthroid (T4-only):** Provides substrate but does not guarantee T3 production. In women with estrogen dominance, can paradoxically worsen hypothyroidism by suppressing TSH while liver cannot produce adequate T3.
- **Cynomel/Cytomel (T3-only):** Bypasses the conversion bottleneck entirely. Peat recommended starting at 5-10 mcg/day, increasing by 5 mcg every 3-5 days if temperature remains low, maintenance 15-50 mcg/day in divided doses. Max single dose: 10 mcg. Take with food/sugar, not fasting.
- **Cynoplus (T4+T3):** Provides T3 directly in a 4:1 T4:T3 ratio, bypassing conversion failure.
- **Armour Thyroid:** Contains T3 in ~4:1 ratio with T4, plus T2, T1, and calcitonin.
- **Estrogen/birth control:** Suppresses T4-to-T3 conversion, worsening functional T3 deficiency.
