# Bilirubin

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat considered bilirubin useful as a window into glucuronidation capacity and liver detoxification, and the enrichment data reveals its deeper role as a product of heme oxygenase activation during stress and aging
**Companion markers:** Direct/indirect bilirubin, ALT/AST, glucose, thyroid markers, estrogen, heme oxygenase, ferritin, iron, CO2/bicarbonate
**Peat's range:** Not specified
**Mainstream range:** Total bilirubin 0.1-1.2 mg/dL; direct bilirubin 0.0-0.3 mg/dL

## Peat's Position

Bilirubin occupies two important roles in Peat's framework. First, it is a marker of glucuronidation — the liver's primary detoxification pathway. Elevated indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin suggests this pathway is impaired. Glucuronidation attaches glucuronic acid (derived from glucose) to bilirubin, making it water-soluble for excretion. This same pathway clears estrogen, drugs, PUFA breakdown products, and other toxins. When glucuronidation is impaired, bilirubin accumulates — but so does estrogen and everything else that depends on this pathway for clearance. An elevated indirect bilirubin therefore signals a bottleneck with consequences far beyond jaundice.

Second, bilirubin is a direct product of heme oxygenase (HO) activity. Peat described heme oxygenase as a positive acute phase reactant that breaks down toxic free heme into three products — free iron, carbon monoxide, and bilirubin — solving one problem (toxic heme) but creating three new toxic threats. During stress and aging, hemoglobin leaks from red blood cells and the free heme activates HO. The bilirubin produced is itself associated with lipid peroxidation and DNA damage, while the released free iron catalyzes further peroxidation and the carbon monoxide poisons cellular respiration. This connects bilirubin to the lipofuscin-HIF-iron-estrogen degenerative spiral: lipofuscin creates intracellular hypoxia, activating HIF, which upregulates heme oxygenase (releasing more iron, CO, and bilirubin), increases iron absorption, and activates aromatase (increasing estrogen).

Glucuronidation requires adequate glucose supply (the substrate for glucuronic acid), thyroid hormone (for enzyme activity), and vitamin A (for liver cell function). A hypothyroid person on a low-carbohydrate diet has impaired glucuronidation on multiple fronts simultaneously. The PUFA detoxification pathway also depends on glucuronidation — the main way PUFA are detoxified is by attaching glucuronic acid, making them water-soluble for urinary excretion. This creates competition between bilirubin, estrogen, and PUFA metabolites for the same liver conjugation capacity.

The estrogen connection is particularly important. Glucuronidation is the main pathway for clearing both bilirubin and estrogen. Gut bacteria producing beta-glucuronidase can cleave the glucuronide bond, releasing conjugated estrogen and bilirubin back into circulation through enterohepatic recirculation. Endotoxin increases beta-glucuronidase activity, meaning gut dysbiosis directly impairs the body's ability to clear both bilirubin and estrogen.

About 60% of newborn babies are jaundiced for a few days, which Peat interpreted as showing that birth stress activates heme oxygenase, turning heme into its toxic components. Free bilirubin in neonates is associated with lipid peroxidation and DNA damage. Better prenatal conditions — avoiding PUFA and excess iron during pregnancy — would reduce this incidence.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine interprets bilirubin primarily in the context of liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis) or hemolytic conditions. Peat's view adds two dimensions that standard medicine underappreciates. First, the metabolic dimension: elevated indirect bilirubin may indicate impaired glucuronidation from hypothyroidism, inadequate glucose, or vitamin A deficiency — conditions not typically connected to bilirubin levels. Second, the heme oxygenase dimension: elevated bilirubin as a marker of chronic HO activation, reflecting the iron-CO-bilirubin stress cascade that drives aging and degenerative disease. The shared pathway between bilirubin and estrogen clearance is recognized in pharmacology but rarely applied in clinical interpretation. The role of gut bacteria (beta-glucuronidase) in recycling both bilirubin and estrogen back into circulation is also underappreciated.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → impaired glucuronidation enzyme activity → elevated unconjugated bilirubin
- Inadequate glucose → insufficient glucuronic acid substrate → impaired conjugation
- Impaired glucuronidation → bilirubin AND estrogen AND PUFA metabolites accumulate (shared pathway)
- Gut bacteria (beta-glucuronidase) → cleave conjugated compounds → bilirubin + estrogen recirculate
- Endotoxin → increased beta-glucuronidase activity → more recirculation of bilirubin + estrogen
- Stress/aging → hemoglobin leaks from RBCs → free heme → heme oxygenase activation → free iron + CO + bilirubin
- Lipofuscin → hypoxia → HIF activation → ↑heme oxygenase → more free iron + CO + bilirubin → more lipid peroxidation (vicious circle)
- Birth stress → HO activation → neonatal jaundice (60% of newborns)

## Sex Differences

The shared glucuronidation pathway for bilirubin and estrogen means that women with higher estrogen production place a greater demand on this pathway, potentially making elevated bilirubin more significant as a marker of estrogen clearance capacity in women. The heme oxygenase pathway also connects to estrogen through HIF-activated aromatase — chronic HO activation increases both bilirubin and estrogen simultaneously.

## Medication Effects

- **Thyroid supplementation:** Supports glucuronidation enzyme activity, improving bilirubin clearance. Also supports liver energy for all conjugation pathways.
- **Adequate fruit sugar:** Provides glucose for glucuronic acid production — the substrate for bilirubin conjugation.
- **Raw carrot salad:** Binds estrogen in the gut, reducing the enterohepatic recirculation burden that competes with bilirubin clearance. Also reduces endotoxin (which drives beta-glucuronidase).
- **Vitamin A:** Required for liver cell function and glucuronidation capacity.
- **Aspirin:** Reduces inflammation and HO activation, potentially reducing bilirubin production from the heme degradation pathway.
