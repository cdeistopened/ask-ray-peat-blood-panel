# Bilirubin

**Reliability:** Tier 2 — Peat considered bilirubin useful as a window into glucuronidation capacity and liver detoxification, but with important interpretive caveats
**Companion markers:** Direct/indirect bilirubin, ALT/AST, glucose, thyroid markers, estrogen, reticulocyte count, haptoglobin, LDH
**Peat's range:** Not specified
**Mainstream range:** Total bilirubin 0.1-1.2 mg/dL; direct bilirubin 0.0-0.3 mg/dL

## Peat's Position

In Peat's framework, bilirubin is most informative as a marker of glucuronidation — the liver's primary detoxification pathway. Elevated indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin suggests this pathway is impaired. Glucuronidation attaches glucuronic acid (derived from glucose) to bilirubin, making it water-soluble for excretion. This same pathway clears estrogen, drugs, and PUFA breakdown products. When glucuronidation is impaired, bilirubin accumulates — but so does estrogen and every other substance that depends on this pathway for clearance. An elevated indirect bilirubin therefore signals a bottleneck that has consequences far beyond jaundice.

Glucuronidation requires adequate glucose supply (the substrate for glucuronic acid), thyroid hormone (for enzyme activity), and vitamin A (for liver cell function). A hypothyroid person on a low-carbohydrate diet has impaired glucuronidation on multiple fronts simultaneously. This connects bilirubin to Peat's broader insistence on adequate carbohydrate (particularly fruit sugar) and thyroid support for liver health.

The estrogen connection is particularly important. Glucuronidation is the main pathway for clearing both bilirubin and estrogen. Gut bacteria producing beta-glucuronidase can cleave the glucuronide bond, releasing conjugated estrogen and bilirubin back into circulation through enterohepatic recirculation. Endotoxin increases beta-glucuronidase activity, meaning gut dysbiosis directly impairs the body's ability to clear both bilirubin and estrogen. This links an elevated bilirubin to potential estrogen dominance through a shared metabolic pathway.

Bilirubin is also a breakdown product of heme from hemoglobin and other heme proteins. Its levels reflect both the rate of RBC turnover (how quickly old red blood cells are being destroyed) and the liver's conjugation capacity (how quickly the liver can process the resulting bilirubin). An elevated bilirubin with signs of hemolysis points to excessive RBC destruction, while an elevated bilirubin with normal hemolysis markers points to impaired liver conjugation.

## Mainstream Inversion

Mainstream medicine interprets bilirubin primarily in the context of liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis) or hemolytic conditions. Peat's view adds a metabolic dimension: elevated indirect bilirubin may indicate impaired glucuronidation from hypothyroidism, inadequate glucose, or vitamin A deficiency — conditions that standard medicine does not typically connect to bilirubin levels. The shared pathway between bilirubin and estrogen clearance is recognized in pharmacology but rarely applied in clinical interpretation of routine bilirubin results. The role of gut bacteria (beta-glucuronidase) in recycling both bilirubin and estrogen back into circulation is also underappreciated in standard practice.

## Causal Connections

- Hypothyroidism → impaired glucuronidation enzyme activity → elevated unconjugated bilirubin
- Inadequate glucose → insufficient glucuronic acid substrate → impaired conjugation
- Impaired glucuronidation → bilirubin AND estrogen accumulate (shared pathway)
- Gut bacteria (beta-glucuronidase) → cleave conjugated compounds → bilirubin + estrogen recirculate (enterohepatic recirculation)
- Endotoxin → increased beta-glucuronidase activity → more recirculation of bilirubin + estrogen
- Excessive RBC turnover → more bilirubin production → overwhelms conjugation capacity
- Glycine → required for heme synthesis → connects bilirubin (heme breakdown) to dietary protein quality

## Sex Differences

No explicit sex-specific differences in bilirubin interpretation were found in the extracted claims. However, the shared glucuronidation pathway for bilirubin and estrogen means that women with higher estrogen production place a greater demand on this pathway, potentially making elevated bilirubin more significant as a marker of estrogen clearance capacity in women.

## Medication Effects

No specific medication effects on bilirubin were discussed in the extracted claims. Within Peat's framework, thyroid supplementation would support glucuronidation enzyme activity, adequate fruit sugar provides glucose for glucuronic acid production, and raw carrot salad helps bind estrogen in the gut, reducing the enterohepatic recirculation burden that competes with bilirubin clearance.
