# Pulse & Temperature Protocol -- Peat's Primary Diagnostic Tools

Peat considered pulse and temperature more reliable than any blood test. His diagnostic hierarchy placed functional indicators at the top: (1) BMR/oxygen consumption (gold standard, abandoned in favor of TSH testing), (2) body temperature + pulse rate (practical daily tool), (3) functional signs (cholesterol, Achilles reflex, warmth of extremities, bowel function, sleep quality), (4) select blood tests. Blood tests ranked last because "the blood isn't a great test because hormones travel with proteins and fats" -- what circulates in the blood does not reliably reflect what is happening in tissues.

---

## Why Pulse and Temperature Come First

Peat's reasoning rested on a simple principle: temperature and pulse directly measure what thyroid hormone does -- drive oxidative metabolism and generate heat. Blood tests measure circulating molecules, which can be misleading for multiple reasons:

1. **Hormones bind to carrier proteins.** Serum levels reflect the circulating fraction, not tissue activity. Brain concentrations of pregnenolone, DHEA, and progesterone are about 10x blood levels. Estrogen stuck in tissue from progesterone deficiency does not appear in blood at all.

2. **Reference ranges include sick people.** "Normal" ranges are based on a metabolically compromised population. A TSH of 3.0 may be functionally hypothyroid. Prolactin ranges were raised to accommodate a hyperprolactinemic population. "Each lab tends to have its own standard, and they usually use people who are sick to establish the 'normal' for a given blood test."

3. **Stress hormones distort lab results.** Cortisol, adrenaline, and estrogen all suppress TSH while simultaneously blocking T4-to-T3 conversion. A stressed person can have "normal" TSH while being severely hypothyroid. "A low thyroid person raises their estrogen, cortisol, and adrenaline, and these add up to lower your TSH and make it look like you have too much thyroid."

4. **The best single test was abandoned.** "The best single test would be the Basal Metabolic Rate from the 1930s -- measuring how much oxygen you breathe in and how much carbon dioxide you exhale. Temperature is just a rough approximation." By BMR standards, 30% of Americans were hypothyroid in 1940; by current conditions, Peat estimated 60%. The switch to TSH testing narrowed the diagnostic window, leaving the vast middle range untreated.

> "The blood isn't a great test because hormones travel with proteins and fats." -- It's Rainmaking Time (2014-06-02)

> "The best single test would be the Basal Metabolic Rate from the 1930s -- measuring how much oxygen you breathe in and how much carbon dioxide you exhale. Temperature is just a rough approximation." -- wiki/substances/hormones/thyroid.md

> "By the standard of oxygen consumption, 30% of Americans were hypothyroid in 1940; now it is more like 60%." -- 2013-11-15 Ask the Herb Doctor; wiki/conditions/hypothyroidism.md

## Temperature Protocol

### How to Measure

Peat referenced Broda Barnes's basal temperature method: measure axillary (armpit) temperature for 10 minutes immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed or any activity. Oral temperature is also acceptable and is the standard for post-breakfast readings. Use a reliable thermometer. Track for at least one week to establish a pattern -- single readings can be misleading due to stress hormone fluctuations, illness, or ambient temperature.

> "Broda Barnes found temperature was adequate for diagnosing. He thought waking temperature should be about 97.8F, rising to about 98.6F after breakfast." -- wiki/people/broda-barnes.md; 2011-02-22 EastWest Healing

### Target Values

- **Waking temperature:** ~97.8F (36.6C) axillary or oral, before getting out of bed. Below 97.4F strongly suggests hypothyroidism.
- **Post-breakfast (30-60 min):** ~98.6F (37C) oral. The rise from waking to post-meal should be 0.5-1.0F. This confirms that the meal stimulates adequate metabolic response and that the liver is converting T4 to T3.
- **Mid-afternoon:** 98.6F oral or higher. Chronically low afternoon temperature indicates metabolic suppression regardless of lab values.

### What the Values Mean

**Low waking temperature (below 97.4F):** Indicates overnight metabolic rate is insufficient. The body is not generating enough heat from oxidative metabolism. Most likely cause: hypothyroidism, either primary or functional (from PUFA accumulation, estrogen dominance, or nutrient deficiency).

**Temperature fails to rise after eating:** This is the critical diagnostic finding. In health, eating a carbohydrate-containing meal replenishes liver glycogen, the liver converts T4 to T3, and metabolic rate increases. If temperature FALLS after eating, the pre-meal readings were artificially elevated by stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline). Food reduces stress hormones, unmasking the underlying metabolic deficit. "Some people wake up with a 98 or 99-degree temperature, but after they eat breakfast, by 10:00 or 11:00 AM, their temperature might fall to 94 or 95 degrees."

**High temperature with cold extremities:** Adrenaline can raise core temperature by shunting blood inward from the periphery. "The adrenaline will make your skin cold to increase your core temperature. So it can be misleading." Always check hand and foot temperature alongside core temperature.

**Temperature masking in summer:** In hot humid weather, hypothyroid people can maintain normal core temperature from environmental heat, but their pulse rate remains low (45-65 bpm), revealing the underlying deficit. Summer temperature readings are less diagnostic than winter readings. In ambiguous cases, rely more on pulse rate.

> "Some people wake up with a 98 or 99-degree temperature, but after they eat breakfast, by 10:00 or 11:00 AM, their temperature might fall to 94 or 95 degrees." -- 2013-11-15 Ask the Herb Doctor: Hashimoto's, Antibodies, Temperature and Pulse

> "In hot, humid summer weather, hypothyroid people could maintain a normal core temperature, but often their pulse rate would still be low -- 45 to 65 beats per minute." -- 2011-02-22 EastWest Healing

### Connection to Blood Markers

- **TSH:** Temperature below 97.4F waking correlates with functional hypothyroidism regardless of TSH. TSH can be "normal" or even suppressed in a hypothyroid person because stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline, estrogen) suppress TSH. Temperature is more reliable than TSH for this reason.
- **Free T3:** Post-breakfast temperature rise confirms T4-to-T3 conversion is occurring in the liver. Failure to rise suggests conversion impairment, even if Free T3 blood level looks adequate.
- **Cholesterol:** High cholesterol is a "mirror image of thyroid function." When someone has low temperature AND high cholesterol, the mechanism is clear: inadequate thyroid is failing to convert cholesterol to pregnenolone and progesterone. People with cholesterol of 450-500 have seen it drop to normal within a week with thyroid supplementation.
- **Cortisol:** Low morning temperature combined with waking at 2-4 AM suggests cortisol is rising to compensate for inadequate thyroid. "Waking temp ~97.8F rising to 98.6F after breakfast; pulse ~80-85 bpm" -- when these are present, cortisol is not needed as a compensatory mechanism.
- **Estrogen:** "Temperature is the quickest way to assess estrogen status because estrogen lowers your temperature. Thyroid and progesterone and DHEA help to maintain a good, steady high metabolic rate."

## Pulse Protocol

### How to Measure

Measure while seated and calm, at least 10 minutes after any activity. Place two fingers on the radial artery (inside of the wrist) or carotid artery (side of neck). Count beats for 60 seconds. Measure alongside hand temperature -- this distinguishes thyroid-driven pulse from adrenaline-driven pulse. Measure at the same times as temperature: upon waking, 30-60 minutes after breakfast, and mid-afternoon.

### Target Values

- **Resting daytime:** 75-85 bpm. This is Peat's optimal range. Below 70 suggests low thyroid or metabolic suppression. Below 60 is concerning. Above 90 at rest may indicate overtreatment with thyroid or acute stress.
- **After breakfast:** Ideally, pulse should come DOWN slightly while temperature goes UP. "When the temperature goes up and pulse goes down, that's what breakfast should do." This pattern indicates that carbohydrate lowered adrenaline while thyroid activated metabolism.
- **Athletic bradycardia (40-50 bpm):** Peat rejected the mainstream view that low resting pulse in athletes indicates fitness. "I've known people who had 40 or 50 pulse at rest and who were really very miserable. And when they got their pulse up to 70 or 80 at rest by taking thyroid, they felt much better."

### What the Values Mean

**Low pulse (below 70 bpm):** In most non-athletes, indicates metabolic suppression. The heart is not receiving enough thyroid stimulation to maintain adequate cardiac output. This correlates with cold extremities, fatigue, and slow cognition.

**High pulse with warm hands:** Likely thyroid-driven -- adequate or slightly high metabolic rate. Not a concern unless accompanied by tremor, insomnia, or excessive sweating.

**High pulse with cold hands:** This is the critical warning sign. "If your hands are cold while your heart rate increases, you're running on adrenaline, which is not good. Your hands should be warm." Adrenaline gives a short cardiac stroke with compensatory fast rate -- inefficient and stressful. Thyroid-driven warmth produces even peripheral vasodilation; adrenaline produces peripheral vasoconstriction with tachycardia. This single check distinguishes healthy metabolic rate from stress-compensated metabolic rate.

**Pulse rises after eating:** If pulse goes UP after eating (while temperature drops or stays flat), the pre-meal state was not stress-driven -- the food is providing substrate for an already-active metabolism. But if pulse was HIGH before eating and DROPS after eating while temperature also drops, the pre-meal state was entirely adrenaline-driven.

> "Resting pulse should be 75-85 bpm during the day. Below 70 suggests low thyroid or metabolic suppression." -- wiki/substances/drugs/thyroid-supplement.md; 2019-11-12 Jodellefit

> "If your hands are cold while your heart rate increases, you're running on adrenaline, which is not good. Your hands should be warm." -- One Radio Network: Fats and Questions (2019-01-24)

> "When the temperature goes up and pulse goes down, that's what breakfast should do." -- 2013-11-15 Ask the Herb Doctor: Hashimoto's, Antibodies, Temperature and Pulse

### Connection to Blood Markers

- **Thyroid markers (TSH, T3, T4):** Pulse below 70 correlates with functional hypothyroidism. But TSH can be misleadingly low when stress hormones suppress it. Pulse is the more reliable indicator. "Healthy people have the lowest TSH, but stress hormones can also lower it. You need to check your temperature, heart rate, and T3 levels."
- **Adrenaline / Cortisol:** High pulse with cold hands = adrenaline-driven. Chronic adrenaline elevation indicates metabolic failure, not vitality. When cells have adequate glucose, thyroid, and oxygen, they do not need adrenaline.
- **CO2 / Bicarbonate:** Low CO2 often accompanies low pulse -- both reflect inadequate oxidative metabolism. Cells that cannot burn glucose efficiently produce less CO2 and generate less heat.
- **Free fatty acids:** Elevated FFAs activate adrenaline, ACTH, cortisol, TSH, and glucagon. "Only polyunsaturated fatty acids have the terrible anti-energy effects." If pulse is high but hands are cold, PUFA-driven FFA release may be driving the adrenaline response.

## Combined Interpretation

The power of pulse and temperature is in reading them TOGETHER, especially before and after eating. Here are the four patterns:

### Pattern 1: Healthy Metabolism
- **Waking:** Temp ~97.8F, pulse 70-80 bpm, hands warm
- **After breakfast:** Temp rises to 98.6F, pulse stays at 75-85 or drops slightly
- **Meaning:** Thyroid-driven metabolism. Liver converting T4 to T3. No excessive stress hormone compensation.
- **Blood markers expected:** TSH low (<1.0), cholesterol 160-220, CO2 26-30+, low cortisol, low FFA

### Pattern 2: Stress-Compensated Hypothyroidism (Most Common)
- **Waking:** Temp 98-99F (looks good), pulse 80-95+ (looks good), hands COLD
- **After breakfast:** Temp FALLS to 95-97F, pulse may drop too
- **Meaning:** Morning readings were artificially elevated by cortisol and adrenaline. Food reduces stress hormones, revealing the underlying metabolic deficit. "If your temperature and pulse rate are slower after eating, that means you're under heavy stress during the night."
- **Blood markers expected:** TSH may be "normal" or even low (suppressed by stress hormones), cholesterol elevated, reverse T3 elevated, FFA elevated, cortisol elevated morning

### Pattern 3: Frank Hypothyroidism
- **Waking:** Temp below 97F, pulse 50-65 bpm, hands cold
- **After breakfast:** Temp rises slightly or does not change, pulse rises slightly
- **Meaning:** Insufficient thyroid hormone for basic metabolic function. Not enough stress hormone compensation either (or adrenals are depleted).
- **Blood markers expected:** TSH elevated (>2.0), cholesterol >220, CO2 low (<24), albumin potentially declining, prolactin rising

### Pattern 4: Adrenaline-Driven (Exercise, Fasting, PUFA)
- **Resting:** Pulse 90+ bpm, hands and feet cold, core temperature unstable
- **After eating sugar or orange juice:** Pulse drops 10-20 bpm, hands warm, feeling of relief
- **Meaning:** Running on stress hormones. Blood sugar unstable. Liver glycogen depleted. Likely high PUFA tissue stores driving FFA release.
- **Blood markers expected:** High FFA, high reverse T3, high cortisol, low CO2, potentially "normal" TSH (suppressed by stress)

> "The adrenaline will make your skin cold to increase your core temperature. So it can be misleading. Unless you check all of those things... if your temperature and pulse rate are slower [after eating], that means you're under heavy stress during the night." -- One Radio Network (2019-01-24)

> "After breakfast, temperature should reach 37C / 98.6F and pulse should be about 80-85 bpm." -- wiki/substances/drugs/thyroid-supplement.md

## Additional Functional Indicators

Beyond temperature and pulse, Peat listed several other functional signs that supplement the diagnostic picture:

- **Warmth of hands and feet:** Should be warm at all times. Cold extremities with fast pulse = adrenaline-driven metabolism.
- **Sleep quality (especially 2-4 AM waking):** "Insomnia is a daytime disorder presenting at night." Waking at 2-4 AM indicates nocturnal hypoglycemia triggering adrenaline and cortisol. The liver ran out of glycogen.
- **Cholesterol level:** High cholesterol = low thyroid. Cholesterol above 200 often reflects inadequate thyroid driving cholesterol-to-hormone conversion.
- **Achilles tendon reflex speed:** A hypothyroid person's foot returns slowly like a pneumatic door closer; good thyroid produces a foot that bounces back like rubber.
- **Water evaporation rate:** A person should evaporate about 2 quarts of water per day (insensible loss). Many hypothyroid people evaporate less than a quart, meaning they are waterlogged.
- **Bowel transit time and digestion:** Regular, well-formed bowel movements without bloating. Impaired digestion allows endotoxin to enter the bloodstream.
- **Hair quality and skin thickness:** Thin, fragile skin indicates cortisol excess. Hair loss correlates with high prolactin and low thyroid.
- **Mental clarity and energy stability between meals:** Needing to eat every 2-3 hours indicates reliance on stress hormones. Adequate liver glycogen should provide stable glucose for 4-5 hours.
- **Carotene levels:** Elevated carotene (orange palms) indicates impaired conversion to Vitamin A -- a classic hypothyroid sign.

> "Other indicators include cholesterol (high cholesterol often means low thyroid), carotene levels, and the speed of muscle relaxation (Achilles tendon reflex). Quick relaxation corresponds to good thyroid function." -- It's Rainmaking Time (2014-06-02); wiki/mechanisms/thyroid-function.md

## Using Temperature and Pulse to Guide Thyroid Dosing

Peat and those following his approach used temperature and pulse to adjust thyroid supplementation rather than relying on blood tests:

- **Starting protocol for T3 (Cynomel):** 5-10 mcg/day in Week 1. Increase by 5 mcg every 3-5 days if temperature remains low. Maintenance: 15-50 mcg/day in divided doses.
- **The goal:** Waking temperature approaching 97.8F, rising to 98.6F after breakfast, resting pulse around 80-85 bpm.
- **Signs of adequate dose:** Temperature and pulse in target range, warm hands, stable energy, no signs of hyperthyroidism (tremor, palpitations, insomnia, excessive sweating).
- **Signs of overdose:** Tremor, insomnia, excessive sweating, pulse consistently above 90 with restlessness.
- **Critical insight:** "In extremely adrenergic states, T3 causes both temperature and heart rate to decrease as it restores metabolic efficiency." When someone is running on adrenaline, adding thyroid LOWERS pulse and temperature by removing the need for stress compensation.
- **The rule:** "If patient feels better with normal temp/pulse and no hyper signs, they're not overdosed" regardless of what TSH says.

> "Use temperature and heart rate to judge thyroid supplement dosage." -- wiki/conditions/hypothyroidism.md

> "In extremely adrenergic states, T3 causes both temperature and heart rate to decrease as it restores metabolic efficiency." -- thyroid-raw, claim 8.12

## When Blood Tests Add Value

Peat did not reject blood tests entirely. He identified specific situations where they add information beyond what pulse and temperature reveal:

1. **Vitamin D (25-OH):** One of the few tests Peat specifically recommended. Target 40-60 ng/mL, with ~50 ng/mL as the sweet spot. "You want to periodically check, have a blood test to see if your vitamin D and thyroid hormone is in the right range." Low Vitamin D is the most common finding correlated with PTH excess.

2. **Free T3 and Free T4:** Useful when symptoms are ambiguous -- especially to check T4-to-T3 conversion ratio. But "numbers should never override clinical presentation."

3. **Total cholesterol:** Functions as a thyroid proxy. Above 220 with low temperature confirms hypothyroidism. Below 160 with low thyroid is especially dangerous -- insufficient precursor for protective steroids.

4. **Albumin:** At 4.9-5.0 g/dL or higher, albumin reflects adequate liver function and nutritional status. "Every point that your albumin drops below 5.0, your mortality rate doubles."

5. **Prolactin:** A useful stress indicator. If prolactin is 18-20 ng/mL, "everything is under stress." Prolactin and TSH rise together under hypothyroidism.

6. **PTH:** Rarely done but extremely relevant. Reflects calcium/Vitamin D/phosphate balance. PTH directly poisons mitochondrial function.

7. **Free fatty acids:** "Rarely tested but extremely important." Elevated FFA indicates stress-driven lipolysis. The composition (PUFA vs saturated) determines toxicity.

**Limitations of functional indicators:** Temperature and pulse cannot tell you specific nutrient levels (Vitamin D, iron status), cannot distinguish between types of hormonal dysfunction (adrenal vs thyroid vs gonadal), and cannot detect early-stage conditions before they affect metabolic rate. A person could have dangerously low Vitamin D with adequate temperature if thyroid is compensating. Blood tests fill these specific gaps.

**The hierarchy holds:** Start with temperature and pulse. If they are normal and the person feels well, blood tests are unlikely to reveal problems. If they are abnormal, blood tests help identify which upstream problem (thyroid, nutrients, hormones) is driving the deficit. "The lived experience of metabolism trumps the laboratory abstraction."

> "Use temperature and heart rate to judge thyroid supplement dosage." -- wiki/conditions/hypothyroidism.md

> "When temperature and pulse rise appropriately, energy is stable, digestion efficient, and body temperature comfortable, thyroid function is adequate regardless of lab values." -- wiki/mechanisms/thyroid-function.md

## Enrichment Additions (2026-03-17)

### Peat's Personal Pulse Target (Specific Numbers)

Peat personally targeted a resting pulse "averaging a little over 90" for the last 35 years. He recommended 85 bpm as a good average for the general population, with his personal practice running higher.

For therapeutic purposes (treating tumors, cataracts, chronic problems), some people have held resting pulse at 100+ bpm for months without harmful effects. The safety margin is wide when the pulse is metabolically driven rather than adrenaline-driven.

> "I think 85/minute resting is a good average. For the last 35 years I have tried to keep it averaging a little over 90." -- email: pulse.md

### Distinguishing Metabolic vs. Adrenaline Pulse

An adrenaline-driven pulse stays variable and reactive -- it spikes with standing, stress, or startle. A metabolically-driven pulse from good thyroid function stays close to the same rate all day while sitting. This stability is the key diagnostic marker.

> "[Pulse rate driven by adrenaline?] If it's from your basic metabolism, it will stay close to that all day while sitting." -- email: pulse.md

### Hypothyroid Adrenaline Compensation

Hypothyroid adrenaline production can be 30-40 times higher than normal. This means chronically elevated heart rates (150+ bpm) can paradoxically indicate hypothyroidism, not hyperthyroidism. These patients normalize within days of starting thyroid supplementation, as the thyroid reduces the need for compensatory adrenaline.

> "Occasionally, a person who has chronically had a heart rate of 150 beats per minute or higher, will have a much lower heart rate after using a thyroid supplement for a few days." -- newsletter: mary-shomon-interview.md

> "The daily production of adrenalin is sometimes 30 or 40 times higher than normal in hypothyroidism." -- newsletter: mary-shomon-interview.md

### Population Temperature Decline Data

Average normal body temperature has dropped by 1.06°F in men and 0.58°F in women since Fahrenheit's era. This decline parallels increases in many serious diseases, but there is virtually no scientific curiosity about whether the trends are related.

> "At present, the temperature of young men averages 1.06 degrees F lower, and that of young women, 0.58 degree F lower." -- newsletter: body-temperature-inflammation-and-aging.md

### The Daytime Temperature Rise as Primary Diagnostic

The temperature rise during the day is the most important diagnostic measure, since nocturnal stress hormones can give a misleading morning impression. A person may wake with artificially elevated temperature from cortisol/adrenaline, which falls after breakfast as stress hormones are replaced by metabolic drive.

> "The temperature rise during the day is the most important thing, since nocturnal stress hormones can give a misleading impression in the morning. Resting pulse rate is another good indicator." -- email: thyroid.md

### Low Temperature and Reactive Oxygen Species

When temperature is lowered, reactive oxygen species (superoxide) increase -- mitochondrial production increases slightly, but elimination decreases greatly. This means low body temperature is not merely a symptom but an active driver of oxidative damage. The "rate of living" theory (that faster metabolism = faster aging) is backwards: higher metabolic rate produces more CO2 and fewer reactive oxygen species.

> "When the body temperature is lowered, reactive oxygen species such as superoxide increase; mitochondrial production of superoxide increases slightly, elimination decreases greatly." -- newsletter: body-temperature-inflammation-and-aging.md

### Nutritional Thermogenic Factors

Substances and nutrients that support temperature include: sodium, calcium, vitamin D, carbohydrates (especially sugar), protein, thyroid hormone, and progesterone. Aspirin is also thermogenic (stimulates mitochondrial oxygen consumption) despite its anti-fever reputation. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is hypothermic and should be avoided.

> "Nutritional thermogenic factors include sodium, calcium, vitamin D, carbohydrates, especially sugar, and protein, which interact with our endogenous energy regulating factors, especially thyroid and progesterone." -- newsletter: body-temperature-inflammation-and-aging.md

### Additional Functional Indicators from Enrichment

**Finger-to-Mouth Temperature Differential:** Checking temperature of hands, feet, and comparing to oral temperature provides a rapid assessment of peripheral circulation. Cold extremities with warm core = adrenaline-driven vasoconstriction. The temperature of the extremities, combined with the water evaporation rate, are better indicators than initial pulse readings for judging thyroid effect.

**Water Evaporation Rate:** A hypothyroid person evaporates less water per day than normal. This is measurable by weighing before and after a period of rest. Low evaporation = waterlogged, hypothyroid.

### Nocturnal Monitoring Protocol

Nocturnal metabolic monitoring is clinically important because darkness itself triggers a catabolic cascade (Thread 19 in metabolic-threads.md):

1. **2-4 AM waking test:** If patient wakes and eats sugar + salt, return to sleep within minutes confirms nocturnal hypoglycemia
2. **Morning hair loss:** Copious morning hair loss corresponds to nocturnal PTH surges
3. **Morning temperature context:** Low morning temperature after good sleep = genuine hypothyroidism. High morning temperature after poor sleep = cortisol/adrenaline compensation (test will correct after eating)

**Bedtime protocol for nocturnal protection:**
- 5-10 mcg T3 at bedtime (produces sleep within minutes)
- Glass of milk or calcium supplement (suppress nocturnal PTH)
- Salty snack (suppress aldosterone)
- Ice cream or sugar/fat combination (maintain blood glucose through the night)

> "Calcium and vitamin D, sufficient to keep parathyroid hormone low, contribute to that process. Salty snacks are especially helpful for bringing on sleep... 5 or 10 mcg of the immediately active T3 at bedtime will usually produce sleep within a few minutes." -- newsletter: sleep-and-aging.md

### High Altitude Thyroid Data

In high altitude populations: TSH is reduced by more than 25%, T3 is 4.5x higher, and T4 is 3x higher. These populations show greater longevity and better health. This data provides a reference point for what "optimal" thyroid function looks like in a population not suppressed by sea-level environmental and dietary stressors.

> "In one study (Alhazmi, et al., 2018), T3 was four and a half times higher in people living at a high altitude, T4 was about three times higher, and TSH was reduced by more than 25%." -- newsletter: body-temperature-inflammation-and-aging.md
