# Women's Health & Cycle Timing -- Lab Interpretation for Women

Peat's framework for interpreting women's lab work centers on one principle: the ratio of progesterone to estrogen determines health outcomes more than the absolute level of either hormone. Every lab marker in a menstruating woman must be interpreted against her cycle day, and many markers that appear "normal" in isolation are pathological when the cycle phase is considered.

---

## The Critical Question

"What cycle day was the draw?" is the single most important question when interpreting a woman's hormone panel. Peat always asked this because:

1. **Progesterone is nearly absent in the follicular phase** (days 1-14) and dominant in the luteal phase (days 15-28). Drawing blood on day 5 vs. day 21 produces completely different progesterone values -- both "normal" for their phase but with opposite clinical implications.

2. **Estrogen peaks twice** -- once at ovulation and once in the mid-luteal phase -- meaning a single draw can catch a peak or trough that misrepresents the patient's typical estrogen exposure.

3. **The P:E ratio shifts dramatically** across the cycle, and it is this ratio -- not either hormone alone -- that determines whether the woman is in a protective (progesterone-dominant) or stressed (estrogen-dominant) state.

4. **Thyroid markers are affected by estrogen levels.** "Women have 5 or 10 times as much thyroid disease as men. The reason is that estrogen interferes with the liver's ability to convert thyroxine (T4) to the active T3 hormone." A thyroid draw during high-estrogen phases will show worse T3 conversion than the same woman at a low-estrogen phase.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claims 2.13-2.14, 2.32); thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims on estrogen-thyroid interaction)

## Cycle Phase Effects on Markers

### Follicular Phase (Days 1-14)

During the follicular phase, the corpus luteum from the previous cycle has regressed and the new follicle has not yet produced significant progesterone. This is a relatively estrogen-dominant phase.

**Hormone markers:**
- **Progesterone:** Very low (typically <1 ng/mL). This is normal for this phase. A progesterone draw during the follicular phase tells you almost nothing about the woman's luteal function.
- **Estrogen (Estradiol):** Rises steadily from days 1-12, peaking just before ovulation. Early follicular E2 is the closest to a "baseline" estrogen measurement.
- **FSH/LH:** FSH rises early to stimulate follicular development. LH remains low until the ovulatory surge. Peat noted that stress suppresses LH specifically while raising other pituitary hormones, so a low LH in a stressed woman during this phase may indicate suppressed ovulation ahead.
- **Prolactin:** Should be low. Elevated prolactin in the follicular phase is a stress marker and may indicate hypothyroidism. "Prolactin and TSH tend to increase together."
- **TSH:** Estrogen's interference with T4-to-T3 conversion means TSH may be slightly elevated during higher-estrogen portions of the follicular phase. A TSH drawn on day 3 (low estrogen) may look better than the same woman's TSH on day 12 (rising estrogen).

**Metabolic markers affected:**
- **Cholesterol:** May be slightly lower during high-estrogen phases as estrogen drives cholesterol conversion (though Peat viewed this as estrogen forcing metabolic activity, not necessarily beneficial).
- **Temperature/pulse:** May run slightly lower in the follicular phase due to relative progesterone absence. This is normal and does not necessarily indicate hypothyroidism if it corrects in the luteal phase.

**Best time to draw:** Early follicular (days 2-5) gives the most "baseline" picture for estrogen and pituitary hormones. However, progesterone drawn at this time is uninformative.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claims 2.13-2.14; Prolactin section, claim 7.2; FSH/LH section, claim 13.1)

### Ovulation (Day ~14)

The LH surge triggers ovulation and the formation of the corpus luteum.

**Marker shifts at ovulation:**
- **LH:** Surges sharply (this is the only time LH is reliably high). Peat noted that stress suppresses LH, so a blunted or absent LH surge = anovulatory cycle. "Stress induces all pituitary hormones except for luteinizing hormone, the one that produces testosterone and progesterone."
- **Estrogen:** Peaks just before the LH surge. This is the cycle's highest estrogen point (outside pregnancy). Drawing estrogen here will give the highest reading.
- **Progesterone:** Begins rising immediately after ovulation as the corpus luteum forms.
- **Temperature:** Classic basal body temperature shift -- a rise of 0.3-0.5F confirms ovulation occurred. Peat considered this temperature shift a more reliable ovulation confirmation than LH testing.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (FSH/LH section, claim 13.1; Estrogen section, claim 2.13)

### Luteal Phase (Days 15-28)

The corpus luteum produces progesterone, reaching peak production around days 19-22. This is the phase that reveals whether the woman's hormonal balance is adequate.

**Hormone markers:**
- **Progesterone:** Should be high -- peak luteal progesterone is the most diagnostically valuable single hormone measurement in a menstruating woman. "A woman produces maybe 30 milligrams a day during the premenstrual time." Serum levels of 5-20 ng/mL are the mainstream range, but Peat focused on the ratio to estrogen rather than absolute values.
- **Estrogen:** Has a secondary rise in the mid-luteal phase. The crucial question is whether progesterone is high enough to dominate.
- **The P:E Ratio:** "Fertility requires 50 to 100 times as much progesterone as estrogen." This ratio, established in animal research, matches human fertility clinic outcomes. When progesterone fails to dominate by at least 50:1, the woman is in a state of relative estrogen dominance regardless of what her absolute numbers show.
- **Prolactin:** Should remain low. Elevated luteal prolactin indicates stress and predicts poor corpus luteum function. "Prolactin is a good indicator of stress on a lab panel."
- **TSH:** May rise in the luteal phase if progesterone is inadequate and estrogen dominates, impairing hepatic T4-to-T3 conversion.

**When to draw progesterone:** Days 19-22 (approximately 5-7 days after ovulation) gives peak corpus luteum function. This is the only time a progesterone draw is diagnostically meaningful.

**Liver adaptation note:** "When you use progesterone all month, the liver develops the ability to excrete it quickly over the first two weeks. The natural cycle has a rest of two weeks where the liver resets itself." This is why Peat recommended cycling progesterone supplements in women -- two weeks on, two weeks off -- matching the natural pattern.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Progesterone section, claims 3.5, 3.14; Estrogen section, claims 2.13, 2.32; Prolactin section, claim 7.9)

### Menstruation

**Iron loss as protective factor:**

Peat's view on menstruation inverted the standard medical concern about iron loss:

"Women generally live longer than men and have lower rates of heart disease and cancer. The standard explanation invokes estrogen's 'protective' effects. Peat's simpler explanation: women menstruate, losing iron regularly, while men accumulate it progressively. After menopause, women's disease rates converge with men's partly because iron loss ceases."

Monthly blood loss removes approximately 30-40 mg of iron per cycle. This prevents the progressive iron accumulation that catalyzes PUFA oxidation, free radical generation, and lipofuscin formation. In Peat's framework, menstruation is not a metabolic cost -- it is a built-in protective mechanism against iron toxicity.

**Implication for lab work:** A menstruating woman's ferritin will naturally be lower than a man's. This is protective, not pathological. Clinicians who aggressively supplement iron to raise ferritin in menstruating women are, in Peat's view, eliminating a natural defense.

**Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Hematocrit/Hemoglobin section, claims 73, 78); iron-inflammation-raw (Iron dietary section, claims 89-92)

## The Estrogen/Progesterone Ratio

This is the single most important concept in Peat's framework for women's health.

### The Master Framework

Peat did not view estrogen and progesterone as "female sex hormones" with complementary roles. He viewed them as metabolic opponents:

- **Estrogen** is a stress hormone that promotes cell proliferation, water retention, glycolysis, inflammation, and excitotoxicity. "Estrogen should be understood as a stress hormone, not a sex hormone."
- **Progesterone** is the primary protective hormone that opposes estrogen at every level: it destroys the estrogen receptor, activates estrogen-clearing enzymes, stabilizes cell membranes, supports oxidative metabolism, and protects the brain. "Progesterone knocks out estrogen in roughly ten different ways."

### The Ratio in Practice

- **Fertility:** Requires P:E ratio of 50:1 to 100:1.
- **General health:** Progesterone should dominate at all times except during the early follicular phase.
- **How to assess without precise numbers:** Temperature is the quickest proxy. "Your temperature is the quickest way, because estrogen lowers your temperature. Thyroid and progesterone and DHEA help to maintain a good, steady high metabolic rate." A woman with a consistently low temperature (below 97.8F waking) and cold hands is likely estrogen-dominant regardless of what her lab numbers show.

### When the Ratio Fails

Estrogen dominance -- whether from insufficient progesterone production, impaired estrogen clearance, or environmental estrogen exposure -- produces a cascade:

1. Estrogen blocks hepatic T4-to-T3 conversion, creating functional hypothyroidism
2. Hypothyroidism slows cholesterol-to-pregnenolone conversion, reducing raw material for progesterone
3. Lower progesterone means less estrogen opposition, worsening dominance
4. Estrogen activates aromatase, converting testosterone and DHEA to more estrogen
5. Elevated estrogen raises prolactin, cortisol, and serotonin

This is a self-reinforcing cycle. Peat's therapeutic approach was to break it at multiple points simultaneously: thyroid support, progesterone supplementation (cyclically), dietary carbohydrate to reduce cortisol, and raw carrot/fiber to clear intestinal estrogen.

## Menopause

### How to Interpret Markers Post-Menopause

Peat rejected the standard "estrogen deficiency" model of menopause:

"Symptoms attributed to 'estrogen deficiency' at menopause -- hot flashes, bone loss, cognitive decline -- are better explained by low thyroid and progesterone, which declining estrogen merely unmasked."

Key points for post-menopausal interpretation:

1. **Serum estrogen drops, but tissue estrogen increases.** "Serum estrogen drops with age, but tissue estrogen actually increases." Blood tests showing "low estrogen" can be completely misleading because estrogen trapped in tissues continues exerting effects without appearing in serum. Fat tissue, breast tissue, and brain tissue produce their own estrogen via aromatase. Post-menopausal women have RISING tissue estrogen despite falling serum estrogen.

2. **In rat studies, when ovaries are removed, rats return to normal estrogen levels within a week** -- but without progesterone. "Sterility in mid-lifespan is caused by unopposed estrogen, not estrogen deficiency." The real menopausal loss is progesterone, not estrogen.

3. **Prolactin rises steadily with aging** and accelerates after menopause. "Prolactin is a stress-reacting hormone that increases steadily with aging." This is pathological, not normal. Post-menopausal prolactin should still be monitored and kept low.

4. **Iron accumulation accelerates** after menstruation stops. "After menopause, women's disease rates converge with men's partly because iron loss ceases." Post-menopausal women should monitor transferrin saturation and consider blood donation.

5. **PTH tends to rise** as calcium absorption declines and vitamin D synthesis decreases with age. "Estrogen promotes calcium loss from bone and increases PTH secretion. Progesterone reduces PTH and improves calcium retention." Post-menopausal women need higher calcium intake (1.5-2g daily) and vitamin D maintenance.

### Peat's Views on HRT

Peat was emphatically against estrogen replacement therapy:

- "A shot of estrogen can cause immediate psychosis. Contraceptive estrogen was directly linked to strokes in young women."
- "Continuous uninterrupted estrogen exposure follows a carcinogenic sequence: first uterine cancer, then breast, then lungs, kidneys, intestine, liver, and brain."
- "Birth control pills cause an epidemic of prolactin-secreting pituitary tumors."

He was emphatically in favor of progesterone replacement:

- "A 52-year-old woman with 'hopeless' dementia from epilepsy recovered completely after absorbing progesterone through her arthritic fingers."
- In late pregnancy, the placenta produces 800-900 mg/day of progesterone, demonstrating its enormous safety margin.
- Therapeutic doses of 10-30 mg were his general recommendation; 100 mg for acute conditions like migraines.
- Synthetic progestins (medroxyprogesterone) are NOT progesterone and should be avoided entirely. "The additives attached to the progesterone molecule specifically block its conversion into that natural range of other steroids."

**Post-menopausal women do not need to cycle progesterone** because there is no follicular phase to rest during. Continuous low-dose progesterone (10-30 mg) is appropriate.

## Birth Control Effects

Peat viewed oral contraceptives as a significant source of iatrogenic disease. Their effects on lab markers:

### Estrogen-Related Effects
- **Prolactin:** Chronic elevation from contraceptive estrogen drives prolactinoma formation. "There has been an epidemic of prolactin-secreting pituitary tumors from contraceptive estrogen."
- **TSH / Thyroid:** Exogenous estrogen impairs hepatic T4-to-T3 conversion. Women on oral contraceptives may show "normal" TSH with functional hypothyroidism. The 5-10x higher rate of thyroid disease in women is partly attributable to estrogen exposure from contraceptives.
- **Cholesterol:** May be artificially lowered by contraceptive estrogen's effect on liver metabolism, masking underlying thyroid issues.
- **Cortisol:** "Estrogen increases cortisol production through three pathways simultaneously: directly on the adrenals, through pituitary stimulation, and through hypothalamic CRH release." Women on OCs may show chronically elevated cortisol.
- **SHBG:** Rises with oral contraceptive use (estrogen stimulates SHBG production). While this sequesters estrogen (protective), it also sequesters testosterone, potentially causing low libido and metabolic changes.
- **Clotting risk:** Contraceptive estrogen was "directly linked to strokes in young women" -- pro-clotting effects are dose-dependent but present at all doses.

### Progesterone-Related Effects
- **Endogenous progesterone production:** Suppressed by contraceptive feedback on the HPO axis. After stopping OCs, progesterone production may take months to recover.
- **Synthetic progestins vs. progesterone:** Most OCs contain synthetic progestins, not progesterone. These "specifically block conversion into the natural range of other steroids" and "probably the worst effect of the synthetic progestins is that they interfere with brain function."

### Recovery After Stopping
- Peat recommended thyroid support, progesterone supplementation (cyclically), pregnenolone, and time for the HPO axis to recover. Temperature and pulse monitoring are more reliable than lab work during the transition period.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claims 2.14-2.16, 2.30; Progesterone section, claim 3.7; Prolactin section, claim 7.2)

## Pregnancy

The extraction corpus contains limited pregnancy-specific claims, but several are significant:

1. **Progesterone production in pregnancy:** "In the last week or two of a healthy pregnancy, a woman's placenta is producing 800 or 900 milligrams of progesterone per day. That doesn't do anything harmful; it is very protective." This demonstrates progesterone's enormous safety margin and explains why pregnant women are relatively protected from many diseases.

2. **Prenatal progesterone and intelligence:** Katharina Dalton treated premenstrual syndrome with progesterone injections continuing through pregnancy. "Her patients' babies tended to average 130 IQ after progesterone treatment. The intelligence of the babies corresponded to the amount of progesterone she gave the mothers." The working-class mothers' expected average IQ was ~90.

3. **Eclampsia and sodium/magnesium:** "In eclampsia, as sodium is lost, adrenals increase aldosterone to retain sodium, but at the expense of losing magnesium faster. Increasing sodium intake turns off aldosterone and stops magnesium loss." This contradicts the standard recommendation to restrict sodium in pre-eclampsia.

4. **Stress protection:** "Pregnant rats with adrenals removed survived stresses that killed non-pregnant adrenalectomized rats." Progesterone alone provided complete stress protection without any cortisol -- suggesting that high-progesterone states (pregnancy) provide extraordinary stress resilience.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Progesterone section, claims 3.3, 3.9, 3.11; Aldosterone section, claim 9.4)

## Markers That Don't Change With Cycle

The following markers can be drawn at any point in the menstrual cycle without significant cycle-dependent variation:

| Marker | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **CO2 / Bicarbonate** | Reflects metabolic efficiency; cycle-independent |
| **Albumin** | Liver synthetic function; not hormone-dependent |
| **Transferrin saturation** | Iron status; not cycle-dependent (though acute blood loss from menstruation temporarily affects iron markers -- draw at least 5 days after period ends) |
| **Vitamin D (25-OH)** | Seasonal but not cycle-dependent |
| **PTH** | Calcium/D/phosphate balance; not directly cycle-dependent |
| **Lactate** | Metabolic marker; cycle-independent |
| **Total cholesterol** | Relatively stable across cycle (slight estrogen-dependent variation but not clinically significant) |
| **Free fatty acids** | Stress marker; cycle-independent |
| **Calcium, magnesium, sodium, potassium** | Electrolytes; not meaningfully cycle-dependent |
| **Liver enzymes (ALT, AST)** | Organ function; cycle-independent |
| **CBC (WBC, RBC, platelets)** | Minimally affected by cycle |
| **BUN / Creatinine** | Kidney function; cycle-independent |

**Markers that MUST account for cycle day:**

| Marker | When to Draw | Why |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| **Progesterone** | Days 19-22 (luteal peak) | Only meaningful at peak corpus luteum function |
| **Estradiol** | Day 3 for baseline; day 21 for luteal context | Peaks at ovulation and mid-luteal |
| **FSH / LH** | Day 3 for baseline | Only meaningful before follicle selection |
| **Prolactin** | Any day, but note cycle day | Slightly affected by estrogen; interpret in context |
| **TSH** | Note cycle day | Estrogen impairs T4-to-T3 conversion; early follicular is least confounded |
| **Cortisol** | Any day, but note cycle day | Estrogen raises cortisol; early follicular gives lowest baseline |

**Sources:** Compiled from cross-referencing all six extraction files for cycle-dependent vs. cycle-independent marker behavior. hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claims 2.14-2.15, 2.32; Progesterone section, claim 3.5); thyroid-raw (estrogen-thyroid interactions); cbc-organ-raw (electrolyte sections); lipid-metabolic-raw (metabolic marker sections)

## Enrichment Additions (2026-03-17)

### Hepcidin-Hormonal Iron Regulation (New Section)

The enrichment reveals a critical hormonal regulatory layer in iron metabolism that is especially important for interpreting women's iron panels.

#### The Hepcidin Axis

Hepcidin is the master regulator of intestinal iron absorption:
- **Estrogen suppresses hepcidin** → increases iron absorption from food
- **Progesterone stimulates hepcidin** → decreases iron absorption from food

This means iron absorption rates change across the menstrual cycle, with pregnancy, and at menopause -- creating clinical implications not captured by standard iron panel interpretation.

#### Key Numbers

- Women absorb **3x as much iron** from an identical meal compared to men
- During pregnancy (high estrogen): absorption increases to **9x** the male rate
- Heavy menstruation loses only **30-40 mg of iron per month** (~1 mg/day) -- "practically impossible NOT to get from diet"

> "While estrogen decreases blood formation, it simultaneously increases iron absorption from food -- women absorb 3x as much iron as men from a similar meal, and during pregnancy (high estrogen) 9x as much." -- newsletter: estrogen-iron-degenerative-aging-and-progesterone.md

#### The Estrogen-Iron Paradox

Estrogen simultaneously:
1. **Suppresses bone marrow** → lower hemoglobin (appears "anemic")
2. **Suppresses hepcidin** → increased iron absorption
3. **Causes water retention** → dilutes blood (lower hematocrit)

Result: a woman can appear anemic on standard labs while accumulating excess tissue iron. This is the core interpretive trap in women's iron panels.

#### Cycle-Phase Iron Interpretation

| Phase | Hepcidin Status | Iron Absorption | Clinical Implication |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Follicular (days 1-14)** | Rising estrogen → hepcidin falling | Increasing absorption | Iron absorption accelerating toward ovulation |
| **Luteal (days 15-28)** | Progesterone rising → hepcidin rising | Decreasing absorption | Natural brake on iron absorption |
| **Menstruation** | Progesterone falls, estrogen low | Moderate | Iron lost through blood offsets absorption |
| **Post-menopause** | Estrogen from aromatase only, no progesterone | Hepcidin brake lost | Accelerating tissue iron accumulation |
| **Pregnancy** | Very high estrogen | 9x male rate | Maximum absorption to support fetal development |

#### Clinical Implications

1. **Menstruating women with low hemoglobin but adequate energy:** May not need iron supplementation. Their low hemoglobin may reflect estrogen's bone marrow suppression and blood dilution, not true iron deficiency. Tissue iron may be adequate or even excessive.

2. **Post-menopausal women:** The loss of progesterone removes the hepcidin brake on iron absorption. Combined with cessation of menstrual iron loss, tissue iron accumulates rapidly. This convergence -- not estrogen loss -- partly explains the convergence of post-menopausal disease rates with men's rates. Monitor transferrin saturation; consider blood donation.

3. **Iron supplementation during pregnancy:** Peat warned that iron supplements during pregnancy are associated with increased jaundice in newborns and may contribute to childhood cancer. Women who didn't take iron supplements had healthy babies without jaundice.

4. **Post-menopause iron panel timing:** Draw iron panels at least 5 days after any bleeding. In post-menopausal women, the absence of the progesterone-hepcidin cycle means iron absorption is relatively constant, so timing within any month is less critical than in cycling women.

**Sources:** cbc-organ-enrichment E5.1-E5.2; nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.33-E6.37; iron-inflammation-enrichment (iron/estrogen sections)

### Pregnancy Iron Caution (New Section)

Peat's view on pregnancy iron supplementation was strongly cautionary:

- Iron supplements during pregnancy have been associated with increased jaundice in newborns
- Women who didn't take iron supplements during pregnancy had healthy babies without jaundice
- Iron supplementation may contribute to increased childhood cancer
- Given that pregnant women already absorb 9x more iron than men, supplementation may be unnecessary and harmful for most

> "It has been observed that women who didn't take iron supplements during pregnancy have healthy babies that don't develop jaundice." -- newsletter: iron-cumulative-danger.md

This contradicts the near-universal prenatal iron supplementation practice. The enrichment data supports interpreting "low" hemoglobin during pregnancy as a physiological adaptation (estrogen-driven blood dilution and marrow suppression), not necessarily a deficiency requiring iron loading.

### Osteoporosis and the Prolactin-Estrogen Trap (New Section)

Enrichment reveals a critical trap in osteoporosis treatment:

**Prolactin contributes to osteoporosis** by mobilizing calcium from bones. Estrogen increases prolactin. Therefore, using estrogen to "prevent" osteoporosis may actually worsen it through the prolactin pathway.

> "Since it is established that excess prolactin does contribute to osteoporosis in humans... the editor of the Yearbook of Endocrinology says 'Consider, if hyperprolactinemia leads to osteoporosis, and the administration of estrogens to postmenopausal women leads to hyperprolactinemia...'" -- newsletter: darkness-water-osteoporosis.md

**Peat's recommended osteoporosis protocol for women:**
- Thyroid optimization (primary)
- Progesterone (reduces PTH, opposes estrogen, blocks aldosterone)
- Vitamin A + bright light
- Pregnenolone or progesterone + DHEA
- Diet: protein + all salts (calcium, magnesium, sodium, potassium)
- A bias toward magnesium rather than calcium would protect against many conditions of aging
- Glass of milk at bedtime (suppresses nocturnal PTH surge that drives overnight bone loss)

**Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.19, E6.56-E6.57
