# Panel Recommendations -- What to Test and Why

This document maps Peat's explicit lab recommendations and the implicit recommendations that follow from his interpretive framework. The core panel comes from his 2019 One Radio Network appearance ("The Goods") where he listed his recommended tests. Symptom-specific additions are derived from his marker-by-marker claims across the full corpus.

---

## Peat's Core Panel (Everyone)

From One Radio Network: The Goods (2019-05-21), supplemented with claims from all extraction files.

| Test | Why | Peat's Target | Notes |
|------|-----|---------------|-------|
| **TSH** | Pituitary thyroid demand -- but interpret cautiously | As close to 0 as possible; >2.0 with symptoms = act | TSH itself causes inflammatory damage; do not treat by TSH alone |
| **Free T3** | Active thyroid hormone | Upper third of range | The hormone that actually works in cells |
| **Free T4** | Thyroid storage form | Mid-range | High T4 with low T3 = conversion problem |

| **Testosterone** | Protective but aromatase-vulnerable | Men: mid-upper range; Women: not routinely specified | In men, always consider alongside estrogen |
| **Prolactin** | Stress indicator | <10 ng/mL ideal; 18-20 = stressed | Mainstream range includes pathological levels; rises with TSH |
| **Free Fatty Acids** | Stress-driven lipolysis marker | As low as possible | Rarely done but "extremely important"; high = running on emergency fat burning |
| **Lactic Acid** | Metabolic failure indicator | As low as possible | Rises when cells shift from oxidative to glycolytic metabolism |

Collect these alongside any blood work:

| Indicator | Target | How to Measure |
|-----------|--------|----------------|
| **Waking temperature** | ~97.8F | Oral, immediately upon waking, before any activity |
| **Post-breakfast temperature** | ~98.6F | 30-60 min after carbohydrate-containing meal |

## Symptom-Specific Additions

### Fatigue / Low Energy

This is the most common complaint Peat addressed. His framework treats fatigue as primarily a thyroid/metabolic problem.

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Reverse T3** | Stress diverts T4 away from active T3 | High rT3 with normal/low T3 = stress-driven hypothyroidism |
| **Transferrin saturation** | Iron overload impairs mitochondria | >30% = possible iron-driven fatigue |
| **Calcium (serum)** | Low calcium raises PTH, which poisons mitochondria | Low-normal calcium with high PTH = energy drain |
| **Magnesium** | Required for ATP function; depleted by aldosterone | Low magnesium = impaired energy production at cellular level |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** Fatigue = cells cannot produce ATP efficiently. The causes, in order of likelihood: (1) hypothyroidism (impaired T4-to-T3 conversion), (2) PUFA tissue saturation blocking glucose oxidation, (3) iron overload damaging mitochondria, (4) elevated PTH/aldosterone/cortisol poisoning mitochondrial function, (5) inadequate carbohydrate intake forcing stress-hormone gluconeogenesis. Temperature and pulse are the first-line diagnostic tools.

**Sources:** thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.1-1.6; Reverse T3 section); lipid-metabolic-raw (Free Fatty Acids, CO2 sections); hormones-raw (PTH section, claims 8.1-8.2; Aldosterone section, claim 9.5); cbc-organ-raw (Magnesium section)

### Weight Gain / Can't Lose Weight

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Reverse T3** | Stress-driven T4 diversion | High rT3 = body conserving energy, promoting fat storage |
| **Estradiol** | Estrogen promotes fat storage | Elevated in both men and women = fat accumulation |
| **Insulin (fasting)** | Insulin resistance indicator | High insulin with normal glucose = cells resisting glucose uptake |
| **Cortisol** (with functional context) | Glucocorticoids promote abdominal fat | Central obesity pattern = cortisol excess |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** "The basic metabolic rate is the key. If you increase thyroid function, you burn more calories at rest." Weight gain is almost always a thyroid/metabolic problem, not a caloric one. Estrogen promotes fat storage and inhibits lipolysis of the right kind (safe fat burning) while promoting dangerous PUFA release. The Randle cycle -- where free fatty acids block glucose oxidation -- creates a vicious cycle: stress releases PUFA, PUFA blocks glucose use, cells demand more glucose, more stress hormones are released. DHEA "is an activator of fat oxidation in muscle. Everything that increases muscle testosterone and DHEA while lowering cortisol favors burning fat safely in muscles."

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claim 2.22; DHEA section, claim 5.5; Cortisol section, claims 1.6-1.9); lipid-metabolic-raw (Free Fatty Acids section; Insulin section); thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims on metabolic rate)

### Hair Loss

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Prolactin** | "Both hirsutism and hair loss are associated with high prolactin" | Steady high prolactin = hair loss; surging prolactin = hirsutism |
| **Ferritin + Transferrin sat** | Iron status affects hair | Both deficiency and excess can cause hair loss |
| **Estradiol** (men) | Aromatase converting testosterone to estrogen | High E2 in men = T being consumed |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** Hair loss in women is usually a thyroid/prolactin problem. "The thyroid supplement is a very reliable way to control prolactin in most women." Hair loss in men typically reflects rising estrogen (via aromatase) consuming testosterone. The pattern matters: steady high prolactin causes diffuse hair loss; surging prolactin causes hirsutism. For resistant cases, add Vitamin B6 and salt to lower prolactin.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Prolactin section, claims 7.6, 7.11); thyroid-raw (TSH section, general thyroid-hair connection); iron-inflammation-raw (Ferritin/iron sections)

### Cold Hands/Feet

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Reverse T3** | Stress-driven hypothyroidism causes peripheral vasoconstriction | High rT3 = adrenaline-driven circulation |
| **Sodium** | Low sodium raises aldosterone | Low sodium = aldosterone-driven vasoconstriction |
| **Calcium** | Low calcium = PTH rise = vasoconstriction | Low calcium with symptoms = PTH problem |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** "If your hands are cold while your heart rate increases, you're running on adrenaline, which is not good. Your hands should be warm." Cold extremities are the cardinal sign of adrenaline-driven metabolism compensating for inadequate thyroid. The distinction: warm hands + moderate pulse (80-85) = thyroid-driven; cold hands + fast pulse = adrenaline-driven. Treatment: thyroid optimization, adequate carbohydrate (prevent nocturnal adrenaline surges), adequate salt (suppress aldosterone).

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Adrenaline section, claim 10.5; Aldosterone section, claims 9.1-9.2); thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.12, 1.17); cbc-organ-raw (Sodium section)

### Insomnia / Sleep Issues

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Blood glucose** (consider CGM) | Nocturnal hypoglycemia triggers awakening | 2-4 AM waking = glucose crash |
| **Cortisol** (AM) | Elevated AM cortisol = nighttime stress response | High AM cortisol after poor sleep = cortisol-driven waking |
| **Sodium** | Low sodium = aldosterone rise at night | Aldosterone "rises during the night" and impairs mitochondria |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** "Insomnia is a daytime disorder presenting at night." The mechanism: inadequate daytime carbohydrate depletes liver glycogen by 2-4 AM, blood sugar drops, adrenaline and cortisol surge to mobilize glucose, waking occurs. Aldosterone and PTH also rise nocturnally, compounding the stress. The diagnostic test: if eating a small amount of sugar and salt at waking allows return to sleep within minutes, the cause is confirmed as nocturnal hypoglycemia. Treatment: more daytime carbohydrate (especially before bed -- orange juice, milk with honey), adequate salt, thyroid optimization.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Adrenaline section, claim 10.3; Aldosterone section, claims 9.1, 9.5; Cortisol section, claims 1.6-1.9); lipid-metabolic-raw (Glucose section)

### Anxiety / Depression

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Prolactin** | Stress indicator; elevated in anxiety states | >10 ng/mL = systemic stress |
| **Pregnenolone** | Brain's primary neurosteroid; deficiency = cognitive/mood issues | Low = impaired brain steroid synthesis |
| **Calcium** | Peat linked low calcium to seizures, spasms, anxiety | Low calcium = neuronal excitability |
| **Magnesium** | Deficiency causes anxiety, muscle tension | Low magnesium = heightened stress response |

**Peat's serotonin inversion:** Peat's most famous contrarian position: serotonin is not a "feel-good" chemical but a stress mediator. "In the gut, serotonin causes inflammation, intestinal spasm, and diarrhea. In the brain, it promotes learned helplessness. In blood vessels, it causes vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation." SSRIs work (when they do) not by "restoring serotonin balance" but by desensitizing serotonin receptors through flooding. Peat's approach to depression: optimize thyroid (primary), ensure adequate progesterone/pregnenolone (brain neurosteroids), reduce endotoxin exposure (gut health), and ensure adequate carbohydrate intake. "When adrenal and thyroid hormones fall, the brain shifts to emergency drivers like adrenaline and serotonin, which increase function temporarily but risk harming vital functions."

**Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Serotonin section, claims 42-52); hormones-raw (Pregnenolone section, claims 6.1-6.5; Prolactin section, claim 7.9); cbc-organ-raw (Calcium/PTH section; Magnesium section)

### Digestive Issues

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Albumin** | Low albumin = liver stress, possibly from endotoxin burden | <4.5 g/dL = chronic inflammatory load |
| **ALT / AST** | Liver enzymes; endotoxin damages liver | Elevated = liver under attack |
| **Bilirubin** | Liver function indicator; mildly elevated can be protective (antioxidant) | Very high = liver dysfunction; mildly elevated = may be protective |

**Peat's endotoxin connection:** "Endotoxin is a toxic component of gram-negative bacterial cell walls that enters the bloodstream when intestinal barrier function is compromised." Impaired digestion leads to endotoxin translocation, which triggers: serotonin release from gut cells, nitric oxide production, estrogen elevation (liver can't clear it), liver damage (reduced albumin synthesis), and systemic inflammation. "Every organ failure has endotoxin involvement." The fiber strategy -- raw carrot, cooked bamboo shoots, cooked mushrooms -- binds endotoxin and estrogen in the gut and prevents reabsorption. Coffee protects the liver directly.

**Sources:** iron-inflammation-raw (Endotoxin section, claims 30-41; Albumin section, claims 55-60; Serotonin section, claims 42-52); cbc-organ-raw (Liver section -- ALT/AST, Bilirubin, Albumin)

### Menstrual Irregularity

**Women-specific panel additions:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Estradiol** | Cycle day 3 and day 21 | Unopposed estrogen = irregular cycles |
| **Progesterone** | Day 21 | Low = inadequate corpus luteum function |
| **Prolactin** | Any day | Elevated prolactin suppresses ovulation |
| **FSH / LH** | Day 3 | Low LH under stress = suppressed ovulation; elevated FSH = ovarian reserve declining |
| **SHBG** | Any day | High = body defending against estrogen excess |
| **Reverse T3** | Any day | Stress-driven hypothyroidism impairs cycle regulation |

**Peat's framework:** Menstrual irregularity is almost always a progesterone deficiency problem, which is almost always a thyroid problem. "In the 1930s, one of the signs for diagnosing hypothyroidism was a progesterone deficiency." Estrogen interferes with thyroid conversion; low thyroid impairs progesterone synthesis; low progesterone allows more estrogen dominance -- a vicious cycle. Treatment: thyroid first, then cyclical progesterone (two weeks on, two weeks off), adequate carbohydrate, fiber for estrogen clearance.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Progesterone section, claims 3.5, 3.14; Estrogen section, claims 2.13-2.14, 2.32; Prolactin section, claims 7.1-7.2, 7.11; FSH/LH section, claims 13.1-13.2)

### Low Libido (Men)

**Male-specific panel additions:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Estradiol** | Aromatase converts T to E under stress | High E2 = T being consumed |
| **SHBG** | Sequesters both T and E | Very high SHBG = reduced free T effect |
| **Prolactin** | "Old men get low testosterone because stressed tissue converts it to estrogen. High estrogen brings up prolactin." | Elevated prolactin = estrogen-driven T suppression |
| **DHEA-S** | Protective androgen; declines with age | Low = systemic steroid cascade failure |

**Peat's framework:** "Testosterone decline in aging men reflects systemic metabolic deterioration (rising estrogen, falling thyroid, PUFA accumulation), not localized testicular failure." The sequence: stress/inflammation activates aromatase, converting testosterone to estrogen. Rising estrogen elevates prolactin and growth hormone. LH is suppressed by stress (uniquely among pituitary hormones), reducing testicular stimulation. Treatment: thyroid first, pregnenolone (100 mg) + DHEA (5-10 mg) before direct testosterone. If testosterone is used, 5-10 mg progesterone "can make the testosterone effect much more potent" by blocking aromatase. Avoid high-dose testosterone (50-100 mg) because "the liver may convert as much testosterone to estrogen as it preserves."

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Testosterone section, claims 4.1-4.10; Prolactin section, claims 7.4-7.5; DHEA section, claim 5.2; Pregnenolone section, claim 6.3; Progesterone section, claim 3.6)

### Infertility

**Both sexes -- additional markers:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Progesterone** (women, day 21) | "Fertility requires 50-100 times as much progesterone as estrogen" | Low P:E ratio = primary infertility cause |
| **Prolactin** (both) | Elevated prolactin suppresses LH in both sexes | >10 ng/mL = impaired gonadal function |
| **Reverse T3** (both) | Stress-driven hypothyroidism impairs fertility | High rT3 = body not supporting reproduction |
| **Vitamin A** (if available) | Required for pregnenolone synthesis | "Pregnenolone synthesis requires three inputs: cholesterol, thyroid hormone, and Vitamin A" |
| **Estradiol** (men) | Aromatase activity | High E2 = fertility-suppressing estrogen dominance |

**Peat's framework:** Infertility is primarily a metabolic/thyroid problem. The body suppresses reproduction when metabolic resources are insufficient -- this is adaptive, not random. In women: ensure adequate progesterone (P:E ratio of 50:1 to 100:1), optimize thyroid, suppress prolactin. In men: reduce aromatase activity (lower estrogen, reduce PUFA, reduce inflammation), support upstream precursors (pregnenolone, DHEA), optimize thyroid. "Stress induces all pituitary hormones except for luteinizing hormone, the one that produces testosterone and progesterone" -- stress directly suppresses the gonadotropin needed for fertility.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Estrogen section, claim 2.13; Progesterone section, claims 3.5, 3.14; Prolactin section, claim 7.5; FSH/LH section, claim 13.1; Pregnenolone section, claim 6.2; Testosterone section, claims 4.1-4.4)

## Tests Peat Says Are a Waste of Money

| Test | Peat's Reasoning | Source |
|------|------------------|--------|
| **Saliva cortisol panels** | "Saliva hormone tests are not reliable for absolute cortisol levels." Free cortisol in saliva varies independently of total production. | hormones-raw, claim 1.1 |
| **Saliva progesterone** | Progesterone is nearly water-insoluble; "what is it bound to?" in saliva is unknown. The measurement premise is flawed. | hormones-raw, claim 12.3 |
| **Isolated LDL** (without full lipid + thyroid context) | Drives statin prescriptions that lower cholesterol below the threshold needed for steroid synthesis. The test's clinical application is harmful. | lipid-metabolic-raw, LDL claims 1-5 |
| **TSH alone** (without T3, T4, rT3, temperature, pulse) | A "normal" TSH can mask severe hypothyroidism because stress hormones suppress TSH while simultaneously causing functional hypothyroidism. | thyroid-raw, claims 1.3-1.7 |
| **Ferritin alone** (without transferrin saturation and CRP) | Acute phase reactant; rises with any inflammation regardless of iron stores. Misleading as a standalone iron test. | iron-inflammation-raw, claims 1-10 |
| **Ceruloplasmin** (as a health target) | "Not my goal." It is an acute phase reactant. Targeting its elevation mistakes inflammation for health. | iron-inflammation-raw, claim 79 |
| **Melatonin levels** | Melatonin is not a "sleep hormone" but a serotonin disposal product. Its level tells you about serotonin burden, not sleep health. Variable results across studies (sometimes pro-inflammatory, sometimes anti-). | hormones-raw, Melatonin section, claims 11.1-11.5 |

## The Functional Data to Collect Alongside

Peat considered this data equal to or more important than blood work. Every blood panel interpretation should incorporate these measurements:

### Temperature Protocol
1. **Waking temperature:** Measure orally immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed, talking, or drinking water. Target: ~97.8F. Below 97.4F = strong hypothyroid signal.
2. **Post-breakfast temperature:** Eat a carbohydrate-containing meal. Measure 30-60 minutes after. Target: ~98.6F. Failure to rise = impaired T4-to-T3 conversion.
3. **Afternoon temperature:** Optional but informative. Should be at or near 98.6F by early afternoon in a healthy person.
4. **Track for at least 7 days** to establish pattern. Single readings are less meaningful than trends.

### Pulse Protocol
1. **Resting pulse:** Measure while seated, calm, at least 10 minutes after any activity. Target: ~80-85 bpm.
2. **Measure alongside hand temperature:** This is the critical diagnostic combination.
   - Warm hands + 80-85 bpm = thyroid-driven metabolism (good)
   - Cold hands + fast pulse (>90) = adrenaline-driven metabolism (stress)
   - Cold hands + slow pulse (<70) = hypothyroid without adrenal compensation
3. **Post-meal pulse:** Should rise slightly (5-10 bpm) after eating, indicating metabolic activation.

### Sleep Assessment
1. **Do you wake between 2-4 AM?** If yes: nocturnal hypoglycemia (liver glycogen depletion triggering adrenaline/cortisol).
2. **Can you return to sleep after eating sugar + salt?** If yes: confirms glycogen depletion as cause.
3. **Do you wake feeling rested?** If not despite adequate hours: nighttime stress hormones preventing restorative sleep.

### Digestive Assessment
1. **Bowel regularity:** Daily, well-formed. Constipation allows estrogen/endotoxin recirculation.
2. **Bloating, gas, or cramping:** Indicates bacterial overgrowth and possible endotoxin exposure.
3. **Response to raw carrot:** Peat's specific recommendation -- a raw carrot daily binds endotoxin and estrogen in the gut.

### Stress Response Assessment
1. **Blood sugar stability:** Can you go 4-5 hours between meals without shaking, irritability, or brain fog?
2. **Salt craving:** Craving salt may indicate low sodium / high aldosterone.
3. **Response to sugar:** If eating sugar (orange juice, honey in milk) produces immediate relief from anxiety or irritability, the person is in a cortisol/adrenaline-driven metabolic state.

**Sources:** hormones-raw (Recommended Panel section; Adrenaline section, claims 10.3, 10.5; Cortisol section, claims 1.6-1.9; Aldosterone section, claims 9.1-9.2); thyroid-raw (TSH section, claims 1.2, 1.12, 1.17); iron-inflammation-raw (Endotoxin section, claims 30-41); nutrients-diagnostics-raw (Diagnostic Philosophy section); lipid-metabolic-raw (Glucose section)

## Enrichment Additions (2026-03-17)

### Updated Core Panel Notes

**TSH target refined:** Peat's specific target was ~0.4 or below (not just "as close to 0 as possible"). People with TSH below 0.4 were the freest from thyroid cancer. Wait at least 2 weeks on a stable T4 dose before drawing blood for interpretation.

**Vitamin D target refined:** Explicit blood level goal was 50 ng/mL -- "the point at which it lowers parathyroid hormone." Supplementation strategy: 2,000 IU standard winter dose; some need 5,000 IU based on blood tests. 10,000 IU safe for a couple of months. Prefer increasing calcium and vitamin K while keeping vitamin D moderate unless blood levels are monitored. Peat personally applied ~20,000 IU topically rather than orally.

**PTH scope expanded:** PTH causes insomnia, seizures, dementia, psychosis, cancer, heart disease, respiratory distress, osteoporosis, sarcopenia, histamine release, inflammation, and soft tissue calcification. PTH ends the hair growth cycle (nocturnal PTH surges drive morning hair loss). If vitamin D has been very low for a long time, parathyroid glands may have enlarged and take extended time to normalize even with generous supplementation.

### New Symptom-Specific Additions

### Bone Loss / Osteoporosis

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **PTH** | Master driver of bone dissolution | Elevated = active bone catabolism |
| **Prolactin** | Mobilizes calcium from bones | Elevated = additional bone loss pathway |
| **Vitamin D (25-OH)** | Low D → high PTH → bone loss | Below 50 ng/mL = PTH not suppressed |
| **Aldosterone** | Nocturnal rise compounds bone loss | Check alongside sodium status |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** Bone loss occurs almost entirely during the night due to nocturnal rises in PTH, cortisol, prolactin, and growth hormone. The breakdown of protein at night is much faster than synthesis, and calcium is lost from bones during sleep. Rational therapy includes thyroid, vitamin A, bright light, pregnenolone or progesterone+DHEA, with a diet rich in protein and all salts (calcium, magnesium, sodium, potassium). A glass of milk at bedtime has the same protective effect on bone as daytime calcium. Estrogen-based osteoporosis treatment may worsen bone loss through the prolactin pathway.

**Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.56-E6.57; E6.19-E6.20

### High Blood Pressure

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **PTH** | Deficiency of calcium or vitamin D raises PTH → raises BP | Elevated PTH with low calcium/D |
| **TSH** | "High TSH is often the main factor in high blood pressure" | TSH > 2.0 with hypertension |
| **Aldosterone** | Salt restriction raises aldosterone → raises BP | Elevated aldosterone, low sodium |
| **Sodium (dietary assessment)** | Low sodium drives aldosterone | Salt craving as clinical indicator |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** Salt restriction is the cornerstone of conventional hypertension treatment, but Peat argued this raises aldosterone, which causes cardiac fibrosis, magnesium depletion, and the very hypertension salt restriction claims to prevent. Deficiency of calcium or vitamin D increases PTH, which raises serum calcium and blood pressure. High TSH is often the main factor. Treatment: thyroid optimization, adequate calcium/vitamin D (suppress PTH), adequate salt (suppress aldosterone), progesterone (receptor-level aldosterone blockade).

**Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.21; hormones-enrichment E4.10-E4.11; metabolic thread 15

### Brain Fog / Cognitive Issues

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **Pregnenolone** | Brain's primary neurosteroid; 10x blood levels in brain | Low = impaired brain steroid synthesis |
| **B1 (thiamine)** | Deficiency causes fog even with adequate thyroid | Trial of 10 mg B1 often diagnostic |
| **PTH** | PTH causes dementia, seizures, psychosis | Elevated = direct neurotoxicity |
| **Prolactin** | Chronic excess causes pituitary tumors → optic nerve pressure | >10 ng/mL = systemic stress |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** Brain fog is most commonly a thyroid problem (impaired glucose oxidation in neurons), but B1 deficiency can cause identical symptoms. "A supplement of 10 mg B1 is often enough to improve focus and prevent fatigue." PTH directly causes dementia and seizures at chronically elevated levels. Pregnenolone is the brain's most concentrated neurosteroid (10x blood levels) and its supplementation (100 mg) provides the brain's most-needed precursor.

**Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.55, E6.14; hormones-enrichment pregnenolone section

### Nocturnal Symptoms (2-4 AM Waking, Night Sweats, Morning Hair Loss)

**Additional markers to add:**
| Test | Why | Pattern to Look For |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| **PTH** | Rises nocturnally; drives bone loss, hair loss, calcification | Draw morning fasting for peak nocturnal effect |
| **Cortisol (AM)** | Nocturnal surge from darkness + low glucose | Elevated AM cortisol after poor sleep |
| **Sodium (dietary)** | Low sodium → aldosterone → nocturnal magnesium loss | Salt craving, muscle cramps at night |
| **Calcium (serum)** | Low calcium → PTH rise → nocturnal cascade | Low-normal with elevated PTH |

**Peat's interpretation framework:** The nocturnal catabolic cascade (Thread 19) explains why seemingly unrelated symptoms cluster together: 2-4 AM waking (blood sugar crash → cortisol), morning hair loss (nocturnal PTH surge), night sweats (adrenaline), and declining bone density (nocturnal PTH + cortisol). The intervention is metabolic protection at bedtime: T3 (5-10 mcg), calcium/milk, salt, and sugar/fat combination. This is NOT a sleep problem -- it is a metabolic protection problem.

**Sources:** nutrients-diagnostics-enrichment E6.20, E6.57-E6.59; hormones-enrichment E4.1-E4.2
